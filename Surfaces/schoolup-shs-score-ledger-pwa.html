Omnischools Senior · the ledger on the teacher's phone

The ledger, in your pocket.

Path C — direct digital entry — is the case the PWA is built for. A teacher marks an end-of-semester exam paper, picks up their phone, taps the score in. No paper book to keep, no individual-assignment workflow to track, just the five-category grid one student at a time. The PWA is the regular Omnischools web app surfaced as installable on the phone home screen — same data, same audit trail, same five categories — but laid out for a phone screen. Section 1 shows the online state. Section 2 shows the same surface with a connection drop, and how the sync strip honestly reports it. Section 3 names what ships in v1 and what is deliberately deferred, because over-promising "works offline" is the failure mode that creates angry users when extended offline doesn't behave the way the marketing implied.

Two views, one toggle, the class chevron always at hand

  • The PWA is the web app, surfaced on the home screen. Same authentication, same data, same audit trail as the desktop ledger view. A score entered on the phone appears immediately in the web app and vice versa — there is no separate "mobile app" with its own state to keep in sync with the server. This is the discipline a PWA gives that a native app does not, and it is the right architectural choice for a product where the desktop and mobile cases are equivalent.
  • Two view modes, one toggle pill, switchable in one tap. Card view shows one student at a time with five large input fields — the right shape for entering scores as you mark, where a teacher wants the visual confirmation that this row is for this student. Grid view shows the whole class in a compact table — the right shape for scanning, spotting outliers, or doing a final review before STPSHS export. Both views read and write the same underlying data; switching is non-destructive. The teacher's last choice persists across sessions per (subject × class × semester) — a teacher who prefers grid view on a class roster of 37 doesn't get pushed back to card view tomorrow.
  • The class chevron beside the class name is the switch between classes. Almost no teacher teaches one class. Mr. Owusu has two Form 2 Maths classes; an Integrated Science teacher might teach four; an English teacher across forms might teach six. The chevron and the "1 of 2" pill make plain that more classes are available — tap to open the bottom sheet showing the full list with each class's status. Section 2 covers the switcher in detail.
  • The green sync strip is the trust surface. Every PWA in v1 shows a clear sync indicator. Green means server-confirmed and last-synced timestamp; the teacher can see at a glance that the scores they just entered are safely on the server. The strip is intentionally always visible — it is not tucked into settings.
  • The Assessment Reference ID rides along, on every card and every row. The same REF-2024-XXXX shows up in card view, grid view, the desktop ledger, and the STPSHS export sheet. The phone is not a different system; it is the same record displayed differently.

Two classes, four, seven · same affordance, no new mental model

  • The chevron and the "1 of 2" pill beside the class name are the switch. Every PWA screen and the desktop ledger show the active class at the top. The chevron tells the teacher there are more classes; the pill says how many. Mr. Owusu, with two Form 2 Maths classes, sees "1 of 2." A typical English teacher running across forms would see "1 of 5." A teacher with one class sees no chevron — the affordance is suppressed when it isn't useful, so the UI doesn't tell first-year teachers their single class is one of one.
  • The bottom sheet lists every class the teacher teaches this semester, with progress at a glance. Each entry shows path used (A, B, or C), completion status across the five categories, student count, and the STPSHS-readiness pill if the window is approaching. Tapping an entry switches the active class; the bottom sheet dismisses; the ledger view (card or grid, whichever the teacher last used) reloads with the new class's roster. Two taps, end to end.
  • The current class is highlighted and is also the default route on open. When Mr. Owusu opens the PWA fresh, it lands on the class he last touched — not on the first class alphabetically, not on a "pick a class" interstitial. The state of the world the teacher last left is the one Omnischools restores. Switching is the deliberate action; staying put is the default. This matters more than it looks: a teacher who taps the home-screen icon between marking sessions wants to land back in the marking, not in a navigation step.
  • Switching is non-destructive. Tapping a different class does not lose anything — pending unsaved scores stay buffered in the local queue, sync resumes as normal, the new class's ledger loads on top. The audit trail records the switch (who looked at which class, when) the same way every other class-context action is logged. The teacher's working state in the prior class is preserved exactly; they can switch back and find their cursor where they left it.
  • The same affordance lives on the desktop ledger view. On the desktop (where the same teacher might be reconciling marks against their paper book on a wider screen), the chevron becomes a horizontal class-tab list above the grid — each tab shows the class name, student count, path used, and a small completion indicator. Tap to switch; the grid below reloads. The conceptual model is identical across form factors — one teacher, several classes, one switch.
  • The class context is also where the chevron tells the teacher when something is wrong. A class with an STPSHS deadline in less than 7 days shows the chevron in gold; a class with the teacher inactive for more than 14 days shows it with a small warn-dot. Without opening the bottom sheet, a teacher juggling several classes can see at a glance from any screen whether one of their other classes needs attention — and tap through directly. This is the same anomaly-rule infrastructure the Vice Headmaster's progress view uses, surfaced for the teacher's own benefit before it ever needs to surface for management.

Honest about what "handles bad connections" means

  • The strip turns gold and tells the teacher exactly what happened. "Connection lost · 3 scores held locally, will sync when reconnected." Not a vague "offline mode" icon, not a silent failure, not optimistic "everything is fine." A teacher entering scores in a staff room with patchy signal needs to know which scores are confirmed and which are pending — and the strip is the answer.
  • Pending inputs are tinted, not hidden. The gold-tinted input fields are scores the teacher entered while disconnected; the indicator badge at the bottom of the card confirms the count. The teacher can keep entering scores, see the totals computed locally, and trust that the buffer holds — but the visual cue means they will never mistake a pending score for a confirmed one.
  • The buffer is small and short-lived in v1. Phase 1 supports a connection drop of a few minutes, the realistic case of a teacher entering scores when signal fades briefly. It does not support a teacher disconnecting for an hour and coming back — that is Phase 2 territory. Marketing the v1 PWA as "works on your phone, handles bad connections" tells the truth about what it does. Marketing it as "works offline" implies extended offline sessions that v1 does not commit to, and that gap is where angry users come from.
  • Pre-cached student rosters mean the page loads without signal. The teacher can open the PWA, see the student cards, even start typing — all without a connection. The sync happens when connectivity returns; the entry is not blocked while it doesn't.

The marketing line is "works on your phone, handles bad connections." Not "works offline."

Phasing connectivity is the discipline that protects against angry users

  • Phase 1 ships first and delivers most of the practical value. The realistic case is a teacher entering scores at home, in a staff room, or at a desk — usually with at least intermittent connectivity. Bad-connection handling and a small buffer cover that case well. Building a full offline-first architecture in v1 would be months of engineering for a load case the product may not actually face.
  • Phase 2 is built when there is real evidence of need, not speculation. The trigger is teachers in low-connectivity areas asking for extended offline sessions. Local IndexedDB store, sync queue, replay-on-reconnect — solvable but substantial. The right time to do it is after the v1 PWA has been live for a semester and the requests have come in from real teachers, not when product strategy assumes they will.
  • Phase 3 is hypothetical capacity, not a commitment. Multi-device conflict resolution is the engineering case that creates the most bugs and the most subtle data-loss failure modes — a sync conflict that silently keeps the wrong version of a student's score is the trust-eroding bug. Build it only if multi-device is a real workflow teachers actually have, which is not the most likely actual case.
  • The marketing line follows the roadmap, not the other way around. "Works on your phone, handles bad connections" is the truth about Phase 1. "Works offline" would be the misleading line that promises Phase 2 capability the product does not have. The expectation gap between marketing and reality is where churn comes from, and Omnischools should not create it.
  • This is the discipline the rest of the product already shows. Oversight's coverage caveats, the STPSHS export's honest "no WAEC API yet," the ledger's "we save you typing, you confirm the read" — all of it is about not over-promising. The PWA roadmap is the same discipline applied to the connectivity story.
