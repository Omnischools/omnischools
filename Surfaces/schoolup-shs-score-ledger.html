Omnischools Senior · for an SHS subject teacher

The ledger, three ways.

SHS teachers in Ghana keep a score ledger — five categories per student per subject per semester: assignments, mid-semester exam, end-of-semester exam, project work, portfolio. Most teachers write it by hand in a paper book. Some compute it from a digital gradebook. A few will want to type it directly into a digital ledger view. Omnischools supports all three, on the same data model underneath — and the choice belongs to the teacher, not the product. The demo is Mr. Owusu, who teaches Mathematics to two Form 2 classes at Asankrangwa SHS — Form 2 Science and Form 2 General — at the end of Semester 2 of the 2025/26 academic year. The class-tab list above the grid switches between his two classes; the grid itself shows the active one. The surfaces that follow are the working ledger view with its class switcher (section 1), the upload-and-diff workflow when a teacher photographs their paper ledger (section 2), and the semester-end export path that feeds STPSHS (section 3).

One data model, the teacher's choice of medium

  • Five categories, NaCCA-standard. Assignments, mid-sem exam, end-of-sem exam, project work, portfolio — the structure that appears on every score ledger in every Ghanaian SHS, public or private, because every SHS teaches the NaCCA curriculum and submits to WAEC. Weights are configured per (subject × school) at enrolment; Asankrangwa SHS runs Mathematics at 15/15/40/15/15, with the end-of-semester exam carrying 40% — the dominant weight, reflecting how most schools treat the terminal assessment. Other subjects at the same school, and other schools entirely, may run different schedules; the surface always shows the configured weights so a parent or auditor can see how the total was computed.
  • The class-tab list above the grid is the switch between Mr. Owusu's classes. Almost no SHS teacher teaches one class. Mr. Owusu has two Form 2 Maths classes; an Integrated Science teacher might teach four; an English teacher across forms might teach six. Each tab shows the class name, student count, path used, completion summary, and a status pill — "current," "ready," or "behind." Tap a tab, the grid below reloads with that class's roster, scores, and progress. The same affordance is on the PWA as a chevron + bottom sheet; the conceptual model is identical across form factors. The active tab persists per teacher per semester — Mr. Owusu lands back on the class he last touched, not on whichever is alphabetically first.
  • Path A is active here, and the auto-compile pattern shows. Four categories already have computed scores from in-semester entries (the green-tint cells); portfolio is the one column waiting for the teacher's end-of-semester judgment. The weighted total is provisional until portfolio is in.
  • The three paths are equivalents, not a progression. The path chooser shows them as three peers, not "best / better / paper fallback." The teacher picks per (subject × class × semester) and can switch. Omnischools deliberately avoids implying that paper is inferior — the branded paper ledger book is a feature, not a transitional artifact.
  • Portfolio is structurally different from the other four. No in-semester events feed it — it is a one-shot end-of-semester discretionary mark for how well the student kept their own assessment papers organised. Omnischools cannot auto-compile it, and the UI says so plainly ("enter at semester end" instead of a progress count).
  • The STPSHS export sits visibly downstream. The right-hand card names the WAEC submission window date and the steps remaining. This is not a separate STPSHS surface; it is one button that becomes active when the ledger is complete. Section 3 below shows what generates.
  • Assessment Reference IDs are present on every row. The `REF-2024-XXXX` codes are the canonical student keys STPSHS assigns at Year 1 registration; the ledger shows them alongside the names so the eventual STPSHS export can be matched per-student. Even before the WAEC API conversation, this matters.

The diff is where Omnischools earns trust beyond digitisation

  • Side-by-side, photo and extraction. The teacher reviews the OCR's read of their handwritten ledger against the original photograph — cell by cell. Low-confidence cells are flagged (orange tint with a small "?" marker) so the teacher knows which ones to scrutinise. The product promise is "we save you typing, you confirm the read" — not "we read your ledger correctly," which would be over-promise.
  • Diff against the prior upload is the integrity layer. A mid-semester upload established a baseline; the end-of-semester upload supersedes it. Cells blank-to-filled are expected (new entries); cells filled-to-different need a teacher's reasoned decision. The four flags here show the four cases — low-confidence overlap, new entry (silent accept), score down (re-grade confirmation), and score gone missing (highest severity).
  • This is where deliberate alteration becomes visible. A teacher who quietly raises a student's mid-sem mark from 60 to 75 between uploads triggers a flag — the system records who reviewed it, when, with what reason. The paper ledger alone has none of this: a teacher can erase and rewrite a score with no audit trail. Omnischools doesn't prevent the change; it makes the change auditable.
  • The discipline is fair to honest teachers. A teacher who genuinely re-graded an assignment selects "re-graded" as the reason, the change is recorded, and the audit trail protects them if a parent or auditor later asks. The friction is small; the trust value is large.
  • Cross-path diffing applies the same logic. A Path A teacher who decides to photograph their paper ledger as a verification step gets the same comparison — Omnischools' auto-compiled scores versus what's actually written in the teacher's paper ledger. The two should match; when they don't, the teacher is told which.
  • The branded ledger book is what makes the OCR practical. The "PAGE 1/2 · F2 SCI · MATHS · S2" stamp in the corner of the scan mock is the QR code's metadata, telling Omnischools which class-subject-semester this page belongs to — and the printed gridlines on a known template are what let the OCR find the right cell. Teachers using their own ledger format still benefit, just with more verification needed.

The export ships independently of any WAEC API conversation

  • The sheet matches STPSHS's Capture Per Subject screen flow. Same column order, same Assessment Reference IDs in the same place, same five categories — so the teacher works top-to-bottom on the printed sheet and top-to-bottom on STPSHS's UI, ticking each row as entered. The 75 minutes of STPSHS entry that used to be 75 minutes of "compute then enter" becomes 30 minutes of "look-and-type."
  • No WAEC cooperation needed. This is a paper artifact Omnischools generates from data the school owns. It does not touch WAEC's infrastructure; it does not depend on an API; it ships now and works on the day Mr. Owusu's STPSHS window opens. If the WAEC API conversation later succeeds, this same data flows machine-to-machine and the printed sheet becomes optional. If it doesn't, the sheet remains the path.
  • The Assessment Reference IDs were quietly the whole story. The Year 1 student-registration bulk upload (the one STPSHS does support) returned REF-2024-XXXX codes that Omnischools stored against each student. Two years later, those codes appear on every export — which is what makes per-student matching reliable when the teacher enters scores manually.
  • The discipline is "STPSHS receives a copy of the ledger's totals," not "Omnischools talks to STPSHS." The school's source of truth is its ledger (whichever path produced it); STPSHS is one of the destinations. Parent reports and Oversight aggregation feed from the same ledger, in parallel. Decoupling the ledger from any one destination is what makes the architecture robust.
  • This is the surface a Headmaster will photograph and send. The sheet is print-ready, branded with the school's name and code, dated, and clearly labelled as the STPSHS-ready format. A school showing this to a GES inspector is showing the work between the ledger and the regulator — exactly the kind of artifact that builds trust beyond just the digital record.
