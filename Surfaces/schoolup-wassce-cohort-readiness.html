Reading the cohort at a glance

  • The histogram is the headmaster's chart, not the academic head's. Mrs Owusu-Ansah works with this view daily; she's the one filtering by programme, hovering over individual students, pulling the at-risk list. But when she meets the Headmaster every Monday morning, she shows this chart — the shape of the cohort. Dr Asare-Bediako reads it in 30 seconds.
  • The 20-bin histogram (one bar per aggregate point from 7 to 28+) shows the cohort's structural reality, not a smoothed line. The bumpiness reveals where Mock 2 marking landed — slightly heavy at agg. 15 and 19, slightly thin at 13 and 24. This is real noise from the marking process, not an artifact of the chart.
  • Programme comparison is the conversation starter for Heads of Department. Science HOD looks at this and sees his programme is doing well; Home Ec HOD looks at this and sees the conversation she needs to have with her teachers about pacing. Both legitimate readings; neither punitive.
  • The regional/national comparison line at the bottom of the histogram is the only external benchmark. Pulled from GES Western Region statistics (published in November) and WAEC national chief examiner reports (published in October). Out-of-date by 6–12 months, but the best external comparison available.

Why the heatmap matters

  • One row per subject, nine columns per grade. The colour gradient encodes pass-band intuition without needing a legend. Green cells on the left are good; red cells on the right are bad; gold in the middle is borderline credit.
  • Concern subjects are tagged in plain language. Elective Math and French are the two below-credit concentrations the academic head escalates to the Headmaster. The tag-with-rationale pattern means the staff brief writes itself: "Elective Math, 33% below credit, intervention plan needed by Jun 3 paper".
  • French is a structural staffing issue, not a teaching issue. Most rural Western Region SHSs cannot recruit a full-time L1 French teacher; the academic head's heatmap surfaces this every year, the HOD signs off, and the problem persists. This is the kind of friction the data wraps around — visible, repeated, not yet solved.
  • The watch tag is gentler than the concern tag. Math (Core) has 18% below credit, which is bad in absolute terms but on the lower end of national benchmarks. The cell-paint and the tag-language are calibrated for the right managerial response — escalate the concerns, watch the watches.

Why "resolved" doesn't mean "they passed"
Vocabulary discipline

Resolved means the school has done its part — the Dean or Form Master has had the conversation, the student understands their position, and a revised target set has been agreed. The student may still not get their first choice in August; the gap between their Mock 2 and their original target may persist.

The status the academic head tracks isn't "the gap closed" — that depends on what each student does in the next 8 weeks of WASSCE. The status is "the conversation happened".

"A school can't will a student into KNUST Medicine. We can be honest about what's likely, what's possible, and what other paths exist. The honesty is the deliverable; the result is theirs."

— Mrs Owusu-Ansah, faculty briefing, 28 Apr 2026

What the at-risk list does

  • Gap size is the primary sort. A student with a +6 gap (six aggregate points above their lowest target) is in deeper trouble than a student with a +2 tight gap. The Dean's worklist puts +6s at the top because those need the hardest conversations and the most time.
  • Y. Aidoo appears here despite being projected within target. She's on the at-risk list not for academics but for medical disruption — she's missed two live papers (Oral English yesterday, English Language 1+2 today). The system widens "at-risk" to include external risk factors, not just Mock 2 projection.
  • "Mother declined Dean call" is a real data point. Some parents don't want the conversation. The system logs the decline, the Form Master picks up the case, and the school stays inside its bounds — never coercive, always present. K. Antwi's mother declined; the Form Master is the tertiary contact.
  • "Resolved" is a documented decision, not a fixed outcome. The vocabulary discipline matters because it prevents the worklist becoming a faux-victory ledger. The Dean counts the conversations, not the eventual admission decisions.

The house cut, briefly

  • House aggregation is the only academic cut where the variation is mostly noise. The four houses run within 2 aggregate points of each other because boarding allocation is academic-blind — students are placed by gender + intake date, not by ability stream. We surface the comparison anyway because the HMs want it for their weekly meeting; we surface it last because it's the least diagnostic of the cuts.
  • The HM weekly digest is the one place the WASSCE module talks to the boarding module on auto-cadence. Friday afternoon, one page per HM. Tier counts. At-risk names. Last week's incidents that might affect academic outcomes. The HM doesn't need to log into the WASSCE module; the digest comes to her.
  • "Slessor 1 medical · Y. Aidoo" is the kind of detail that matters. Mr Boateng (Slessor HM) saw this at 11:15 today when the SC-12 filed; he visited the hospital at 14:00 during his break. The data flowed to him via the sickbay → boarding HM notification rule, the WASSCE module just reflects the state.
