Why this section comes first

  • WASSCE is high-stakes, irrevocable, and out-of-the-school's-hands. Once a student is registered with WAEC, the school cannot change their programme, swap electives, or add a subject. Setup is frozen for the cohort whose exams have started. This page is read-only for the F3 cohort; the "Edit" button activates for F2 (tracking toward 2027).
  • Four programmes, not five. Asankrangwa runs Science, Business, General Arts, and Home Economics. No Agriculture or Visual Arts track (the nearest school offering Agric is Tarkwa SHS, 38 km away). Schools that do offer Agric/Visual Arts get the same matrix layout — five or six cards, same shape.
  • Cores are universal, electives signal the programme. All 240 students sit the same four core papers — English Lang, Math (Core), Integrated Science, Social Studies. The electives diverge: Science gets Chem/Phys/Bio/Elec Math; Business gets Accounting/Costing/Bus Mgt/Econ; Arts and Home Ec branch wider.
  • The "or" pattern matters. Business students can take Elective Maths OR a fourth specialist business elective. Arts students choose 4 from a list of 7+. The schema captures both "required" and "optional" electives, and the registration step records which choice each student made.
  • The grading band visual is the most-referenced part of the page. Teachers in faculty meetings, parents in admissions discussions, students at orientation. Putting A1–F9 in their natural progression with the aggregate explanation underneath does the explaining once.

How mocks become readiness

  • Two mocks, not three. Mock 1 (Nov) is diagnostic — too early to be predictive but in time to remediate. Mock 2 (Mar) is the predictive one and the source for every readiness statement. Some schools run three; the marginal value of a third mock in a 60-day pre-WASSCE window is less than the marginal cost of teacher marking time. We stop at two.
  • Mock 2 must be marked by the same teachers who'll see the readiness reports. Subject teachers mark their own students' papers — not for grade inflation, but because the act of marking is the teacher reading every script and seeing exactly where the cohort is weak. That diagnostic is more valuable than the grade.
  • 80% predictive accuracy is the honest number. Omnischools publishes it on the readiness statement so no parent is misled into thinking the Mock 2 grade is the WASSCE grade. The 20% who drift get a written caveat: "Mock 2 is a prediction, not a promise; the WASSCE you sit will determine your tertiary path".
  • The distribution histogram is the headmaster's cohort summary in one chart. When the headmaster meets the PTA at the end of Semester 2, this chart is what he shows. 16% in top tier, 54% in middle, 13% in weak tier, 1.7% with no path. He doesn't need to know more than that to plan resource allocation across the next 8 weeks.

Every F3 student tags up to three target programmes after Mock 1 (revised after Mock 2). How the match works
Per student × per programme
1.
Programme cut-off published by university. KNUST Biochemistry = 11. Legon Medicine = 6. UCC Nursing = 12. The cut-off table is updated annually from each university's admissions page.
2.
Subject prerequisites checked. KNUST Biochem requires credits in English, Math, Int Sci, Biology, Chemistry + Physics or Elec Math. The system checks whether the student is sitting all required subjects (set at registration).
3.
Mock 2 aggregate compared to cut-off. Student's projected aggregate vs programme cut-off. Margin reported in points. Less than 2 points = "tight"; 2–5 points = "comfortable"; more than 5 = "very comfortable".
4.
Three target programmes per student. The "stretch" (top choice), the "match" (most likely), and the "safety" (back-up). Three is the maximum; some students tag fewer.
5.
Dean reviews mismatches. Any student whose top 2 targets exceed their Mock 2 aggregate gets a guidance interview. The Dean has reviewed 87 such cases across the 2024/25 academic year.

The target system in practice

  • Stretch · match · safety is borrowed from US college-admissions terminology. Imperfect fit (Ghana's process is single-cycle and university-mediated, not high-school-mediated) but the three-tier framing helps students understand they shouldn't tag all three at the same difficulty level.
  • Cut-offs are indicative, not guarantees. The same KNUST Biochemistry programme that took aggregate 11 in 2024 might raise to 10 in 2026 if the applicant pool is stronger. The system stores last-known cut-offs from official university admissions portals and updates annually.
  • 87 Dean guidance interviews per year is significant labour. One Dean of Students cannot run 240 conversations; she runs about 90 (the at-risk subset) and the Form Masters run the rest. The number falls naturally from the 13-24 middle band that the Mock 2 distribution shows.
  • Untagged students are a flag, not a fault. Some F3 students legitimately don't know what they want to do — and the Dean's interview surfaces this earlier than a parent SMS would. The "no first-choice tagged" row at the bottom of the universities table is a worklist, not a shame.

What the registration roster is for

  • One row per candidate, the data WAEC needs to see. Index number, programme, papers sitting, accommodations. The school owns the source-of-truth; WAEC gets the authoritative export. The export ran 14 Feb 2026 for this cohort.
  • Three flags today — one consequential, two administrative. Y. Aidoo's medical exemption is consequential: she's missing live papers. The NHIS-expired and the Free SHS reconciliation flags are bursar-side, not WAEC-side; they don't stop her sitting.
  • Accommodations sit in the same row as the candidate. Extra time for chronic conditions (E. Mensah's sickle cell), 1.5× time for visual impairment (K. Mensa), pastoral monitoring (A. Quartey). Different needs, same column — the matron, the Dean, and the WAEC liaison see them together.
  • WAEC SC-form filing is a real workflow. SC-3 (sight/hearing/physical), SC-7 (chronic medical condition known in advance), SC-12 (exam-day medical emergency). Each has a different timeline; the SC-12 for Y. Aidoo had to be filed before she missed a paper. Today's filing was at 11:00, 30 minutes before the 09:30 English Lang 2 (Essay) paper had been scheduled to end.
  • "No candidate denied WASSCE for fee reasons" is the Free SHS rule. When the district doesn't reconcile in time (P. Donkor's case), the school covers the gap and seeks reimbursement after. Omnischools's billing module carries the receivable; the WASSCE module carries the seat.

WASSCE · Setup · Policy anchors

Policy anchors.

What the WASSCE module is built on. Three regulators (WAEC, GES, MoE), one cohort, one set of rules. The anchors below are referenced in every readiness statement.
WAEC policy anchors
West African Examinations Council · 1952
Centre code
SU-0184 · Asankrangwa Senior High School · Western Region · Wassa Amenfi West District. Reviewed annually before registration window opens (Jan).
2026 calendar return
First International WASSCE since 2019. Ghana on Ghana-only calendar 2020–2025 due to COVID disruption. 21 Apr → 19 Jun 2026.
Special consideration forms
SC-3 (sensory/physical), SC-7 (known chronic condition), SC-12 (exam-day medical). Filed via WAEC online portal; medical cert from registered facility required.
Grading + aggregate
A1=1, B2=2, B3=3, C4=4, C5=5, C6=6, D7=7, E8=8, F9=9. Aggregate = best 3 cores + best 3 electives. Lower = better.
GES operational anchors
Ghana Education Service · school authority
Free SHS coverage
All WASSCE fees for school candidates covered by GES (2017 Free SHS policy). GHS 1,400 per candidate for core registration. Specialist electives may incur add-ons; school reconciles with GES district office.
"No candidate denied"
No student can be denied WASSCE registration for fee reasons. If district reconciliation lags, school carries the receivable. Omnischools billing module flags this; WASSCE module ignores it.
Calendar alignment
Per the GES SHS 2025/26 academic calendar, F3 Semester 2 runs 3 May → 21 Jun 2026 for single-track schools (a shortened Semester 2 since F3 ends earlier than F1–F2 to make way for WASSCE). F3 students complete WASSCE during Semester 2; F1–F2 students continue Semester 2 through 21 Aug 2026 per the same calendar. School-leaver ceremony scheduled for Fri 26 Jun (week after WASSCE ends).
Discipline pause
F3 disciplinary actions paused during WASSCE writing period unless safety-related. Boarding discipline ladder pauses for F3 from Apr 21 — penalty fees still accrue, but suspension and deboardinization on hold. Resumes Jun 22.

What the anchors do

  • Policy anchors prevent re-litigation. When a parent asks "why is the aggregate calculated this way" or "why didn't you wait for fees", the readiness statement points to the WAEC rule or the GES policy. The school doesn't invent the rules; it implements them.
  • Centre code is the school's identity at WAEC. SU-0184 is the immutable identifier across all WAEC interactions — registration, results, certificates, anomaly reports. Every student's index number is anchored on the centre code.
  • The Free SHS rule is binding. P. Donkor's GHS 240 reconciliation gap doesn't stop her sitting; it accrues as a receivable that the school chases the GES district to clear. This rule has been in place since 2017 and is the foundation of cohort completeness — 240 out of 240 students sit WASSCE regardless of fee status.
  • The discipline pause is a calm, written rule. No Headmaster wants a Form 3 student deboardinized mid-WASSCE; the policy makes the pause structural rather than judgment-call. Penalty fees still accrue (the financial trigger from boarding discipline remains operational), but the disciplinary track itself rests. Resumes after the final paper.
