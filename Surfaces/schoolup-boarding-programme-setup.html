The boarding programme · structure, rhythm, ladder

What the Senior Housemaster and Admin configure once at programme setup, and revisit once a year at start-of-session. Six Houses, each with a colour, a capacity, and a Housemaster who lives in the bungalow attached to the dormitory block. The daily rhythm from rising at 4:30 AM to lights out at 9:30 PM, every weekday for thirty weeks. Three policy doctrines — exeats, visiting, and deboardinization — that govern how a student crosses the school gate during term. None of this is incidental. It is the architecture of what boarding actually is.

What lives on this surface

  • Schema-shaping decisions only. Houses, capacities, schedules, policies. Changes here propagate to every other boarding surface — bunk allocation, daily life, exeats, visiting day, discipline.
  • Edits are versioned, not destructive. Changing a House's capacity from 120 to 132 creates a new version; the prior version stays in audit. Mid-year capacity changes are rare but happen.
  • The daily schedule is a template. Individual days can override (e.g. Speech & Prize Giving Day, NSMQ travel day) — but the template is what every Monday-to-Friday inherits.
  • The five-rung ladder is the operating contract between Housemaster and parent. Parents see steps 1—5 in their child's record when escalation begins. Transparency is part of the design.

What does NOT live here

  • Per-student bed assignment — that is surface 02 (House roster & allocation)
  • Tonight's prep attendance — that is surface 04 (Daily life)
  • An individual exeat in flight — that is surface 05 (Exeat management)
  • This Sunday's visitors — that is surface 06 (Visiting day operations)
  • The bond J. Adjei signed last Tuesday — that is surface 07 (Discipline)

Editable but rarely edited

  • Once a year at start-of-session. Headmaster + Senior HM sit together for an afternoon. Confirm House list, confirm HM assignments, confirm calendar, confirm policy tweaks. Lock for the year.
  • Audit catches everything. An edit on 23 March logs which staff member, from which IP, with what reason. No silent changes.
02
Why boarding is its own operating system
Editorial · structural adjacency, not feature
The architectural choice this surface is making

Boarding is not a feature of the school module. It is structurally adjacent to it.

Eighteen sessions into the design of Omnischools, every surface we have built so far reads from one canonical thing: the student. The student belongs to a Class, has a Form Master, has subjects, has a transcript, has parents, has an attendance record. The whole architecture is a constellation around the student. A school management system is, fundamentally, a student management system with administrative scaffolding around it.

Boarding breaks that. A boarder is still a student — they still attend Form 2 GA A, they still write Maths quizzes, they still need a school-leaver report. But during the eighteen hours a day they are not in a classroom, they are running a parallel operating system. They have a House (not a Class). They have a Housemaster (not a Form Master — though sometimes the same person). They have a bunk (not a desk). They have inspection in the morning and prep in the evening. They get three exeats a term and one visiting Sunday a month. They can be deboardinized — a thing no day student can have happen to them.

If we modelled boarding as a feature of the school module, we would be folding fifteen state transitions per student per day into the same table that tracks attendance in Maths class. That table would become unmanageable, and worse, it would force boarding-specific logic to leak into surfaces that day-student schools should never have to see. The cleaner architecture treats boarding as a distinct module that the school module is aware of — students have a `residency_type` (BOARDER / DAY / DEBOARDINIZED), and the boarding module owns everything that residency_type implies.

This separation is what makes the rest of the system work for Ghanaian SHSs of every shape. A pure-day school flips the boarding module off entirely and never sees this surface. A pure-boarding school flips it on and uses every part. A mixed school — the most common case in the Free-SHS era — operates both modules side by side, with the residency_type field on the student record being the join. The Headmistress's day-student dashboard does not show inspections; the Housemistress's boarding dashboard does not show non-boarders. Each role sees what its role is responsible for.

The same separation maps cleanly onto the data model. Boarding's tables — houses, dormitories, bunks, daily_schedules, exeats, visiting_log, inspections, bond_artefacts, deboardinization_records — live in their own namespace. They reference the student by ID. They never write back to the student record except through one column: residency_type, which the deboardinization workflow flips. Everything else is owned by the boarding module. Clean borders make clean code, and clean code makes a school that doesn't have to teach its software what kind of school it is.

Counter-argument we considered

  • "Just put a 'boarder' flag on the student record and add boarding fields inline." Simpler at first. Fails at month two. Inspection records, exeat counts, bond artefacts — none of them belong on the student record. The student record bloats. Day-student schools end up with hundreds of NULL columns.
  • "Make boarding a sub-module of the residential/wellbeing module." Closer, but conflates pastoral care with residential operations. Pastoral care (VLC, FM notes, the character paragraph) is about who the student is becoming. Boarding is about where they sleep and who watches them sleep there. Different work, different staff, different surfaces.

Read this surface as

  • The contract between school and the boarding module. Six Houses with these names. This rhythm. These policies. This ladder. Everything downstream — surfaces 02 through 07 — is the implementation of what is declared here.
