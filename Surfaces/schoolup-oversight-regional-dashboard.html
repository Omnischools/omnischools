Omnischools Oversight · the regional tier

One tier up · the region.

The regional dashboard is the district dashboard's pattern lifted one level: a regional director sees their districts the way a district director sees schools. Same five themes, same anomaly logic, same drill-down — but the unit is the district, and the regional director can open any district's dashboard and follow it down to a single school. The demo is the Western Region, fourteen districts, with Wassa Amenfi West appearing as one row that drills into every surface already built. The regional view also carries a question the district view cannot: how the districts compare to one another, and which are pulling the region.

The district dashboard, lifted one tier

  • The pattern is deliberately identical. Same KPI strip, same period banner, same provenance row as the district dashboard — a regional director who has seen a district dashboard reads this with no relearning. Only the unit changes: pupils become 198,400 not 14,820, schools become 508 not 40, and the rollup is of 14 districts.
  • The scope-chip names the regional jurisdiction. "Western Region · 14 districts" where the district view said "Wassa Amenfi West." The regional director A. Owusu sits above the district director D. Mensah-Bonsu in the same provisioning hierarchy — Decision 5's nesting made concrete.
  • Coverage is a headline KPI at this tier. 431 of 508 schools — and the card shows the year's progress (41 onboarded). At regional scale the coverage gap is large enough to be a managed programme, so it earns a KPI rather than a footnote, and the figure is honest about what the region cannot yet see.
  • Every regional figure is explicitly a rollup. The period banner states it: each number is the sum or mean of 14 districts. A regional director must know their 66% WASSCE figure is a district-weighted mean, not a separate measurement — the surface says so rather than implying a regional-level source.
  • The ceiling holds upward too. "Sibling regions not visible here" — a regional director sees their region and down, never sideways into another region. The same jurisdiction discipline as the district tier, one level up.

The districts table is the regional junction box

  • Every row drills into a full district dashboard. A regional director tapping Wassa Amenfi West lands on exactly the surface its own director D. Mensah-Bonsu uses — and from there into the school list, a school detail view, the enrolment analysis. The whole district product is reachable from one regional row. The hierarchy is navigable top to bottom.
  • The home-district highlight orients the director. Even a regional director thinks in terms of "my patch" — Wassa Amenfi West is gold-tinted as the reference row. It is also a deliberate demo thread: the district every other Oversight surface has used, now seen as one line in its region.
  • Coverage sits beside every rate, and the colour rule is stated. Prestea-Huni Valley and Wassa Amenfi Central both show 59% WASSCE — but Central is at 61% coverage, so its figure is the least settled in the region. The table refuses to let a regional director rank Central last on a number that thin coverage makes ambiguous; the provenance row spells out the rule.
  • The rank cards name the ends honestly. Strongest district carries both its rate and its coverage; the "needs attention" card does too. A regional director gets the headline — Sekondi-Takoradi leading, Central trailing — without losing the caveat that makes the trailing case provisional.
  • Compare is the workspace entry, one tier up. Select districts, hit Compare, land in the comparison workspace at its district-vs-district tier — the same surface section 3 of the workspace demonstrated. The regional director's natural path is dashboard → spot an outlier → compare or drill.

The regional tier earns its own signals

  • The regional dashboard is not just a bigger district dashboard — it sees patterns the district cannot. "Core Maths is the weakest core in 11 of 14 districts" is invisible from inside any one district; it only appears when 14 districts are read together. That cross-district pattern is the regional tier's distinct value, and it argues for a regional programme over eleven separate district responses.
  • Coverage drives the top regional priority. The first flag is not a performance problem — it is Wassa Amenfi Central's 61% coverage. The regional dashboard inherits the enrolment surface's discipline: a district's weak figures are not actionable until its coverage is closed, so the regional director's clearest task is an onboarding push, explicitly framed as "not an intervention."
  • Anomalies are framed at the right altitude. Prestea-Huni Valley's district-wide 27.3:1 PTR is a "regional staffing reallocation question" — the regional director can move teachers between districts in a way a district director cannot. Each flag is written to the action its tier can actually take.
  • The onboarding gap is localised. 77 missing schools, concentrated in 3 named districts — the surface turns a regional statistic into a targeted programme. Same logic as the district dashboard's coverage flag, scaled up.
  • Comparison and anomaly reuse the established engines. Same rule-based anomaly logic as the district and operational tiers; same comparison workspace, entered at the district-vs-district tier. The regional dashboard is new surface area but no new mechanisms — consistency is the point.
  • This completes the regional dashboard. The region at a glance (section 1), the 14 districts with drill-down (section 2), and the regional-level signals (section 3) — the district pattern lifted one tier, with the cross-district view that only this tier provides. The fourth Batch 2 surface; the national dashboard is the last.
