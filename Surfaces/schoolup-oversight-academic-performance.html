Omnischools Oversight · examination outcomes

How the district performs.

Performance is the metric GES leans on most heavily for school evaluation and intervention targeting, so it sits beside enrolment as a primary Oversight theme. This surface covers two kinds of performance data, kept deliberately distinct. Sections 1–3 handle external examination outcomes — the terminal, WAEC-graded BECE and WASSCE results — by school, Form, subject, sex, and trend. Section 4 handles internal and continuous performance — the schools' own termly and annual assessment results across every class, and mock examination results for candidate cohorts. The two are not merged: external exams are authoritative and arrive once a year; internal data is live but carries coverage and moderation caveats. The headline metric for external exams is the qualification rate — the share of candidates graded credit or above (BECE grades 1–6, WASSCE A1–C6) — never a raw pass rate. A two-level period selector at the top of each section switches exam type and period. The demo jurisdiction is Wassa Amenfi West.

The headline, and what sits under it

  • The qualification rate leads — explicitly defined. 63% is the share of WASSCE candidates graded credit or above (A1–C6), the tertiary-entry threshold. The hero card states the definition in words and shows the count behind it (1,228 of 1,949), so the figure can never be mistaken for a raw pass rate.
  • The subject grid explains the headline. A single 63% tells a director nothing actionable; the four-subject breakdown does. Core Mathematics at 54% — weakest and the only core falling — is named as the largest drag and the obvious intervention target. Performance analysis is only useful when it points somewhere.
  • A two-level period selector frames the whole surface. The top selector has two rows: type — WASSCE, BECE, Mock, Internal termly, Internal annual — and period, the year (and term, for internal data). Plus an All/Boys/Girls candidate cut. Sections 1–3 respond to the external exam types; the Mock and Internal options open section 4. From the first screen a director sees that performance here is more than the terminal exams.
  • Regional context is given without leaving the surface. "Below the Western Region average of 66%" — a director sees their district against the tier above immediately. The full peer comparison is the comparison workspace; the headline still carries one anchoring number.
  • WAEC sourcing is stated. Per Decision 7, examination results are a loaded reference extract — there is no public school-level WAEC dataset. The surface draws on the official WAEC extract and the exam-year toggle makes the cohort explicit.

Two cuts a director cannot do without

  • The sex cut turns "performance" into an equity question. A 63% headline hides that girls lead overall but trail by 6 points in Core Maths. GES equity work needs that specificity — a Mathematics intervention here should be sex-aware, because the gap is in one subject, not across the board.
  • The trend is the cut that judges strategy. Any single year is noise; four cohorts climbing 54→63 is signal. The chart shows a district genuinely improving — and still 3 points short of its region. A director reads the trend to know whether what they are already doing is working.
  • Trend stays within one examination. WASSCE cohorts are compared only to WASSCE — never averaged with BECE, which tests different pupils at a different stage. The provenance row states this; mixing them would produce a meaningless line.
  • The 2026 cohort is previewed. Form 3 enrolment (1,284) is next year's WASSCE candidate count — the trend chart implicitly extends one year forward, so a director sees the cohort whose result they can still influence.
  • Both panels carry the regional anchor. "3 points behind the region" appears in the sex panel's framing and the trend's stat list. The district is never shown in isolation — improvement is real, but measured against the tier above.

The average has authors

  • The district figure decomposes to schools. 63% district-wide is the weighted result of three very different schools — Asankrangwa at 71%, Amenfiman at 58%. A director acts on schools, not on an average; the table names which school to visit.
  • The table identifies the largest drag, not just the lowest score. Amenfiman SHS is lowest at 60% — and it is also the biggest cohort, so it pulls the district average down most. Lowest-rate and largest-drag are different questions; the surface answers the one that targets intervention.
  • A local benchmark is shown alongside the problem. Asankrangwa SHS at 71% and rising sits in the same table as Amenfiman — "what is achievable here" is visible next to "where the gap is." That makes the case for intervention concrete rather than abstract.
  • Core Maths is carried as its own column. Section 1 named it the district's weak core; the school table keeps a Core Maths column so a director sees which schools drive that weakness — Amenfiman at 49% is the worst, consistent with its overall position.
  • Every row drills into a school performance detail. The table is the junction into the per-school view — the same drill-down pattern as the dashboard's school list. And it stays aggregate: no named candidate records here, consistent with the access boundary.
  • Sections 1–3 cover the external examinations in full. District headline and subjects, the sex and trend cuts, school-by-school with comparison — the WAEC-graded BECE and WASSCE picture. Section 4 turns to the internal and continuous performance data that GES also monitors between those terminal exams.

Internal performance — the data between the exams

  • This section answers GES's other performance question. BECE and WASSCE are terminal and annual; they say nothing about a Primary 4 class, or about a candidate cohort halfway through its final year. Termly and annual internal results cover every class, every term — the continuous picture a director needs between the headline exams.
  • The source caveat is the first thing in the section, not a footnote. Internal data is the schools' own gradebook marking. Two honest limits stated up front: coverage (28 of 34 schools — not every reporting school uses the gradebook) and moderation (unmoderated, so reliable for a school against itself but only loosely comparable between schools). This mirrors the enrolment surface's discipline — the surface states where its data is partial.
  • The two-level selector is where your dropdown suggestion lands. Exam type — WASSCE, BECE, Mock, Internal termly, Internal annual — crossed with period, plus a class/subject/gender cut. The same selector pattern runs across all four sections; internal and mock simply take their place in it as first-class options.
  • Termly results are cut by class/Form, subject, or gender. A district director asks "which year-groups are slipping," a curriculum officer asks "which subjects," an equity review asks "which gender" — one toggle, three questions. The class/Form cut shown surfaces the SHS Form 3 slip, the cohort that matters most.
  • Mock exams are treated as a predictor, not just another score. Shown beside the same cohort's prior actual WASSCE, the mock becomes a leading indicator: Amenfiman's cohort tracking 11 points low, with a term and a half left to intervene. The surface also names the reverse signal — a mock far above prior actual suggests lenient marking, not a triumph.
  • Internal and external stay distinct but cross-corroborate. The Form 3 termly slip and the Amenfiman mock shortfall point at the same cohorts the external sections flagged. Kept in separate sections with separate caveats, but a director reading both sees one consistent story — which is what makes the early warning credible.
