An extra step — once a day

Two-factor authentication is required for admins (Florence and Joyce touch financial records and student data) but not surfaced for parents and students (the user base is mobile-first and many have basic phones — forcing 2FA would lock people out). The interesting design moments are the plain-English walkthrough for admins who haven't set this up before, the recovery flow when a phone is lost or stolen, and the grace period that prevents day-of lockouts. Editorial commitment: respect the user, frame as protection, never as alarm.

What is two-factor authentication?

When you sign in, instead of just typing your password, you also enter a 6-digit number that changes every 30 seconds.

That number comes from an app on your phone — Google Authenticator or Authy — which generates the codes locally · no internet needed · works even if your data bundle is finished.

Even if someone learns your password, they can't sign in without your phone in their hand. Your password becomes useless on its own; the phone is the second key.

Why we ask: admins record payments, void invoices, edit student records · those actions need an extra layer of protection that parents and teachers don't need.

Why this layout

  • H1 framing intentionally light: "An extra step — once a day." Editorial commitment to non-alarm · the typical SaaS framing ("YOUR ACCOUNT IS AT RISK!") is rejected here · 2FA is presented as protection, not as response to threat. Page lede continues this: "An extra step at sign-in protects the school's records." Calm, factual, dignified.
  • Sidebar lists "Security & 2FA" as the final Settings sub-item · sister to Audit log, Data export, Retention policy. Settings sub-nav is now full: 10 items reflecting the operational depth of the platform · admin learns the mental model once and finds related controls.
  • Status overview card mirrors the audit-log hero — same navy-with-radial-gold treatment, same pillar-plus-3-stats layout. Visual consistency across Settings sub-pages; admin recognises "this is a status overview" pattern from the moment they land.
  • Status framing leads with the not-yet-complete state: "2 of 3 admins enrolled — Joyce pending." Not "67% complete!" — that's a SaaS dashboard tell · instead names Joyce specifically, says she has 9 days remaining, lists the consequence ("school is not yet fully protected"). The action is concrete; admin knows what to do.
  • Three stats: required progress / teacher opt-in / grace remaining. Grace remaining is the most operationally useful; Florence sees "9d" and knows when Joyce will be auto-enforced if she hasn't enrolled.
  • Walkthrough section is the heart of the file · 4-step plain-English flow demonstrating step 2 (QR code scan) actively. Editorial commitment: every step is explained for an admin who has never set up 2FA before · this is most Ghanaian basic school admins.
  • Walkthrough head copy: "Once set up, you'll do this once a day when you first sign in from a device · trusted devices remember you for 30 days." Friction expectations stated upfront; admin doesn't fear that 2FA will block them every minute.
  • Left explainer panel: "What is two-factor authentication?" with 3 paragraphs of clear English. The "becomes useless on its own" framing is critical: explains the security property in user terms · "Even if someone learns your password, they can't sign in without your phone in their hand."
  • "Why we ask" takeaway box: "admins record payments, void invoices, edit student records · those actions need an extra layer of protection that parents and teachers don't need." Justifies the asymmetry · Joyce learns why she has 2FA but the parents don't · the answer is risk-proportional, not arbitrary.
  • QR panel with both QR and manual key: "Or type this key manually if your phone's camera isn't working." Important in environments with older phones; some Android cameras struggle with QR · the manual fallback is structurally equivalent and doesn't feel like a degraded path.
  • Account label shown next to manual key: "Omnischools · joyce@christtheking.edu.gh." Joyce sees what the authenticator app will display when she opens it later · so she can find the right entry among other 2FA codes.
  • OTP input mock with 6 separate digit boxes · matches the OTP-on-login pattern from the auth file · same 6-box visual everywhere OTP is entered · admin and parent OTP feel like the same primitive.
  • Recovery codes panel in terra-tinted card with strong visual emphasis · "SHOWN ONCE · NEVER AGAIN" badge · 10 codes in 2-column grid with mono font · numbered 01—10 for tracking which have been used. Three save actions: PDF download, Print now, Copy all · admin chooses what fits their preservation habit.
  • "Print this list and keep it with the school's important documents — not in your email or phone" · operationally specific advice; tells admin where to NOT keep the codes · prevents the most common mistake of "I have them in WhatsApp."
  • "Each code works once" stated explicitly · the surface specifies behaviour rather than letting admin discover it under pressure.
  • Three role cards with colour-coded borders: Admins (terra REQUIRED) / Teachers (gold ENCOURAGED) / Parents & students (neutral NOT SURFACED). Tier of friction matches tier of risk; this is the policy stated visually.
  • Each role card has 3 reasons · risk profile, surfacing approach, self-disable rules. The asymmetry between roles is justified each time; admin reading this card understands why this isn't capricious gating.
  • "OTP-on-every-login already provides 2FA-like protection" for parents · the parents already get an OTP every time they log in (their phone is the auth factor) · 2FA on top would be redundant friction. Editorial framing acknowledges that parents are already 2FA'd by the design of the parent portal · the asymmetry is principled, not lazy.
  • "Available in advanced settings for power users · not promoted" for parents · opt-in path exists for the rare technically-savvy parent · we don't gate, we just don't push.
  • Three recovery scenarios as cards: A (lost phone, codes available) / B (lost phone, no codes) / C (solo admin locked out). Each scenario has 4 numbered mini-steps · admin understands the path before they're under pressure.
  • Scenario B uses dual-control via second admin · cross-references the segregation-of-duties pattern from the activation file · same dual-control philosophy applied to recovery; the second admin is the school's escape hatch.
  • Scenario C (solo admin) requires support intervention with school registration document re-verification · 2-3 business days · longest recovery path. The cost of being a single-admin school is paid here · this is a real consequence that should encourage second-admin enrolment over time.
  • "SMS verification to verified phone · same number on file" in scenario C · defends against SIM swap attacks; an attacker who SIM-swaps Esther's number can't trigger reset because the support flow also requires the school registration document.
  • Audit log mention in every scenario: "audit log records the recovery event" / "audit log records both Florence's approval and the reset" / "audit log records support intervention." 2FA flows are part of the audit narrative; recovery events are the highest-stakes auth events and deserve full audit visibility.
  • Save bar at the bottom of section 01: "Continue to step 3 · enter the code?" with meta line about trusted-device enrolment ("up to 3 trusted devices"). Standard Omnischools save bar pattern; the user is mid-flow and the save bar shows what comes next.
  • Trusted device count limited to 3 · admin's phone, tablet, school computer · not unlimited; if a 4th device tries to enrol, oldest gets bumped · prevents creep where every machine the admin has ever used is trusted.
  • "Trusted devices remember you for 30 days" · the friction window · admin signs in once a month per device · doesn't OTP on every action. Calibrated against operational reality; school admins use the same laptop/phone every day · forcing daily OTP would be friction without security gain.
