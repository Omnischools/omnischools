Why this layout

  • The parent surface is fundamentally different in voice. No flag severity tags, no class rankings, no pattern detection labels, no audit trails. The same data the admin dashboard surfaces for diagnosis is here surfaced for daily life — calmer, warmer, written in the parent's own way of talking about their child.
  • "Omnischools · Christ the King JHS" app tag at the top reminds the parent which school's app this is. Some parents have children at multiple schools and use multiple Omnischools apps; the tag helps disambiguate. The school's identity is more prominent than Omnischools's — it's the school's relationship with the parent, with Omnischools underneath as plumbing.
  • Greeting addresses the parent by name in big italic Fraunces: "Good evening, Patricia" / "Good evening, Mary." Same warm voice as the morning digest sent to admin. Personal, not corporate.
  • Date sub-line gives operational context: "Friday 2 May · school is now closed for the weekend." The parent who opens the app at 7 PM doesn't have to wonder if there are pending registers or live updates — the school day is over, this is the after-hours view.
  • Child switcher pills below the greeting. Patricia has two kids (Akosua + Kofi); both are visible, Akosua active. Mary has only James, so only one pill shows. Multi-child families are first-class, not an afterthought; the switcher is always present.
  • Five tabs: Home / Attendance / Grades / Fees / Inbox. Attendance is its own tab, not buried under "Academic" or "Records." Same first-class status as Fees because both are daily concerns.
  • State A — Today hero in green-tinted card. "Akosua was at school all day" with the headline in italic Fraunces. The green wash is reassurance; the parent reading "she was at school" + "she arrived on time" + status confirmation can close the app and feel informed in 5 seconds.
  • "Marked present at 8:05 AM by Mr. Mensah" in the status line. The teacher's name humanises the system; "marked present by the system" would feel cold. The parent feels Akosua was seen by a real person, not just logged.
  • This-week row uses 5 day tiles (Mon-Fri only). Weekends are not shown — the parent doesn't need them on a weekday/school view. Saves space; matches the parent's mental model of "the school week."
  • Day tile for today has gold ring outline + "Today" label. Same convention as the desktop teacher view. Visual continuity across surfaces; same Friday tile means the same thing whether you're a teacher or a parent.
  • Term summary shows percentage + days fraction + breakdown bar + last term comparison. Three pieces of context that answer different questions: "How is she doing?" (96%) · "Out of how many?" (45 of 47) · "Better or worse than before?" (last term was 94%). Honest comparison, no commentary.
  • "Last term: 94% · 56 of 60 days" grounded in mono. Not "improving" or "trending up" — just the number. Parent draws their own conclusion. The system doesn't editorialise.
  • Planned-absence CTA is a card, not a button. Action card with gold "+" icon, Fraunces title, supporting copy explaining when to use it. The card is part of the page's information architecture, not a floating action that interrupts.
  • State B's hero is terra-tinted with pointed language. "James was not at school today." Not euphemistic ("had a school day off") and not punitive ("absent without permission") — direct factual. The italic gold "not at school" softens the edge slightly with brand voice.
  • Detail line on State B: "He has been absent for 5 school days in a row, since Tuesday last week." Specific, memory-helping. Mary may not have realised it had been five days — the system tells her honestly. "The school hasn't received a reason for these absences yet" sets up the school-contact card below.
  • School-contact card is the most important addition in State B. Terra border, "!" icon, named attribution: "Florence Addo (Headmistress) sent a WhatsApp message yesterday afternoon and tried to call this morning." Tells Mary what the school has done so she's not surprised when she sees Florence's WhatsApp; reassures her that the school is being thoughtful, not just sending automated SMS.
  • "Please get in touch when you can — it doesn't have to be formal, even a brief message helps." The voice the school would use to a friend. Lowers the threshold for Mary to reach out. Many parents avoid contacting the school because they think it has to be a formal explanation; this language says "we just want to hear from you."
  • Two contact buttons: Call school (navy) + WhatsApp (WA-green). No SMS option in this state because Mary is the one who needs to initiate; SMS is broadcast-shaped, not conversation-shaped. Phone or WhatsApp are the two real bidirectional channels.
  • "What the school has noted" section in State B shows recent absences with reasons. "Reason: Not yet provided · marked as no contact from home" — neutral phrasing that names the school's data state without blame. "Tell the school what happened →" link gives Mary a path to retroactively explain past absences.
  • The corrective link uses second-person verb. "Tell the school what happened" — Mary is the agent, the school is the recipient. Not "Provide a reason" (passive) or "Update record" (system-speak).
  • Term summary in terra-tinted variant for State B. The 61% is rendered in terra Fraunces — same colour vocabulary as everywhere else, but no "Below threshold" label. The colour conveys the state; the parent doesn't need a verbal verdict.
  • State C — Planned absence form is three sections: Which child / When / Reason. Numbered gold circles match the system's existing form pattern (Record Payment, Send Reminders). Familiar architecture; nothing new to learn.
  • Child selector as tile picker rather than dropdown. Mary sees James's tile selected; "+ Add another" tile suggests she could include Kofi if both kids are off (e.g., they're both attending the same family event).
  • "How long?" toggle: One day vs Several days. Most planned absences are single-day (doctor visits, small family events). Multi-day support exists but is the secondary path. Optimising for the common case without hiding the alternative.
  • Date input rendered in Fraunces serif: "Tuesday · 6 May 2026" — clear and human. Not "06/05/2026" or "2026-05-06." The day of the week matters; parents think in days, not date strings.
  • Field help line: "School day · Term 1 will continue normally." Confirms that Tuesday is in fact a school day (not a public holiday or term break) so Mary knows the absence will be recorded. If she'd accidentally picked a weekend or a holiday, the help would say so.
  • Six reason tiles in a 2-column grid: Doctor, Sick, Family event, Travel, Religious, Other. Same options as the teacher's exception drawer, in parent voice. "Doctor / hospital · medical visit" and "Sick at home · unwell, resting" are distinguished because they're operationally different (doctor visit = excused with reason; home illness = excused with note).
  • Optional note field: "Anything you want the school to know?" Pre-filled with example: "Booked appointment for routine check-up at Korle Bu. James will be back at school Wednesday morning." The example shows the right level of detail — practical, conversational, not over-explained.
  • Form summary card at the bottom in gold-tinted card. "You're letting the school know that James will be absent Tuesday 6 May for a doctor's appointment. The school will mark the day as excused and won't send the usual absence SMS that afternoon." Plain English summary of what submitting will do — including the operational consequence (no auto-SMS). Parent submits with informed confidence.
  • "Send to school" button instead of "Submit." The verb matches the mental model — Mary is sending information to the school, not submitting a form to a system. Same data, gentler framing.
  • What's not here: No flag explanations, no pattern descriptions, no auto-prompts to "improve attendance," no predictive language ("if this continues..."), no comparisons to other students. The parent surface is information, not coaching. Coaching, if needed, comes from a human at the school.
  • Last term comparison and historical absences are deliberately summarised, not detailed. The full term register exists in the admin/teacher views; the parent view abstracts. Mary can see "47 of 60 days last term" but doesn't get a 60-day grid she didn't ask for. The right level of detail for the right reader.
