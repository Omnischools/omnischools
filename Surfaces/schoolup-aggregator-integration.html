Hubtel, plumbed in

The MVP1 manual flow doesn't go away — Joyce can still record cash payments and direct MoMo-to-MoMo transfers exactly as before. But for the 80%+ of payments that come in via Hubtel's checkout, the system handles the entire round trip: link to parent, parent pays, webhook fires, invoice is auto-matched, receipt is auto-sent. Joyce moves from data entry to oversight. Three states: how the parent gets the link, what Joyce sees when payments arrive, where the school connects their merchant account.

Why this layout

  • Three views in one row tell the story end-to-end. Parent receives SMS (left), school sees what they sent (middle), parent taps the link and lands on Hubtel's checkout (right). The middle panel is the only screen the school operator builds; the two phone views are the parent experience that the system enables.
  • SMS on the parent's phone is short and plain. "James Mensah · JHS 2A · Term 1 fees · Balance due GHS 340.00 · Pay securely: pay.htl.gh/ck-q9x4m" — under 160 characters with the link as the call to action. "Reply HELP if you have questions" gives parents an out if something looks wrong; preserves the trust signal that the school is real.
  • The link uses a short Hubtel domain (`pay.htl.gh/ck-q9x4m`) not the school's domain. Parents recognise Hubtel; "pay.christtheking.edu.gh" might look like phishing. Borrowing Hubtel's brand authority is a feature, not a compromise.
  • SMS sender ID "CHRIST KING" uses the truncated 11-character pattern from the SMS sign-off rules already locked in. Parents recognise the school name; Hubtel's brand sits inside the message as the payment provider.
  • Middle panel: school view of payment request creation. Eyebrow "School view · billing", headline "Send a payment link", sub-line explains what Hubtel will handle. The invoice mini-card at the top recaps what's being paid for so the operator can verify before sending.
  • "Pays through Hubtel: GHS 340.00" in the invoice card matches the amount due. Fees absorbed means the parent sees and pays GHS 340 — the GHS 6.63 Hubtel fee is invisible to them and netted off the school's deposit. Honest with the operator about this exact amount the parent will see.
  • Generated payment link card in gold-tinted bg with QR code on the left, URL + copy button on the right. QR code matters because some parents prefer to scan from a printed receipt rather than tap a link in SMS. Same payload, two delivery formats.
  • The QR is rendered as a real-looking QR pattern with three corner markers and dot pattern. Not a placeholder square; design matters because the operator will see the actual QR and learn the visual pattern.
  • Channel options as checkbox cards: SMS / WhatsApp / Email. SMS and WhatsApp pre-checked because both are available; Email greyed out because there's no address on file. "add an email to enable" tells operator how to unlock the option without forcing it.
  • Channel options show context per recipient: "+233 24 555 0421 · GHS 0.05" for SMS (cost transparency), "opted in · last seen 2h ago" for WhatsApp (deliverability signal), "no address on file" for Email (operational gap). Each channel surfaces what matters for that channel.
  • Hubtel checkout uses Hubtel's actual brand colours and patterns. Hubtel-blue header band with white "Hubtel" logo + "🔒 SECURE" badge. The page looks like Hubtel because it IS Hubtel — the school's brand identity steps back when the parent enters the payment surface. Parents trust Hubtel as a known payment processor; pretending it's still Omnischools would be confusing.
  • Merchant card at the top of checkout shows Christ the King's identity in the school's brand styling (navy "CK" tile in gold) inside the Hubtel frame. "verified merchant · Accra" tells the parent they're paying the right entity. Visual marker that this is a legitimate transaction, not a scam.
  • Amount display dominates the checkout — GHS 340.00 in big Fraunces with the term + student name underneath. The parent's eye lands on what they're paying for before the payment method choices. "Term 1 fees · James Mensah · JHS 2A" is the human-readable purpose.
  • Four payment methods: MTN, Telecel, AirtelTigo, Card. MTN selected by default because it's the dominant Ghanaian network. Each method shown with its actual brand colours — yellow MTN, purple Telecel, terra AirtelTigo, navy card. Familiar visual language; parents pick what they have.
  • Phone input pre-filled with parent's MoMo number from the school's records. Reduces typos on the most error-prone field. Parent can edit if they want to use a different number.
  • "Pay GHS 340.00" with lock icon on the call-to-action button. Amount in the button reinforces what's about to happen; lock icon + "PCI compliant" footer build trust at the commit moment.
  • Footer: "Powered by Hubtel · PCI compliant". PCI compliance is meaningful even if most parents don't know what it means — banks and card issuers use the term, builds reflexive trust. The school doesn't need PCI compliance because the school never touches card data; Hubtel does.
  • What's not shown: the USSD prompt that follows. After the parent taps Pay, MTN's USSD layer takes over (their phone gets a *170# style prompt asking for PIN). That's outside Omnischools's surface — the parent sees the same MoMo flow they always do. The system trusts the network here.

Why this layout

  • The reconciliation feed is Joyce's new home base. In MVP1 she was navigating to the Record Payment drawer multiple times a day; in MVP2 she opens this page in the morning and watches it through the day. The visual rhythm is "auto-handled stuff scrolls past" + "exceptions sit waiting in the right pane." Joyce's role shifts from data entry to oversight.
  • 5-tile stat strip leads with the hero "Today's collected" in navy-gold treatment matching the dashboard pattern. Subline "net deposit GHS 11,610.96 · GHS 231.04 in fees" shows both gross and net at the headline level — Joyce sees both numbers without drilling. The fees figure makes the cost of automation visible.
  • "96% match rate today" sub-line on the auto-matched tile tells Joyce the system is working as expected. Match rate is a leading indicator of reconciliation health; if it dropped to 70% one day, something would be off (parents using new phone numbers, an aggregator config issue, etc.).
  • Two-pane layout: live feed on the left (1.7fr), exceptions on the right (1fr). The proportions reflect the work — most payments auto-match and just need to scroll past for awareness; exceptions need clicking. Wider feed for scanning, narrower exceptions for action.
  • "Live" tag on the feed card with pulsing green dot. Visual signal that this view auto-updates as webhooks arrive. Joyce doesn't need to refresh; payments appear at the top as they happen.
  • Just-arrived row gets brief flash + NEW indicator. The Akosua Boateng row at the top of the feed has a green "NEW" tag and a 4-second background flash from green-bg fading to surface. Movement tells Joyce something happened without being noisy — animation fades, the row settles into the steady-state feed.
  • Each feed row: status icon, student card, transaction details, amount block. Status icons are saturated colour circles with letter — green ✓ for matched, gold ⏳ for in-progress, terra ! for exceptions. Same icon vocabulary as the attendance flag system; Joyce already knows what each colour means.
  • Detail line tells the human story: "Paid full term fees · receipt sent to Patricia Boateng via SMS + WhatsApp" / "2nd installment paid · balance now GHS 0.00 · receipt sent" / "Awaiting USSD confirmation · Mary Mensah's phone · 4 min elapsed". Each row is a sentence Joyce could read aloud to explain what happened.
  • Meta line in mono with technical detail: "REF KMS-9TXP4 · INV-2025-0098 · matched on amount + phone". For audit and debugging — when Joyce or the auditor needs to trace a payment, the Hubtel reference + invoice ID + match strategy is right there.
  • "matched on amount + phone" / "matched on student ID" attribution explains how the system matched. Joyce learns the matching logic by watching it work; she can develop intuition for which payments will auto-match vs which won't (e.g., guardian-with-multiple-children using same phone for both = phone-only match is ambiguous, will need manual handling).
  • Amount block shows gross + net + method. "GHS 340.00 [MTN] · net GHS 333.37". Both gross and net visible — gross is what the parent sees and what counts toward the school's collection; net is what hits the bank. Joyce can reconcile against the bank statement using net.
  • Method tags use brand colours: yellow MTN, purple TC (Telecel), terra AT (AirtelTigo), navy CARD. Same vocabulary as the Hubtel checkout in State A. Visual continuity across the system; method colours mean the same thing wherever they appear.
  • In-progress row (James Mensah's pending payment) in gold ⏳. "Awaiting USSD confirmation · Mary Mensah's phone · 4 min elapsed" tells Joyce a payment is mid-flight but not yet confirmed. 4 min is normal for USSD flows (parents finding their phone, entering PIN, confirming); 15+ minutes would suggest abandonment.
  • The pending row uses session ID (SESS-Q9X4M) instead of payment ref (REF-XXX). Different identifier because the payment hasn't completed yet — there's no Hubtel reference to log. When (if) it completes, the row updates to the matched style with a real REF.
  • Exceptions queue on the right is the action pane. Three exception types covered: No match (phone number not in guardian list), Amount mismatch (overpayment or underpayment), Multiple matches (parent has two children, ambiguous which invoice to apply). Each is a real edge case in Ghanaian school finance.
  • Each exception row has 2 action buttons inline. "Match to student / Refund" for unknown sender; "Apply as credit / Refund GHS 10" for overpayment; "Choose student / Split payment" for multiple matches. The two actions are always: do something with it, or send it back. Joyce doesn't have to navigate elsewhere for routine exceptions.
  • Exception type pills are colour-coded: "No match" terra (genuinely problematic), "Multiple matches" terra (needs decision), "Amount mismatch" warn (less urgent — money is in, just need to allocate). Severity gradient inside exceptions helps Joyce prioritise the queue.
  • Footer note in the exceptions card: "Match rate 96% today · Exceptions are typically resolved within 24 hours". Sets expectations — Joyce knows that 3 exceptions today is normal; if she had 30, something would be wrong with the system.
  • "Record cash payment →" CTA in the page header preserves the manual flow from MVP1. Cash payments don't go through Hubtel (parent walks in with bills, pays at the office); Joyce records those manually as before. The aggregator integration adds capability, doesn't remove the existing flow.
  • What's not here: No payment reversal or chargeback handling on this surface — those are rare and go through a separate flow with admin approval. No bank reconciliation against Hubtel's statement (that's a month-end task with its own report). No fee analytics ("you've spent X on Hubtel fees this term") — that belongs in a finance report.

Why this layout

  • Connection card at the top dominates the page. Hubtel's actual brand colour (their distinctive blue) for the logo tile + green "Live" pill + merchant ID + last-verified timestamp. The connection is the foundational fact — if Hubtel isn't live, nothing else on this page matters.
  • Reconnect / Disconnect actions on the right. Reconnect is the "I changed my Hubtel password and need to re-auth" path; Disconnect is in terra to signal it's significant — turning off Hubtel means going back to manual reconciliation. Asymmetric visual weight reflects asymmetric consequence.
  • Region 1 — Fee transparency leads. Two side-by-side cards: "Fees absorbed by school" (the actual configuration with the fee math worked out) and "What this buys you" (the value proposition). Cost transparency before benefit transparency — the school has already paid for this; showing them what they get reinforces the decision.
  • Fee block shows the actual GHS-to-GHS math: "Parent pays 340.00 → Hubtel fee −6.63 → School receives 333.37". The arithmetic is undeniable; admin sees exactly where each cedi goes. The total row uses Fraunces + green to highlight the school's net.
  • "GHS 2,068 per term" rough total in the fee-meta line. Per-payment percentages obscure scale; the term-level number is honest. Some schools will see 2,068 and decide it's worth it; others will reconsider. The settings page enables the decision rather than burying the cost.
  • "Cash payments and direct MoMo transfers are unaffected" reassurance in the fee-meta. The school can still accept cash without fees; Hubtel only kicks in for online payments. Honest scoping of where the cost applies.
  • "What this buys you" card uses 4 green-checkmark bullets. Auto-reconciliation / All four methods at one rate / PCI-compliant card processing / Automatic receipts. Concrete value delivered, not marketing language. Each bullet is a real operational benefit Joyce experiences daily.
  • Region 2 — Methods listed with brand colours and market share. "MTN ~70% / Telecel ~18% / AirtelTigo ~10% / Cards ~2%" gives admin context for which methods matter most. All toggled on by default, with a "leave all on" recommendation — disabling a method just creates friction for parents.
  • Single fee pill (1.95%) on every method — Hubtel's pricing is uniform across methods. This is a feature: schools don't have to think "is MTN cheaper than Telecel?" Pricing simplicity.
  • Region 3 — Payout schedule with three pills. Daily / Weekly (selected) / Monthly. Most schools pick weekly — daily is over-frequent for school finance flows; monthly delays cash too much. The selected schedule fills with gold to match the rest of the system's "this is the lever" convention.
  • Payout summary in plain language: "Funds collected through the week settle on Monday morning. Next payout: Mon 27 Oct · estimated GHS 18,400." The Fraunces italic gold "Next payout" phrase sets expectations — admin knows when to expect the deposit and roughly how much.
  • Bank destination card shows the actual bank, masked account number, and "Verified" green pill. The card looks like a payment card — bank logo tile in navy/gold, account number in JetBrains Mono with masked digits, account name. Familiar visual idiom.
  • "For security, this can't be edited from inside Omnischools" warning under the bank card. Bank-account changes are a common attack vector — if a bad actor compromises a Omnischools admin account, redirecting payouts is the most damaging thing they could do. Putting that change behind Hubtel's separate login is a real security boundary, not theatre.
  • Region 4 — Connection health is the technical-but-readable section. Two cards: webhook delivery + reconciliation safety net. Both with mono-formatted stats that look like a developer tool but read in plain language for a non-developer admin.
  • "99.94% success rate · 7d" with 2 failed (auto-retried, succeeded)" sub-line. The system is honest that webhooks occasionally fail; equally honest that the retry mechanism caught both failures. Trust through visible imperfection — claiming 100% would be suspicious; showing 99.94% with the recovery story is credible.
  • Reconciliation safety net card explains the polling backup. "Even if a webhook is missed, Omnischools polls Hubtel's payment list every 15 minutes." This is real engineering reassurance — webhooks are unreliable in practice; defensive polling catches what the push misses. Admin doesn't need to understand the technical detail, but the card establishes that the system is robust.
  • "Recovered via polling · 7d: 2 payments" stat proves the safety net works. Same 2 number as the webhook failures — the polling caught exactly the ones that the webhook missed. Symmetric numbers tell a coherent story.
  • What's not here: No fee comparison vs other aggregators ("Paystack would charge X" — invites second-guessing), no historical trend on match rate (one term of data is too little to chart), no test-payment console (defer to Hubtel's own test mode), no API key management (Hubtel handles that on their side, OAuth-style).
  • What's also not here: No multi-tenant aggregator support. A school is connected to one Hubtel merchant account; switching providers (e.g., to Paystack) would mean disconnecting Hubtel and reconnecting to Paystack. Not common in practice; not worth the complexity.
  • "View Hubtel docs ↗" CTA in the page header opens Hubtel's documentation in a new tab. Omnischools doesn't try to embed Hubtel's docs; if admin needs deeper detail (API specifics, edge cases), the right place is Hubtel's own docs maintained by Hubtel.
