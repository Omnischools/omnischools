Omnischools Oversight · comparison as a first-class mode

Set them side by side.

Every dashboard and analysis surface in Oversight has an "Open comparison" affordance — and this is where they all lead. The comparison workspace puts selected schools, districts, or regions next to each other on any theme: enrolment, performance, attendance, staffing, fees, or the enrolment-vs-population rate. It is what turns Oversight from a set of single-jurisdiction readouts into an analytical tool — a number is only meaningful against a peer. Comparison is scoped to the user's jurisdiction ceiling: a district director compares schools and sibling districts but never regions they cannot see; a regional director compares districts and sibling regions; the national tier compares anything. The demo is a district director comparing the three senior high schools of Wassa Amenfi West.

The picker is the access model, made concrete

  • Comparison is the mode that makes Oversight analytical. A single dashboard answers "how is my district." Comparison answers "compared to what" — and that is the question that turns a number into a judgement. Decision 2 made comparison first-class; this surface is where the affordance scattered across every dashboard finally resolves.
  • The tier toggle enforces the jurisdiction ceiling visibly. Schools and Districts are selectable; Regions is shown but disabled, because a district director has no region-level visibility. The limit is not hidden — the director sees the option exists and sees that it is not theirs. A regional director would find Regions enabled.
  • The "within" scope keeps the picker honest. A district director picks schools within Wassa Amenfi West and sibling districts within the Western Region — nothing further. The picker offers only what the jurisdiction permits, so an out-of-scope comparison is impossible to even assemble, not just refused at the end.
  • The benchmark column is a deliberate, pinned construct. "District average" is added as a styled-apart benchmark, not just another school. Every comparison wants a reference line — the mean — and pinning it means each entity is always read against it rather than only against each other.
  • Eight entities is the stated ceiling. Comparison degrades into noise past a point; the surface caps it and says so. A workspace that let a director stack twenty schools would not be a comparison, it would be the school list again.

One table, read two ways

  • Read across a row to compare; read down a column to profile. The same table answers both — "who has the best WASSCE rate" is a row, "what is Amenfiman's whole picture" is a column. The metric labels are pinned left and the benchmark pinned right, so neither reading loses its anchor as a director scrolls.
  • Best and worst are marked, not left to the eye. Every row tags its leading and trailing entity. A director scanning eight metrics across four columns should not have to compute which number is highest — the surface does it, and the colour follows the same green/red the rest of Oversight uses.
  • The benchmark column turns comparison into evaluation. Three schools against each other tells a director the local order; against the district average it tells them who is genuinely under-performing. Amenfiman trails the mean on performance, PTR, and vacancies together — the column makes that pattern impossible to miss.
  • All five themes sit in one table. Enrolment, performance, attendance, staffing, fees — comparison is not theme-locked. A director comparing schools usually wants the whole picture, because a school's weak WASSCE rate and its 27.8:1 PTR are likely the same story; seeing them stacked makes the link visible.
  • Like-for-like is enforced. The provenance row notes it — only schools of the same level compare directly, because a JHS qualification rate and a WASSCE qualification rate are different exams. The picker in section 1 keeps a comparison within one level for exactly this reason.
  • Comparison stays aggregate. No named records here — the workspace compares institutions, never pupils, consistent with the access boundary the school detail view sets out.

The same workspace, one tier up

  • District-vs-district uses the identical layout. A director who learned the school comparison reads the district comparison with no relearning — same columns, same benchmark, same best/worst marking. Only the entities change. One workspace serves every tier.
  • The jurisdiction ceiling holds here too. A district director can step up one tier to compare districts within their own region — but Regions stays disabled. They see their district among its siblings; they never see another region's internals. The ceiling is consistent with section 1's picker.
  • Coverage travels into the comparison as a first-class row. This is the lesson from the enrolment surface, carried through: a district's recorded enrolment rate cannot be read without its school coverage. Wassa Amenfi Central at 22-of-36 is flagged so its low rates are not mistaken for confirmed under-performance — the comparison refuses to rank on ambiguous data.
  • The two insight cards separate finding from caveat. "What the comparison shows" and "what it cannot yet show" sit side by side. A director gets the read — West leads Primary, trails on WASSCE — and the honest limit in the same glance. Oversight states both rather than presenting a clean ranking that the data does not support.
  • This completes the comparison workspace. Build the set within the jurisdiction ceiling (section 1), compare on every theme with a pinned benchmark (section 2), step up a tier with coverage kept honest (section 3) — the surface every "Open comparison" button across Oversight now resolves to.
