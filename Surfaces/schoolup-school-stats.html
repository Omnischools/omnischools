Why this layout

  • "School at a glance" not "Statistics". Same data, friendlier framing. The headmistress doesn't think of her school as statistics; she thinks of it as her school. The page title — "Christ the King JHS" — anchors the report to the institution rather than the genre.
  • Snapshot pill at the top — "Snapshot as of Friday, 1 May 2026 · 10:24 AM · academic year 2025/26 · Term 1." Stats change daily as students join/leave; the pill makes the freshness explicit. Useful for any export or PDF — the GES return needs to know exactly when it was generated.
  • "GES return PDF" header button — generates a pre-formatted PDF matching the Ghana Education Service return template. Schools file these termly; making it one click instead of an hour of copying is a real value prop. Same data, formatted to GES's expectations.
  • Featured KPI is total students in navy. The first question every visitor asks. +12 since term start tells trajectory in one phrase.
  • Student:teacher ratio is the headcount KPI subtitle. Ministry-grade metric — 16:1 reads as healthy (Ghana basic schools target 1:35 max, often 1:25-30 in urban private). Ratios over 25:1 would warrant flagging.
  • Class composition table is the load-bearing element. Every class with capacity bar, target line, student count, gender split mini-bar. The visual progress bar lets the eye scan to crowded classes (JHS 3A at 117%) and underused ones (JHS 1B at 80%).
  • Capacity target as a vertical line on the bar, not a separate column. The bar shows "where this class is" relative to "where it should be" in one glance. Color shifts from green (under target) to gold (at/near target) to terra (over).
  • JHS 3A flagged as "Over" in terracotta. 41 students against a 35 target — actionable insight for the headmistress. Either re-balance assignments next term or formally raise the target with the board.
  • Gender mini-bars per class using a quiet pink/blue palette deliberately distinct from the gold/terra of fee/discount data — different domain, different signal. Female:male ratio per class is something headmistresses watch (over-skewed classes can affect group dynamics).
  • Donut chart for school-wide gender with a hollow center showing the total. The slight majority annotation ("52% · slight majority") interprets the visual rather than leaving it to be read.
  • Enrolment flow as a tally, not a chart. Four event types (new admissions, withdrew, transferred out, graduated) with counts and View buttons. Each row is auditable — auditor clicks to see the actual student list.
  • Withdrew vs Transferred separately tracked. Different reasons, different conversations. A school with high withdrawals has a financial or experience problem; a school with high transfers is in a high-mobility area. Lumping them as "exits" hides the distinction.
  • Graduated count stays separate with its own gold dot icon. Graduates aren't losses — they're successes. The 38 who graduated last year went on to SHS; that's a school's job done, not a leak.
  • Net change footer consolidates the math: +15 in, −3 out, = +12 net. The graduation count from the previous academic year is excluded from this term's net.

Why this layout

  • Scale explainer is non-negotiable on this report. The 1-9 BECE scale (1 = best, 9 = fail) is counter-intuitive for anyone not from Ghana. Without an explicit explainer at the top of the page, readers will misinterpret a "school average of 3.4" as bad. The grade scale strip with color-coded interpretation is the single most important piece of design hygiene on this page.
  • Color follows meaning, not magnitude. Grades 1-3 = green (excellent/very good/good), 4-6 = gold (high avg / average / low avg), 7-9 = terracotta (pass / weak / fail). This means a "small number" reads as positive in the chart and a "big number" reads as negative — matching the scale, not standard convention.
  • Performance hero card in navy with the headline "3.4" and italic gold "good" interpretation. The interpretation is as important as the number. Right column reads off the strongest/weakest dimensions so the headmistress sees the texture in three seconds.
  • "Data completeness 87%" in the right column is honest. If the gradebook isn't fully populated, the headline number reflects partial truth. Telling the user this prevents over-confidence in the number.
  • Partial-data banner appears below the hero when any class is significantly under-graded. JHS 2B at 1 of 8 subjects shows the partial signal both in the banner (general callout) and in the table row (the "1/8" in terracotta). Banner is general; row treatment is specific.
  • By-class table mirrors the finance reports' shape deliberately. Same six-column structure as the term-collection by-class breakdown. Headmistress learns the layout once, applies it to multiple reports.
  • Trend column with directional arrows. ↑ green for "improved" (lower number than last term), ↓ terra for "worsened", → grey for flat. The −0.3 / +0.4 numbers are signed; minus is good. Same scale-aware convention as elsewhere on the page.
  • JHS 2B trend shows "—" with "partial data" annotation. Cannot compute term-on-term trend on a class with one subject's grades. Refusing to compute is more honest than computing on partial data and showing an unstable trend.
  • Subject hot/cold spots as a single grid of 8 cards rather than a bar chart. Small enough to scan visually; the colour does the ranking. Maths at 4.1 with "needs focus" annotation is the actionable signal — the headmistress has a conversation with the maths department.
  • Subject ranking is school-wide, not by class. Per-class subject performance is in the drill-through (click a class). The school-wide view tells the headmistress where to focus departmental attention; per-class is where she goes after that.
  • 71% of students in grades 1-4 in the hero context — the "high average or better" band is meaningful in Ghanaian education because it's the cutoff for "passing well" by ministry standards. Aggregate distributional stats matter, not just the mean.
  • No teacher names beyond the class teacher. The class is teacher-owned but the report stops short of "Mrs. Boateng's class scored 4.2." Avoids the trap of teacher-blaming a number that's mostly determined by cohort composition. Class identity is the unit; teaching attribution is a separate conversation.
