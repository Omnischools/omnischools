Why the bunk is the unit · and why moves are logged
Editorial · the spatial document the school actually runs on
The bunk as primary key

In a boarding house, the bunk is the address.

If you ask a Housemaster where a student is at 9:35 PM on a Tuesday — five minutes after lights out — they don't tell you which class the student is in, or which Form. They tell you Dorm D, Bunk 3. The bunk is the student's spatial identity in the boarding system, and every operational question — who inspected your locker this morning, who is your bunk-mate on either side, where will you sleep tonight, where is your chop box stored — keys off it.

The data model reflects this. A student record carries a residency_type. A boarder also has a `current_bunk_id` foreign key into the bunks table. Bunks know their dormitory, dormitories know their House, Houses know their school. The student-to-bunk link is *current*; the history is a separate `bunk_allocation_history` table that records every move, when, why, by whom. A student can occupy at most one bunk at a time — enforced at the database level. A bunk can hold at most one student — also enforced. The simple invariants are what makes the operational layer trustworthy.

Why log every move? Because boarding bunk changes are not casual. They are negotiations. A Form 1 student moved from Dorm F to Dorm G mid-term has probably been bullied, or has a medical issue, or has asked to be near a friend, or has been part of a disciplinary intervention. Each of these reasons matters and each is worth recording. Six months later, when the same student has another move proposed, the Housemaster wants to see the previous move's context. Five years later, when a former student writes for a reference letter and mentions that they were moved to Dorm A in Form 3 for "leadership development," the school wants to be able to verify that.

The history table is append-only. It records *every* change — including the resumption-day initial allocation, the once-a-year promotional moves (Form 1 → Form 2 dorms), and the disciplinary moves. The Housemaster's view shows the current state; the history is one click deeper.

One more invariant worth naming: a bunk has a single Housemaster. Bunks belong to dormitories, dormitories belong to Houses, and Houses have one resident Housemaster (with possibly an Assistant). When responsibility for a student needs to be determined — for a pastoral issue, for a discipline event, for a parent enquiry — the chain from student to responsible adult is one query deep: find the student's bunk, follow the chain up to the House, ask the Housemaster. Clear, fast, accountable. That is the architecture this surface enforces.

What the spatial document does not contain

  • Bunk-mate friendship maps. The school knows who sleeps near whom but does not infer social ties from physical proximity. Friendship is not data.
  • Bunk preferences. Students don't pick bunks. Form 1s are allocated to junior dorms by HM; Form 3s graduate to senior dorms; mid-form moves happen for cause.
  • Per-bunk fees. All bunks within a House cost the same. The premium is the House (or rather, the School) — not the bunk.
