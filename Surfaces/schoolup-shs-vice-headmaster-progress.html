Omnischools Senior · the management view of ledger entry

Who's done, who's not.

The Vice Headmaster Academic — or Head of Academic, depending on the school — runs the academic calendar. They need to know, ahead of every STPSHS submission window and every parent-report deadline, which teachers have completed their ledger entries and which have not. The discipline is precise: this surface shows completion progress, not score values. The Vice Headmaster sees that Mr. Owusu has entered all five categories for Form 2 Science Maths; they do not see what those scores are. Accountability without surveillance. Section 1 is the per-teacher progress table. Section 2 is the at-risk flags. Section 3 is the Headmaster's cascade roll-up — same data, aggregated one level up.

Completion progress, deliberately — not score surveillance

  • The navy banner names the discipline before anyone scrolls. "Completion progress, not score values." The Vice Headmaster cannot accidentally read this surface as a window into individual teachers' marks — the framing is explicit, the columns show category-entered states (✓ / ¾ / —), and the surface never shows a number from any student's gradebook. Inspecting actual scores requires navigating to the gradebook itself, which is audit-logged the same way Oversight's compliance record view is. Accountability without surveillance — the right posture for a tool that respects teacher autonomy over marks themselves.
  • Path A / B / C visible per row. A teacher chose auto-compile, paper-scan, or direct-digital for each class-subject. The Vice Headmaster sees which, because path choice affects what "complete" looks like — a Path C teacher entering scores cell-by-cell shows progress earlier than a Path B teacher waiting to scan at semester end. The pill colours match the ledger surface's colour vocabulary so the system feels consistent.
  • Sorted by STPSHS readiness, behind first. The Vice Headmaster's first job is to find the teachers who are not going to be ready when the submission window opens. The table defaults to that sort — Mr. Akoto's two Form 3 Arts classes show first, at-risk with zero categories entered and a 19-days-stale last-activity flag. The default order makes the most consequential cases visually loudest.
  • Asg / MS / ES / Proj / Port — the same five categories everywhere. The columns match the ledger view, the STPSHS export sheet, the report card, the analytics DB schema. One data model, surfaced in different views for different roles. The Vice Headmaster's view and the teacher's view share the same vocabulary, which means a conversation between them about "you haven't entered the mid-sem for Form 2 Maths yet" needs no translation.
  • "Behind · 4/5" tells the story precisely. A teacher with four of five categories done is *behind* (the window is approaching), not *at risk* (the window has arrived and they haven't moved). The three tiers — Ready / Behind / At risk — are calibrated against the calendar, not against an arbitrary completion percentage. Section 2 below shows how the at-risk flags get triggered.

Same anomaly discipline as Oversight, applied inside the school

  • The rules are config, not code. The five rules running here — teacher inactivity, STPSHS window approach, entry-rate outlier, score-down between uploads (from the ledger surface's diff logic), portfolio-only-pending — live in the same kind of `ref_anomaly_rule` table the Oversight anomaly queue uses. Each rule is inspectable as readable logic, severity-graded, enable/disable-able, and tunable by the school. The Headmaster can add a school-specific rule (`"no class incomplete by week 9"`) without a deploy.
  • Three severity tiers, calibrated against the calendar. High means "the window is approaching and a teacher needs to act this week." Medium means "something looks off and a brief conversation would help." Low means "noted, not urgent." The Vice Headmaster's working day shouldn't be filled with low-severity flags; the noise level is calibrated to surface what matters.
  • Each flag carries the rule that raised it. The small meta-line — "Rule: teacher inactivity 14+ days during semester · severity high" — names the discipline that produced the flag. This is the same readability-of-logic that Oversight's anomaly queue applies: a teacher who is flagged can be told why in language that does not require interpretation.
  • The medium flag is the one most school products would not surface. 38 scores in 4 minutes might be a competent teacher who has the marks lined up and types fast; might also be a teacher inventing scores under deadline pressure. Most school software would not flag it. Omnischools surfaces it as a conversation prompt, not an accusation — the action button reads "Review with Mrs. Coleman" because the Vice Headmaster's job is to talk first, conclude second.
  • "Message all flagged teachers" is the bulk action. A Vice Headmaster who finds three flags at the start of the week can compose one templated message that goes to each flagged teacher with their specific gap referenced — "you have N categories outstanding for class X, the window opens in 17 days, can we talk?" — instead of three individual notes. The discipline reduces the management overhead of the system rather than creating it.

One level up, same data, the Headmaster's question answered

  • The Headmaster does not want per-teacher detail by default. A Headmaster runs the whole school; per-teacher granularity is the Vice Headmaster's domain. The roll-up answers the Headmaster's actual question — "is the school ready for STPSHS?" — at the subject level. Three subjects ready, five partial, one at risk. The answer fits on one screen.
  • The discipline that surfaces the actual action item. Underneath the three numbers, the gold-bordered card names the most urgent case (Mr. Akoto's Government classes) and what the Vice Headmaster is already doing about it. The Headmaster does not need to dig — the system surfaces the case where their personal attention or sign-off is most likely to be needed. The action button is "Message Mrs. Anim," not "Message Mr. Akoto" — because escalating past the line manager is the wrong move and the system reflects the hierarchy.
  • The cascade is genuinely the Vice Headmaster's data rolled up, not a separate calculation. Same per-teacher progress, aggregated by subject. A Headmaster who wants to see Mr. Akoto's row specifically clicks "Open full progress view" and lands in the Vice Headmaster's view — same data, deeper granularity. No data lives in only one of the two surfaces.
  • Subjects, not just totals. "9 ready / 8 behind / 1 at risk" would be the right total but the wrong framing — the Headmaster needs to know which subjects, because Government being at risk is a different problem from Mathematics being at risk (Government is one teacher; Mathematics is six). The subject-list line under each tile names the specific subjects and per-subject teacher counts, so the Headmaster reads the situation, not just the number.
  • "Brief the board" is the action button. The Headmaster's reporting obligations run upward to the Board of Governors and onward to GES through Oversight. The roll-up surface is also a briefing-input surface — a Headmaster preparing for a board meeting or a district director's visit drops these three figures and the action note into their briefing. Same data, third surface, same five-category model underneath.
