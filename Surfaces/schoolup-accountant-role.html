Why this layout

  • Two-card binary choice with concrete recommendations. Headmistress doesn't need to think abstractly about role models — she picks the one that matches her school.
  • Card 2 (separated) is selected by default only as a wireframe convention. In production, no default — make her actively choose. Either choice should feel right; neither is "the upsell."
  • "Typical for schools with..." footnote on each card gives a soft anchor. Schools self-identify by size; the description does the role-design work for them.
  • Accountant invite fields revealed below, not in a separate step. Reduces clicks — if the school has an accountant, they probably already know the email.
  • Permission summary surfaced upfront, not buried in a "learn more" link. Headmistress sees exactly what the accountant will and won't be able to do before sending the invite. Trust by transparency.
  • Three permission states: ✓ green (full action), i gold (read-only), ✕ neutral (no access). The gold mid-state matters — accountant needs to see students to invoice them but not edit student records.
  • Step count went from 6 to 7. Adding this question cleanly without burying it is worth the extra step. Headmistress can skip the accountant invite by leaving fields blank — just enables the role for later.
  • "You can change this anytime" reassurance in the rail. Adding/removing the accountant role doesn't migrate data, just toggles permissions.

Why this layout

  • Same shell as admin, different sidebar. Joseph navigates the app the way Florence does — same drawer, same buttons, same MoMo flows. Only the available items differ.
  • Six sidebar items, not nine. Dashboard, Students (view), Classes (view), Billing (active), Reports, Profile. No Staff, no Announcements, no Achievements, no Settings. Reduces cognitive load and prevents accidental incursions into admin territory.
  • "VIEW" badge on read-only items sets expectation up front. Joseph knows tapping Students will let him see students but not edit them — no surprise click into an empty form.
  • "Reports" gets its own top-level item for the accountant. For admins it can live under Billing; for accountants it's the second most-used screen. Match the surface to the user.
  • Welcome banner on first login says exactly what role he has, who invited him, and what the badge convention means. Shows once, dismissable.
  • "Today's payments" card is a small but specific accountant feature. Headmistress wants term totals; accountant wants today's tally to balance against the cash drawer at end-of-day.
  • Same KPIs as admin billing. No reason to fragment — total billed / collected / outstanding / overdue are the four numbers that matter to anyone in finance.
  • Footer shows "Accountant" not "Admin." Important for Joseph's own confidence ("yes, this is my account") and for any colleague glancing at his screen.
