Omnischools Oversight · the signature analysis

Who is enrolled — and who is missing.

This is the analysis that justifies Oversight existing as a separate product. A single school knows its own enrolment; no school can see the wider picture of children in its catchment. GES can begin to — by setting enrolment at each stage against the population of children who are of the official age for that stage. The comparison is strictly stage-for-stage on a matched age basis: JHS enrolment is measured against the count of children of JHS age (~12–14) living in the district, Primary against Primary-age children (~6–11), never against total population and never across stages. But the surface is careful about one thing: Omnischools only sees enrolment at schools that are on the platform — 34 of the district's 40 schools. So the difference between population and what we record is an unmeasured gap, not an out-of-school count. Part of that gap is children enrolled at the 6 schools we cannot see; only the remainder is a possible out-of-school signal. This surface shows the gap and splits it — it does not claim a single out-of-school number while coverage is partial. The reference population comes from the Ghana Statistical Service 2021 Census; the demo jurisdiction is Wassa Amenfi West.

Enrolment vs population.

Setting recorded enrolment at each stage against the school-age population for that same stage — and being honest about what partial school coverage means for the result.
∑
How this comparison is constructed
Every stage is compared only against the population of children who are of the official age for that stage. JHS enrolment is set against children aged 12–14; it is never compared to total district population, and never to a different stage's age band. This keeps the comparison honest — a district is not "under-enrolled" simply because it has many infants or many adults. But a second honesty matters just as much: Omnischools only records enrolment at schools on the platform. The difference between population and recorded enrolment is therefore an unmeasured gap — it is not an out-of-school count, because part of it is children at schools we cannot yet see.

Why this is not an "out of school" number — yet
The district's EMIS register lists 40 schools; 34 are on Omnischools and report into this analysis. The other 6 schools have pupils who are enrolled — those children are in school, just not visible to us. So the unmeasured gap has two parts that must never be merged:
Part 1 · explainable
Children enrolled at the 6 non-Omnischools schools. In school — outside our coverage. Resolved by onboarding those schools, not by intervention.
Part 2 · the real signal
The remainder after Part 1 is set aside — a possible out-of-school signal worth investigating. Only this part is an enrolment concern.
A true out-of-school figure needs full district coverage. Until all 40 schools report, this surface shows the gap and its split — it does not collapse them into one "out of school" headline. Onboarding the remaining 6 schools is what turns this estimate into a real count.

These are the official ages. Real cohorts include over-age and under-age pupils — a child repeating P3 or starting JHS late. The analysis reports the official-age comparison as the headline and flags large over-age populations separately, because an over-age JHS pupil is enrolled but also a sign of earlier repetition.

The method has to be visible — and honest about its blind spot

  • The surface refuses to claim an out-of-school number while coverage is partial. The most important design decision here is a negative one: Omnischools sees 34 of 40 schools, so the population-minus-enrolment difference is an unmeasured gap, not an out-of-school count. A chunk of it is children enrolled at the 6 invisible schools. Collapsing that into one alarming "out of school" headline would mislead a director the moment they repeated it upward — so the surface does not.
  • The gap is split into two parts that are never merged. Part 1 is explainable — children at non-Omnischools schools, resolved by onboarding not intervention. Part 2 is the remainder, the only part that is a genuine enrolment concern. Keeping these separate is what makes the analysis trustworthy at partial coverage.
  • Full coverage is named as the precondition for a real count. The surface states plainly that a true out-of-school figure needs all 40 schools reporting — and that onboarding the remaining 6 is what converts this estimate into a count. The coverage gap is not a footnote; it is the stated limit of the analysis.
  • Stage-for-stage on a matched age basis is still the rule. JHS recorded enrolment compares to the 12–14 population, not to total population and not to another stage. The comparison is only meaningful when numerator and denominator describe the same children — that holds regardless of coverage.
  • The age-for-stage mapping is configuration, and over-age pupils are named as a wrinkle. Ghana's KG/Primary/JHS/SHS age bands are data a GES administrator sets; real cohorts include over-age and under-age children, flagged separately rather than hidden.
  • The reference population is sourced explicitly. GSS 2021 Census, district age tables (Volume 3B). Per Decision 7 this is loaded reference data — the surface names its origin so a director knows the denominator's vintage.

Reading the stage bands

  • Each band is one honest division. Recorded enrolment over age-matched population — the shaded track is the 2021 Census population for that age band, the solid bar is what the 34 Omnischools schools recorded. The shaded remainder is the unmeasured gap, not an out-of-school count: it includes children at the 6 non-Omnischools schools.
  • The split banner sits above the bands deliberately. Before a director reads any stage, the banner has already established that the 3,940-child gap is two things — ~2,410 likely at invisible schools, ~1,530 a possible out-of-school signal — and that they are not added back into one figure. The per-stage bands then show where the gap concentrates, read through that frame.
  • The SHS gap carries an extra caveat beyond coverage. SHS recorded enrolment is low partly for the coverage reason, and partly because SHS placement via CSSPS is national — district children are routinely placed at SHS in other districts and would not appear here even at full coverage. The provenance row says so; a director must not read the SHS rate as pure local drop-off.
  • Primary is near-universal; the ends of the system show the widest gaps. Recorded Primary enrolment at 96% against KG 81% and SHS 51% — but "widest gap" is not yet "most out-of-school children," because the gap is unresolved. The shape is a signal of where to look, not a verdict.
  • Colour encodes gap width, not confirmed severity. Green Primary, amber KG and JHS, red SHS — the same palette as the dashboard. It flags where the unmeasured gap is largest and so where investigation and school-onboarding should focus first.

The gap has a location — read through coverage

  • The circuit table adds a coverage column, and it changes everything. A circuit's recorded rate cannot be read without knowing how many of its schools are on Omnischools. Samreboi's 80.5% looks like the worst under-enrolment in the district — but at 7 of 10 schools, much of that gap is simply the 3 schools we cannot see. Asankrangwa Central's smaller gap is more trustworthy precisely because it is at full 12/12 coverage.
  • The circuit to worry about is "full coverage, wide gap." The surface says this explicitly: a low rate plus partial coverage is ambiguous; a low rate plus full coverage is a real signal. This reframes the director's task — first close coverage, then read the gap — rather than chasing what might be an artefact.
  • Onboarding the missing schools is the resolution, and the surface keeps saying so. Every level — the banner split, the stage bands, this circuit table — points to the same fix: get all 40 schools reporting. That converts an unmeasured gap into a measured one. It is the recurring call to action because it is the honest one.
  • Every circuit row keeps the matched-age rule. Samreboi's rate is its recorded JHS enrolment over Samreboi's JHS-age population. The stage-for-stage discipline holds at circuit level; coverage is the second caveat layered on top, not a replacement for it.
  • Cross-signal corroboration still helps — carefully. Samreboi shows a wide gap, the worst PTR, and the steepest BECE drop. PTR and BECE come from schools that are on Omnischools, so they are measured; together they make Samreboi worth a visit even though its enrolment gap alone is coverage-ambiguous.
  • Comparison against neighbours preserves the method. Putting Wassa Amenfi West beside Wassa Amenfi East stays stage-for-stage and should also surface each district's coverage, so a director never compares a well-covered district's rate against a poorly-covered one as if equal.
