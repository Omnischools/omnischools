The light log captures the boarding-day relevant fact: who is not in their usual place during scheduled activities? The boarding day surface only needs the operational fact: M. Akoto is in sick bay, not in Dorm A, not at prep tonight unless cleared.

What this surface compresses

  • The inspection register. Previously a paper book held by the HM in their bungalow. Now searchable, time-stamped, parent-visible.
  • The prep roll. Previously a clipboard at the prep room door. Now auto-populated from the House roster, late-flags fire to HM phone.
  • The siesta enforcement notice. Previously a sign on the wall. Now a check on whether any of the 119 students moved out of their dorm during the silent hour (door sensor feed, future).
  • The sick bay slip. Previously a triplicate carbon book — student, matron, parent. Now a digital log with parent SMS automatic.

Why the inspection is daily and weekly

  • Daily inspection (06:10, 15 min) is the quick check — bunks made, lockers shut, attire complete. Pass/partial/fail per dorm. Patterns matter more than any single failure.
  • Weekly inspection (Sat 8 AM, 60 min) is the deep check — whole-House, including spaces no student visits daily (washrooms, drying lines, the chop box storage room, the bicycle shed). Separate dataset, separate cadence.
  • Don't conflate them. A school that only does the weekly version misses the daily-attire drift; one that only does the daily misses the structural rot.

Why daily life is the surface used most
Editorial · the everyday as the architectural test
The Housemaster's homepage

If a Housemaster only ever opens one surface, it's this one.

The resumption-day surface is dramatic and gets two days a year. The discipline ladder is consequential and gets used when needed. The bunk allocation is foundational and gets reviewed once a term. But this surface — the boarding day in progress — is the Housemaster's homepage. Every day, twice a day, three times a day, Mr Mensah opens this and looks at it. It is the surface that decides whether the software gets opened or abandoned.

The design challenge is the opposite of resumption day. There, we had to compress one chaotic day into a single screen. Here, we have to make a screen that is quiet enough to be background when the day is going well, and noisy enough to be foreground when something is going wrong. The "NOW" strip is gold and pulsing when something is happening. The summary tiles flip from green to warn when an inspection slips. The Wednesday flag fires only on Wednesdays. The surface earns the Housemaster's attention only when there is something to attend to.

One thing this surface deliberately does not do: it does not try to be the matron's surface, the dean's surface, or the headmaster's surface. The matron's full sick bay log — symptoms, treatment, medication, parent calls — is a separate module that gets its own batch. From here, we see only the operational fact: who is not in their usual place during scheduled activities. The dean's pastoral view — VLC, FM notes, character paragraph — is held in VLC, not here. The headmaster's dashboard pulls from this surface but does not live in it. Surface separation is what makes any one surface usable.

The fifteen state transitions per day — rising, bathing, duties, inspection, roll call, assembly, classes, lunch, siesta, wash-down, readwide, supper, prep, night prayer, lights out — are all on this screen, but only one is highlighted at any moment. The "NOW" pattern was designed for this surface and earned its place. The Housemaster glances at the screen, sees "Siesta · 46 minutes remaining", knows the House is silent, and can return to whatever else is on her mind. That is the design success: the screen does not require attention, it offers it.

One small but defendable choice: the day timeline reads left-to-right with the past in green, NOW in gold, future faded. This is not a metaphor we invented — it's the universal direction of human time-perception. The Housemaster does not need to learn it. The green slots have happened; the gold slot is happening; the faded slots are coming. The clipboard is gone, the WhatsApp group is gone, the carbon-copy book is gone, and in its place is a one-screen status of one hundred and nineteen young people, half of whom are sleeping, all of whom are accounted for.

The screen earns its place by

  • Being honest. If inspection partially failed Dorm D, the surface says so. It does not soften, hide, or aggregate the issue into a green bar.
  • Being current. NOW is NOW. Not "as of 6 AM today." Not "last refreshed 3 hours ago." Live.
  • Being quiet when nothing is wrong. A clean inspection day shows seven green tiles and one gold-NOW pulse. The eye glances and moves on.
  • Being loud when something is wrong. A failed inspection turns terra. A missing student at prep fires an alert. The Wednesday flag fires only on Wednesdays.

Where this surface fails gracefully

  • Offline. The Housemaster's phone is the source of truth at the dorm — internet is unreliable in the bungalow. The mobile version of this surface queues updates and sync when reconnected. Audit catches the gap.
  • Tomorrow. The same surface, scrolled forward one day, shows yesterday's history. Pattern detection across days is one click further.
