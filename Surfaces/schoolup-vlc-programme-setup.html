Eleven values, twenty-two sessions, one year

Values Learning Communities (VLC) are Omnischools's student-facing pastoral programme — the counterpart to PLCs for teachers. Every Wednesday at 2:30 PM, every class meets for sixty minutes around one of eleven core values drawn from Ghanaian school tradition. Form Master frames, two trained Peer Guides lead small-group discussions, the class share-backs in plenary, every student journals privately for ten minutes, and the FM closes. The rhythm is the design. Twenty-two sessions over thirty teaching weeks leaves eight weeks of slack for the realities of a Ghanaian school calendar — exams, sports, public holidays, and the days the rain takes the roof off the assembly hall.

Why this pattern, and not Prefects

Peer Guides are not Prefects. A Prefect carries authority — enforces rules, reports infractions, represents the school. A Peer Guide facilitates a conversation — leads small-group discussion of a value, holds space for a peer's struggle, escalates a pastoral concern to the Form Master. Different role, different selection, different training. A student can hold both at once · most don't. The PG role exists to put students in front of each other on questions that adults asking would distort.

What this surface configures

  • The 11 values are an ordered list per school. Default is the Omnischools canonical set; schools can reorder, rename English/Twi, or extend to 12-13 with Dean approval.
  • Each value has session A & session B — A introduces the idea, B applies it. Curriculum library holds the prompts; Dean & senior staff can edit.
  • The Wednesday cadence is master-timetable protected. Classes can defer with Dean approval (exam week, sports day) — auto-reschedules to the following Wednesday.
  • 5-phase rhythm is locked but configurable. Schools can adjust durations; the 5+25+15+10+5 default is the Omnischools opinion based on teacher feedback during pilot.
  • Term arc is sequence-locked. Foundations → Interpersonal → Integration. The same order every academic year. Builds from self → others → community over 30 weeks.
  • Peer Guides are configured here. Eligibility (F2/F3 only), gender pairing default, tenure length, training cadence. Per-class roster is on surface 2.

Permissions

  • Headmaster · view all · approves any sequence change
  • Form Master · view their class's schedule · cannot edit curriculum

VLC is not RME · why both exist
Editorial · the boundary that matters
The boundary

RME teaches about values. VLC creates the space to practice them.

Ghanaian schools already teach Religious & Moral Education and Citizenship Education — these are examinable NaCCA subjects, with curriculum, textbooks, weekly periods, end-of-term assessments, and grades that appear on transcripts. They are essential. They are not the same thing as VLC, and trying to make VLC replace them or duplicate them would be a category error.

RME is taught by a subject teacher with a syllabus and a grading rubric. The teacher knows what the answer is — or at least, what the examination wants the student to write. VLC is led by the Form Master and two Peer Guides, on a Wednesday afternoon, in the class's own room, with no exam at the end. There is no grade on the transcript. There is no right answer to write down. There is a private journal entry, a Form Master who knows the class as humans, and the slow accumulation of twenty-two sessions across nine months that becomes the character paragraph in the student's school-leaver report.

The Form Master leads VLC for the same reason a guidance counsellor leads pastoral care: they know the students. They've taught them Maths or English for two terms already. They've watched them through a flu outbreak and a house competition and a difficult assembly. When Value 6 is Compassion and a student goes quiet, the FM knows what that quiet means — in a way no subject teacher rotating through a syllabus could. The Peer Guides add the dimension only students can bring: the friend who can ask the question that an adult asking would distort.

Already exists · NaCCA-mandated
RME & Citizenship Education
  • Examinable subject · WASSCE grade on transcript
  • Taught by subject teacher with textbook
  • Weekly periods · ~3-4 per week
  • End-of-term tests, end-of-year exams
  • Curriculum prescribed nationally
  • Goal: knowledge of moral & civic principles
New addition · Omnischools design
VLC · this programme
  • Not examined · no grade · pastoral by nature
  • Led by Form Master + 2 Peer Guides
  • One 60-min session per week · Wednesday
  • Private journal · contributes to character paragraph
  • Curriculum school-designed · 11 values + 2 sessions each
  • Goal: practice of values in a community of peers

The two are complementary, not competing. A school running both has the examinable knowledge work and the lived community work each in their proper place. One teaches the syllabus, the other forms the person.

Why this matters for design

  • Don't put VLC entries on the transcript. The transcript is the formal academic record. VLC outputs (reflection journals, FM pastoral notes) live on the school-leaver character paragraph — a separate document.
  • Don't treat reflections like assignments. Reflections are append-only, never graded, never visible to peers. The student writes, the FM reads, that's it.
  • Don't try to "grade" the values. A student who writes a thin reflection isn't failing VLC. The reflection is for them, not for you. Engagement matters; performance does not.
  • Don't let parents see the journal. Pastoral confidentiality. The FM may share themes if a serious concern arises, not the entries themselves.
