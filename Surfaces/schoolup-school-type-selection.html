Step 2 · What kind of school?

One question, three answers, an enormous downstream impact. The school-type selection in the onboarding wizard determines which modules activate, which surfaces appear in nav, which compliance rails the school is plugged into. Get this right at minute three and everything else falls into place; get it wrong and admin reconfigures painfully later. The design has to feel calm even though the choice is significant.

Why this is Step 2, not Step 1

Step 1 captures who the admin is and the school's legal name — facts they can fill in instantly. Step 2 asks them to think about their school's structure, which deserves the moment of attention. Putting it first would feel interrogative; putting it after identity creates a natural rhythm: here is me, here is what I run, here is how it looks. The lede acknowledges the choice can be changed, removing decision pressure.

Calls worth flagging

  • Three options, not seven. Real Ghanaian school types span KG-only, Primary-only, JHS-only, SHS, SHTS, Vocational Institutes, and combinations. Reducing to three options trades configurability for clarity — admin doesn't see "KG-only" as a separate choice because under the hood it's a Basic school with only KG classes activated.
  • Pricing is shown. Hiding price until checkout breaks trust. Admin sees the range, knows they're in the right tier, no surprise at the end.
  • Setup time estimates (15m / 45m / 60m) are honest, not aspirational. SHS is meaningfully more complex to set up because programmes and Houses need configuration — surface that upfront so admin sets aside the time.
  • The "Senior" option is gold-highlighted as selected by default in this mock — in production, no option is pre-selected. Admin must actively choose. The mock shows what it looks like when selected.
  • "Multi-tier school" is the right framing for school groups/complexes — Multi-tenant SaaS terminology ("multi-school account") would confuse admins. Multi-tier means multi-level in education-speak, which is exactly what it is.

The cascade · what each choice unlocks downstream
How Step 2 reshapes Steps 3–7

If admin picks each option, the rest of the wizard adapts

configures by school type · no manual toggling
Basic · KG / Primary / JHS
  • Step 4 · Academic year · 3 terms, standard GES calendar
  • Step 5 · Classes · year-based (KG1 → JHS3) with optional sections
  • Step 5 · Subjects · GES basic-school curriculum pre-loaded
  • Step 6 · Fees · single fee structure per year
  • Step 7 · Staff · single role per teacher
  • Nav after setup: Dashboard · Students · Classes · Attendance · Billing · Comms · Reports · Settings
Senior · SHS / SHTS
  • Step 3.5 (new) · Programmes & Houses · admin activates 1–7 programmes, defines House list
  • Step 4 · Academic year · 3 terms, SHS-specific exam windows
  • Step 5 · Classes · Form × Programme × Section (e.g. "Form 1 General Arts A")
  • Step 5 · Subjects · core block + electives per programme
  • Step 6 · Fees · Free SHS toggle · boarding vs day fee split
  • Step 7 · Staff · multi-role + GES rank fields
  • Nav: adds Programmes · Houses · PLC · CPD · VLC · Boarding
Multi-tier · Basic + Senior
  • Step 2.5 (new) · Tier configuration · admin sets which tiers run, optionally names campuses
  • Steps 3–7 split: separate setup per tier, shared identity and branding
  • Cross-tier reports activated · group-level dashboard at root
  • Admin permissions can be scoped per tier (a basic-school admin doesn't see SHS data)
  • Billing supports family-level invoicing across tiers (a parent with a JHS3 child and an SHS1 child gets one invoice if they want)
  • Nav: root dashboard with per-tier nav inside
The principle · activate, don't surface

Admin doesn't see this cascade. The wizard just adapts. Picking Senior at Step 2 silently injects "Step 3.5 · Programmes & Houses" into the flow; picking Multi-tier injects "Step 2.5 · Tier configuration" before branding. The user experience is "the wizard knew what to ask next" — not "I checked 4 sub-options on a configuration page". Adaptation that feels like attentiveness, not menus.

Calls worth flagging

  • "Step 3.5" and "Step 2.5" are illustrative — in production the steps just renumber. The wizard never shows fractional steps to admin. Internal naming for design clarity only.
  • Multi-tier shared identity is the right default — most school groups have one brand (Christ the King Schools), one set of contact details, one set of admin staff. The complexity is in operations, not branding.
  • The basic school nav is shorter than SHS nav by design — features hidden by school type are not greyed-out, they're absent. A basic-school admin should never see "PLC" anywhere because PLC doesn't apply to them.
  • The "Free SHS toggle" in the senior cascade matters — public SHS schools have all fees absorbed by government; private SHS schools charge normally. This is one checkbox but it cascades through billing significantly.
  • Cross-tier family invoicing in Multi-tier is the trust-significant feature — one MoMo prompt covers fees for both children. Builds parent goodwill in ways the school feels indirectly.

The mobile reality

Some admins will onboard on their phone — the SMS link with the setup magic-link opens on whatever device they're holding. The wizard must work on a 380px-wide screen.

One option per row, full width. The horizontal stats panel from desktop becomes a single-line meta under each option. The cascade preview doesn't show on mobile — it's a desktop affordance for the admin who wants to understand the implications. Mobile users trust the wizard to ask the right next questions.

What's stripped

Gone from mobile: the "what's included" bullets, the setup time estimate, the cascade preview. The reassurance message also moves below the fold.

What stays: the option name, a single descriptor line, the price range, the radio selector. Everything else can be discovered or trusted.

The mobile flow is for an admin who already knows roughly what they want. Detailed comparison is a desktop affordance.

Mobile-first vs mobile-fallback

Omnischools is mobile-first for parents and teachers but desktop-natural for admin work. The wizard is an admin task — most admins will run it from a desk or staff-room computer. Mobile support is a fallback for the admin who started the process on their phone after getting the SMS invite. Make it work, don't make it primary.

04
Edge cases · changing your mind
After onboarding · when school type needs to change

Three scenarios · three answers

all routable from Settings → School type
Easy · pick wrong, change early
  • Admin set up as Basic, realises it's actually Senior · within first 7 days, before students enrolled
  • One-click change from Settings → School type
  • Wizard re-runs from Step 2.5 (Programmes & Houses) onward · keeps identity and branding
  • No data lost · audit log records the change
Awkward · already in use
  • School has been operational for weeks, type now needs to change · students enrolled, fees paid, attendance recorded
  • Omnischools accepts the change but flags fields that need manual reconciliation
  • Example: Basic → Senior · existing classes need to map to Form/Programme/Section · admin walks through a guided reconciliation
  • Audit log records reconciliation steps · old data stays
Promotion · adding a tier · the natural growth case
  • Basic school adds a Senior section · the most common reason for type change · happens often in growing schools
  • One-click promotion from Basic → Multi-tier · keeps everything in the Basic side untouched
  • Omnischools adds a new wizard flow for the Senior tier · admin sets up Senior programmes, Houses, classes
  • Pricing changes from monthly Basic rate to Multi-tier rate · prorated for the current billing month
  • Existing students stay in their Basic classes · new students can be admitted to Senior · the JHS3 cohort gets a "Promote to Form 1" flow at end of year
The principle · types are not commitments

School-type selection is meaningful but not permanent. Ghanaian schools grow, restructure, merge, split, add boarding, add new levels — the product has to accommodate that reality. The wizard's "you can change this later" language is not a polite lie; the change paths exist and are designed honestly. The hardest case is "awkward · already in use" and it's still solvable, just slower.

Calls worth flagging

  • The "promotion" case is the most common in our target market — a private basic school sees demand and adds a senior section. Omnischools's natural growth path supports this without forcing the school to migrate to a new account.
  • Manual reconciliation for "Awkward" cases is a guided flow, not a free-text form. Admin walks through each class and assigns it to a Form/Programme/Section. Omnischools suggests defaults based on class name ("JHS 3 maps to Form 1? Or do you mean it stays as a Basic class?").
  • The "audit log records the change" line matters — type change is one of the highest-impact admin actions; auditors will want to know who did it and why. Same forward-only audit pattern used throughout Omnischools.
  • Pricing change on promotion is prorated, not retroactive — the school doesn't get billed for Multi-tier rate for the past 3 months. Builds trust by being fair on the seam.
  • "Demotion" (Senior → Basic) is technically possible but vanishingly rare and not worth surfacing as an obvious path. Goes through support if needed, not self-service. Don't make easy something that's rarely the right answer.
