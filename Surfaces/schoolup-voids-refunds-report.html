Why this layout

  • Health banner at the top sets interpretive frame before any numbers. "Healthy reversal rate · 0.78% of collected" with a green checkmark immediately tells the headmistress this isn't a problem term. The benchmark (under 2%) is named so admin knows where the line is. If reversals exceed 2%, the banner flips to a warn-styled "needs attention" treatment.
  • Featured KPI in terra-bordered card instead of the usual navy. Voids/refunds are inherently negative-flow events; the colour signals that without exaggerating. The headline number "−GHS 740.00" makes the cedi impact immediate.
  • Voids and refunds split into separate KPI cards, not blended. They're conceptually different (per the void/refund flow earlier — void = wrong record, refund = money returned). Blending would lose the distinction the rest of the product preserves.
  • "Avg per event GHS 246.67 · in line with avg payment size" — a reassurance sentence. If the average reversal were GHS 4,000 in a school where typical payments are GHS 400, that pattern would warrant alarm. Surfacing the comparison answers the question before it's asked.
  • Trend chart shows weeks with separated void/refund segments. Stacked bars within each week column. Most weeks are empty (zero reversals); the few with activity stand out. Same x-axis as the term-collection chart so the eye can compare timing.
  • Comparison annotation in the legend — "vs 5 events in Term 3 · 2025 last year" — lets the reader contextualise. 3 this term vs 5 last term reads as improving without needing a separate trend page.
  • Reason categories surfaced as a list, not a donut. Six categories are too many for a donut to read clearly; the list ranks by count with cedi values. Empty categories ("Withdrew · 0 · —") shown at reduced opacity so the audience knows the system tracks them, just none happened.
  • "Wrong amount entered · GHS 0.00 net effect" — the void was for GHS 4,000 (the wrong entry) but a correct GHS 400 was re-recorded immediately, so net cash impact is zero. The annotation prevents the reader thinking the school lost GHS 4,000.
  • Void vs refund split cards show the two streams side-by-side with their tag-lines ("the record was wrong" / "we sent money back"). Each has its own breakdown by sub-category. The "Money usually didn't move" / "Money actually went back" pills are the pedagogical reminder of what the difference means.
  • Recorded-by analysis with concentration threshold. Headmistress sees who recorded each reversal — useful for training. Threshold for the warning flag is 50% concentration; below that, both people are flagged "Normal" green. With 3 events, single-person concentrations are statistically meaningless, so the threshold prevents false alarms.
  • "Helps spot training opportunities" framing — not "blame the staff." The right framing matters here. Nobody likes being singled out; the language and the high threshold protect against weaponising the report.
  • Event list with full reason text in italic. Each row carries the actual reason recorded at void time. Auditor scans the column and reads why each reversal happened without clicking through. The receipt/replacement IDs are mono-font and gold for the receipt number — same convention as audit log.
  • Refund row has soft-red row tint to distinguish from voids. Voids are corrections; refunds involve actual money movement. Visual signal reinforces the distinction.
  • "Open" button per row goes to the payment detail page (already designed) where the full audit timeline lives. Report links into existing surfaces; doesn't recreate them.
