Why this layout

  • Checklist instead of "no data" message. The headmistress just spent 15 minutes in onboarding — she expects to see results. The checklist tells her there's still work to do, and exactly what.
  • Progress bar is segmented, not continuous. Each segment maps to one task — gives a clearer "I've done X of Y" signal than a sliding percentage.
  • Step 3 (add students) is the active one, highlighted gold. The other 4 are either done (struck through, gold checkmark) or waiting. One unambiguous next action.
  • "Locked" state on step 4 is honest — issuing invoices requires students. Better to show the dependency than to let her click and see an error.
  • "Skip for now" on step 5 distinguishes truly optional from required. The first announcement is nice; running the school doesn't depend on it.
  • Side panel sets expectation about quietness. A blank dashboard reads as broken; the timeline reframes it as the normal week-one state. "It's quiet because nobody has done anything yet — that's the way it should be" is the key line.
  • Callback offer for stuck users. Many headmistresses won't ask for help in writing but will pick up a call. The promise of a real human in Accra (not chatbot) is concrete trust.
  • No fake KPIs. Some products show "GHS 0.00 collected" with stylised charts on day one; we don't. A zero with a gradient still says "this is how the product looks normally" — confusing the user about what success looks like later.

Why this layout

  • Welcome card uses navy + gold, not white. The teacher's first dashboard moment is celebratory — she's joined the school's system. Dark hero treatment marks the occasion.
  • Class assignments shown immediately — the most valuable thing the teacher can see is "what am I responsible for here?" Form teacher status flagged on JHS 2A because that role carries more responsibility.
  • "Take a quick tour" / "View my classes" — two paths for two personalities. Tour for cautious teachers, jump-in-and-explore for confident ones.
  • "While you wait for students" tile is the most important. Most teachers would assume they can do nothing until students arrive — actually they can draft assignments, prep gradebook columns, write announcements. Productive idle.
  • Reference to Florence by name is intentional. Not "the admin" — a person. Reduces the corporate feeling and makes the school feel small and human.
  • The lede is reassuring, not informational. "Your students are on their way" reframes the empty state as a process, not a problem.
  • No fake numbers. The dashboard doesn't show "0 students" or "0 assignments" anywhere — empty cards are designed as cards, not as data displays returning zero.

Parent · day one

Why this layout

  • Same shape, same hero card on both screens. Student and parent see "your account / you're following" with the child's name in navy. Visual consistency reinforces that they're seeing two views of one shared truth.
  • Hero copy reframes empty as "early." "Term 1 just started" is honest and forward-looking. Avoids the pitfall of "Nothing here yet!" with a sad face.
  • Time-anchored expectations. "From Term 1, Week 2" or "Within the first week" tells the user when content will appear, not just that it will. Specificity buys patience.
  • Coming-soon cards are styled like real cards, not skeletons or placeholders. Sets the visual rhythm of what filled cards will look like — when grades arrive, the user already knows where they'll be.
  • The achievements card is the only one with an active CTA ("+ Add one now" in green). Achievements are user-generated and don't need teachers — student can post immediately. Gives a small but real win on day one.
  • Parent's tip row is about fees, student's is about Mr. Mensah. Each role gets the most-relevant hint.
  • Tab bars unchanged. Empty doesn't mean reduced navigation — the user should still see the full app and be able to explore.
  • No "skip for now" or "dismiss" on these cards. They auto-disappear when content arrives; not interactive clutter to dismiss.
