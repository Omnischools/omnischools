Omnischools Oversight · the gated exception

When a name is actually needed.

Every other Oversight surface is aggregate — counts, rates, trends, no individual identifiable. This is the one controlled exception. For specific compliance work — verifying a Free SHS claim, investigating a withdrawal anomaly, a statutory audit, a safeguarding case — GES sometimes needs an individual student's or teacher's record. The compliance record view is where that happens, and it is deliberately built with friction: a reason must be stated before anything is shown, the access is written to the audit log the moment it is granted, and only the fields the stated reason justifies are revealed. Per Decision 6, this is GES's own internal control over how its officers use a power they legitimately hold. The demo shows a district director opening one student record to verify a Free SHS enrolment claim.

The gate is the control, not a formality

  • Friction here is the design working. Every other Oversight surface opens instantly because it is aggregate. This one stops the user and demands a reason — the friction is deliberate, because a named-record access should never be a casual click. The gate banner spells out that the user is leaving aggregate view.
  • The reason is a controlled list, and it shapes what is revealed. Four grounds — claim verification, withdrawal anomaly, statutory audit, safeguarding. The hint says it plainly: the reason limits which fields unlock, so claim verification does not expose health data. Purpose-bound access, enforced by the form, not just promised.
  • The explanation is stored verbatim in the audit log. Not a tick-box — a written justification including, where one exists, a case reference. A reviewer reading the log later sees the actual reasoning, in the officer's own words. That is what makes the log auditable rather than decorative.
  • The consent line names the real consequence. "Permanently logged against my name… subject to GES audit review." The officer acknowledges the specific accountability, not a generic agreement — consistent with Decision 6's framing that this is GES's internal control over its own staff.
  • The jurisdiction ceiling holds even here. The school must be within Wassa Amenfi West, and that choice scopes everything below it. A district director cannot reach into another district's records through the compliance path — the gate does not override the scope rule, it operates inside it.
  • Students and teachers are found differently — because GES holds different keys. GES maintains the teacher establishment register, so a teacher is looked up directly by staff ID. GES holds no student identifier — student IDs are assigned by each school's own system — so a student cannot be looked up by ID at all. Instead the officer browses the school's roster, narrowed by form, programme and gender. Pretending a director arrives knowing a student ID would have been a false assumption; the form is honest about which key exists for which record type.
  • The student roster opens only after the justification is logged. A list of a school's students — even names and classes — is itself named data. So the roster is not a free pre-step before the gate; it is part of what the logged justification authorises, and the log records that a roster was browsed, not only the record finally chosen. The roster also opens filtered, never as a full 1,284-name dump — the officer narrows by form and programme first, so a single-record lookup exposes 132 names, not the whole school.

The record reveals only what the reason earns

  • The roster browse is step one, and it is already inside the gate. Because GES holds no student ID, the officer arrives at a filtered roster — Form 2 Science, 132 students — and picks the record the case concerns. This is not a free directory: access R-0094 was logged before the roster opened, so browsing it is already an audited action. The matching row is highlighted because the claim flag is itself aggregate metadata that helps the officer land on the right student without opening several records.
  • Field-level scoping is the heart of the record itself. The reason chosen at the gate does not just unlock "the record" — it unlocks a subset of fields. Free SHS claim verification releases enrolment, placement and fee status; it withholds health and guardian-contact data. A reason and the data it justifies are bound together.
  • Withheld fields are shown as withheld, not hidden. The two redacted fields appear in the grid, greyed, labelled "not released for this reason." The officer sees that more exists and sees they did not get it — transparency about the boundary itself, and a pointer that health data needs a safeguarding reason.
  • The access strip is always visible above the record. "Logged as R-0094 against D. Mensah-Bonsu… visible to GES regional, national & audit review." While the officer reads a named record, the surface never lets them forget the access is recorded — the accountability sits on the same screen as the data.
  • The record is read-only. Oversight reads a named record across the analytics boundary; it never edits one. The operational record stays the single source of truth — Oversight observes, it does not write back.
  • The access resolves to an outcome. The audit-confirm block closes the loop — the claim is cleared, and that resolution is logged alongside the access. A named-record access that produced a result is the legitimate case; the log captures both the access and what it was for.
  • This is the controlled exception that makes "aggregate by default" credible. Oversight can say every other surface is aggregate precisely because the one place a name appears is gated, scoped, logged and reviewed. Section 3 turns to the log itself.

The officer sees their own log — because that is the point

  • An officer's own access history is shown back to them. Not to let them police it, but because visible accountability changes behaviour: a director who can see their nine accesses listed, reasoned and outcome-tracked, treats the tenth with care. The log is not hidden in an admin tier — it is on the officer's own screen.
  • Every access carries its outcome. Claim cleared, transfer confirmed, referred to welfare, escalated to region — the log records not just that a record was opened but what the compliance work concluded. An access with no outcome would itself be a flag; the log makes the legitimate cases self-evidently legitimate.
  • The log is append-only. The provenance row states it: entries cannot be edited or deleted, by anyone. This is the same append-only audit principle the operational app uses — a compliance log that could be altered would be worthless. Once R-0094 is written, it stays.
  • Safeguarding entries are case-restricted even in the officer's own history. R-0085 shows the access happened and its reason, but the record identity is withheld — child-protection cases are restricted further than ordinary compliance work, even from the accessing officer's review screen. The boundary has its own internal gradient.
  • This history is a slice of the district-wide log. The officer sees their own accesses; the full district and regional log lives in the Oversight access & audit surface (Batch 3, still to come) that audit staff review. Same append-only entries, two windows onto them.
  • This surface completes the compliance record view. The gate (section 1), the field-scoped record (section 2), and the officer's own access history (section 3) — the one controlled exception to aggregate-only, built so that using it is always deliberate, always scoped, and always recorded.
