Omnischools Oversight · the national tier

The whole system, one screen.

The national dashboard is the top of the hierarchy — the Ministry of Education's view of all sixteen regions. It is the same pattern as the district and regional dashboards, lifted one final tier: regions are the unit, every region drills down into its districts and from there into schools. But the national tier carries two things the lower tiers cannot. It has no jurisdiction ceiling — national staff see every region, every district, every school. And it is the only tier where policy-level indicators belong: Free SHS participation, the national pupil-teacher ratio against GES target, gender parity, transition rates — the numbers that answer to Parliament rather than to a school. This surface completes Oversight Batch 2.

The top of the hierarchy

  • The same pattern, one final tier up. Identical KPI strip, period banner and provenance row as the district and regional dashboards — the unit is now the region, the rollup is of 16. National staff who have seen any lower dashboard read this immediately. Consistency across all three tiers was the design goal from the district dashboard onward.
  • The national tier has no jurisdiction ceiling. The provenance row says it plainly: all regions visible. Where a district director could not see sibling districts' internals and a regional director could not see other regions, national staff see everything — every region, district and school. The hierarchy's top is the one tier without a sideways limit.
  • Coverage at 75.6% is the honest national headline. A quarter of Ghana's schools are not yet on Omnischools — and the dashboard says so as a KPI, not a footnote. The same coverage discipline as every Oversight surface, at the largest scale: national figures are real but partial, and the surface never pretends otherwise.
  • The crest reads "Ministry of Education." The lower tiers carried "Ghana Education Service"; at national Oversight the accountable body is the Ministry. The demo national user E. Mahama sits at the top of the Decision 5 provisioning chain — the GES Oversight super-admin tier.
  • Every national figure is a 16-region rollup. The period banner states it. The national PTR of 26.9:1 is a region-weighted mean, not a separate national measurement — national staff presenting to Parliament need to know the figure's construction.

Sixteen regions, and the gap between them

  • Every row drills the full hierarchy. National staff tapping Western Region land on the regional dashboard A. Owusu uses, then into Wassa Amenfi West's district dashboard, then a school detail view. Three tiers of drill-down from one national row — the whole product is one connected hierarchy.
  • The Western Region row ties the demo together. 431 of 508 schools, 198,400 pupils, 66% WASSCE — the exact figures the regional dashboard opened with. The demo thread runs unbroken: school → district → region → nation, every tier consistent with the last.
  • The spread panel is the national tier's signature read. Lower tiers care about their own average; the national tier's real concern is disparity — the 18-point WASSCE gap, the 33-point coverage gap between best and worst region. The spread bars show range and mean together, because national policy exists to close gaps, not to admire averages.
  • The equity story is stated, not just plotted. The northern regions sit at the low end of performance, coverage and PTR together — a pattern the spread makes visible and the note names outright. This is the read a national director takes into a policy discussion.
  • Coverage colour and the read-with-coverage discipline carry through. Northern Region's 54% WASSCE sits beside 58% coverage — the lowest-covered region's performance figure is the least settled, exactly as the regional and district tiers flagged. The honesty rule holds at national scale.

The tier where policy lives

  • Policy indicators belong only at the national tier. Free SHS participation, national PTR against target, gender parity, JHS-to-SHS transition — these answer to Parliament and the national education plan, not to a headteacher or a district director. The lower dashboards correctly never showed them; the national dashboard is where they finally appear.
  • Every indicator is shown against its target. A policy number without its target is just a number — the gauge marker shows where the figure should be, so transition at 78% against a 95% target reads instantly as the widest gap. National staff brief Parliament on distance-to-target, not on raw values.
  • The coverage caveat governs even the policy tier. Every indicator carries the reminder that it is an estimate from 75.6% of schools. The Free SHS card makes it explicit — the 6% shortfall is mostly unmeasured schools, not confirmed non-participation. Oversight's central honesty rule holds all the way to the top.
  • The anomalies are structural, not incidental. At national scale the flags are not single schools — they are the northern belt trailing on every measure, a 17-point transition gap, a 2,240-school coverage hole. The surface names that these overlap: the blind spot and the equity problem are the same five regions. That convergence is the national policy story.
  • This completes Oversight Batch 2. The enrolment-vs-population analysis, the academic performance analysis, the comparison workspace, the regional dashboard and now the national dashboard — five surfaces. Together with Batch 1's three, Oversight now runs unbroken from a newly provisioned district director's first sign-in to the Ministry's national policy view, every tier consistent and every figure honest about coverage.
