What this surface answers

  • Is VLC running? Top strip · curriculum coverage card · matrix bars. One glance tells you whether the programme is held together this semester.
  • Where is the pastoral attention? The flags grid · the active cases · the pattern across them. The Dean and Headmaster see concentration, not just incidents.
  • Which Form Master needs a conversation this week? The flagged-row class. Form 3 GA shows up red on multiple cells — that's where the Friday Dean meeting starts.
  • Are the Peer Guides being trained? 92% headline · gap visibility on the matrix · vacancy queue · escalation if attendance drops below 80%.
  • Is the calendar holding? Curriculum position bar per class · the variance band (±1 session is fine, ±2 is a conversation, ±3 is a redesign).

What this surface deliberately doesn't show

  • No journal content. Reflections live on the student journal surface, FM-only.
  • No student rankings. No leaderboards · no "best class for VLC." VLC is not a competition.
  • No staff comparisons. Form Masters aren't ranked by VLC outcomes. Sessions held is a hygiene metric, not a performance one.
02
Why a dashboard, and why this one
Editorial · the dashboard as rhythm-check, not scoreboard
The view from the head office

A pastoral programme needs management, not measurement.

The instinct, when designing a school-wide dashboard for a values programme, is to start grading: best class, worst class, leaderboard, rankings, percentile, color-coded by performance. That instinct is wrong. VLC is not a competition. A class with two pastoral flags is not failing; it's potentially doing the work better than a class with zero flags, because something is surfacing. A class that's one session behind isn't underperforming; it might have a Form Master who paused for a difficult conversation, which is exactly what the system is for.

What the Headmaster and Dean actually need is a rhythm-check: is the programme running, is the calendar holding, are the flags being attended to, are the Peer Guides being trained, is the curriculum being delivered. These are operational questions, not performance ones. The dashboard answers them in one screen, with enough drill-down that the Friday morning admin meeting can be five minutes on VLC, not thirty.

The decisions this surface drives are small but high-frequency: which Form Master to talk to this week, which class needs a Dean visit, which pastoral flag needs to escalate, which PG training is approaching, which class is running ahead and can be slowed. The dashboard exists to surface those decisions early — before they become crises, before they become end-of-term audits. The aggregate numbers up top are honest about the year. The matrix in the middle is honest about the classes. The flags grid is honest about the concentration of pastoral work. Nothing is hidden by averaging.

One last design choice worth naming: this dashboard does not surface journal content. Not to the Headmaster, not even to the Dean in summary form. The journal is FM + Dean for case oversight only, and even there the Dean reads cases — not aggregates. The dashboard surfaces flags (which are by their nature pastoral metadata, not student content), attendance, submission rates, and curriculum position. Aggregating reflection content into school-wide statistics would distort what reflections are. The student writes for two readers. The dashboard does not become a third.

The wrong dashboard would do these things

  • Rank classes by "values engagement." Turns the programme into a competition Form Masters game.
  • Surface anonymised reflection themes school-wide. Breaks the FM-student trust that makes reflections honest.
  • Score Form Masters by attendance %. Turns the FM into an attendance-officer instead of a values facilitator.
  • Display flags publicly inside the dashboard rollup. Privacy violation · pastoral confidentiality.
  • Trend pastoral flag counts as a "school health metric." Schools with more flags may be safer, not worse.
