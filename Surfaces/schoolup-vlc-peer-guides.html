What this surface manages

  • Per-class PG slots. Every F2/F3 class has 2 slots — boys' rep and girls' rep. Form 1 classes show "no PGs by policy" cards.
  • Vacancy protocol. When a PG steps aside, the class card flips to vacancy state. Class members nominate up to 4 candidates within one week, FM holds a class vote, winner finishes the term.
  • Training attendance is tracked per session. Drives the 92% headline metric. Below 80% triggers a Dean conversation with the missing PG · pattern of absence may signal stepping aside.
  • Tenure rotation is calendared. 2026/27 Semester 1 selection opens 15 July · current Form 3 PGs roll off (WASSCE prep), some Form 2 PGs roll off (1-term tenure ended), new candidates step in.
  • Leadership stats feed the year-end character paragraph. Each PG's service is recognised on the school-leaver reference letter — concrete contribution, not just a line.

Why not a longer tenure?

  • One term feels too short, but it's deliberate. Three reasons: (1) leadership opportunity spreads wider across the cohort, (2) burnout doesn't accumulate, (3) class gets multiple PG personalities across the year — different students surface different conversations.
  • Re-selection is allowed. A PG who served Semester 1 of this year can stand again for next year's Semester 1. Several do · about 20-30%. Most don't · they've had their turn.

Peer Guide vs Prefect · two different student leadership roles
Editorial · why both can coexist
The distinction that matters

A Prefect holds authority. A Peer Guide holds space.

Ghanaian SHS has a long-running Prefect system — Senior Prefect, House Prefects, Class Captains, Dining Hall Prefects, Library Prefects, Sports Prefects. They are the school's student-leadership scaffold and they work. They enforce rules, represent the school at functions, liaise between students and staff, and in some schools sit on disciplinary committees. The role is authority-bearing by design. A Class Captain who can't enforce a rule is no Class Captain.

Peer Guides are something else. The role exists because a Form Master can lead a value conversation, and a Prefect can keep the classroom in order, but neither one can be the peer asking the question. When the value of the week is Compassion and the Form Master asks "who in your life is struggling and how could you help" — the answers a student gives an adult will be different from the answers that same student gives a friend in a small group. The Peer Guide is the friend version of the conversation. Same student, same value, different access to the truth.

Selection reflects the distinction. Prefects are appointed — by the Headmaster, often based on academic standing, conduct record, and visible leadership over time. Peer Guides are elected by their classmates, with Form Master approval as a check. The difference matters: a Prefect's authority comes from the institution, a Peer Guide's standing comes from peers. Different sources, different legitimacy, different jobs.

A student can be both. Several are — the same person who's House Prefect can also be Peer Guide in their classroom. They wear different hats in different rooms. The system does not collapse them into one role because the work is different: one keeps order, the other holds space. Both are needed.

The practical implication

  • Don't appoint Peer Guides from above. The class election step is the source of legitimacy. Skipping it makes a Peer Guide functionally a Prefect.
  • Don't make PG service compete with Prefect duty. Different timeslots, different rooms, different functions. A student can wear both hats without conflict.
  • Don't recognise PGs in the same channel as Prefects. Prefects line up at assembly, get badges. PGs get a sentence on the character paragraph. Different recognition, different intent.
