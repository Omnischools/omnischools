Children who need more

Captures special needs enrolment that the GES annual census currently leaves as a hatched manual section. Once a school records this here, the census section auto-fills with counts by category and gender. Designed cautiously: privacy-first, parent-consented, with restrained vocabulary that avoids labelling-as-diagnosis when no formal diagnosis exists. The system tracks operational support needs, not medical conditions.

Tracks students who need additional support · feeds the GES annual census special needs section · admin-only access · parents must consent before recording

Treated as sensitive personal data
Records here are visible only to school administrators, not teachers (unless an administrator explicitly grants access for accommodation planning). Parents must provide written consent before a record is created. Categories describe support needs, not medical diagnoses — formal diagnoses are recorded only when supplied by a qualified clinician. Schools that prefer not to record at student level can still complete the GES census section by hand; this module is opt-in.

Why this layout

  • Privacy banner is the first thing on the page. "Treated as sensitive personal data" with explicit policy: admin-only by default, parents must consent, categories describe support needs not diagnoses, opt-in module. Sets the boundary before the data appears.
  • "Schools that prefer not to record at student level can still complete the GES census section by hand" — the module is opt-in. Some schools will reasonably decide the privacy cost outweighs the auto-fill benefit. Honest about that choice.
  • Hero shows 8 of 312 (2.6%) with formal/observed split: "3 with formal diagnosis, 5 with informal/observed need". Distinguishes recorded medical fact from teacher observation; both belong in the support register but only one is a "diagnosis." Important for honest GES reporting.
  • Census preview shows all 12 hatched cells from the annual census now auto-filled with counts. Same 6×2 grid as appears in the GES census file. Direct visual evidence that adopting this module flips a manual census section to auto.
  • "Privacy preserved (no names exported)" meta line — the GES export is aggregate counts only. Student-level data never leaves the school's instance. Census needs counts, not names; the system only exports what's needed.
  • 5 categories: Visual / Hearing / Physical / Intellectual / Speech with distinct colour vocabulary (visual gold, hearing green, physical terra, intellectual purple, speech warn). Matches the 5 GES census categories — no expansion to clinical taxonomy; the census defines the buckets.
  • 3-tier severity: Mild / Moderate / Severe in green/warn/terra. Operationally meaningful for accommodation planning without requiring clinical assessment. A teacher can describe severity without diagnosing.
  • Support & accommodations column uses small rounded tags: "Front-row seating / Larger print / Reading buddy / Right side of class / Ground-floor classroom / Extended writing time / Oral assessment option / Modified curriculum / Smaller-group instruction / Extra exam time / No forced public reading / Patience during oral answers". Concrete operational accommodations teachers can act on; not abstract.
  • Diagnosis column distinguishes formal vs observed: "Diagnosed by ophthalmologist · Dr. Asare Eye Clinic · 2022" vs "Observed by class teacher · Diagnosis pending". Honest about the source of the determination; parents and reviewers see what's clinical and what's school-observed.
  • Consent state visible on every row: "consent on file" appears on every record because the system enforces consent before record creation. If consent isn't on file, the record doesn't exist. Pending-consent families show up as a count in the hero ("1 family") with their child counted in census aggregates only without student-level detail.
  • Realistic Ghanaian clinical sources: Dr. Asare Eye Clinic, UGMC (University of Ghana Medical Centre), Korle Bu ENT, Accra Psychology Centre. Real institutions, not fictional; supports operational realism for schools that recognise their own referral network.
  • "Walks with crutches after polio" / "Stammer" / "Limited fine motor control" — plain-English descriptions of conditions. The system uses everyday language rather than clinical terminology, matching how Ghanaian teachers actually describe student needs.
  • What this module deliberately does NOT do: No detailed clinical history, no medication tracking, no behavioural incident logs, no IEP (Individualised Education Programme) authoring. Omnischools is not an SEN platform; it captures the GES census-level facts plus practical accommodation context. Schools needing deeper SEN tracking would use a specialised system.
