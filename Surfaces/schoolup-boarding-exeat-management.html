Exeat management · the term's three legal exits

The exeat is the formal contract by which a boarder leaves campus during the semester. Three scheduled exeats per semester (the school calendar dictates), plus special exeats parents can request for funerals, illness in the family, or church engagements. Fee-owing students must take the scheduled exeat home and bring the money back — built into the workflow. The exeat card is a printable artefact, the digital record is the contract. The return-by 4 PM rule is enforced — past that, a late-return SMS chain fires to the parent. Today, May 14, the next scheduled exeat is in 17 days (Sun 31 May).

The three types of exeat

  • Scheduled · on the school calendar · 3 per semester per student · auto-approved if no flags · parent-confirmed via SMS · most boarders take 2-3 a term
  • Special · parent-initiated · for weddings, funerals, medical, family illness · Senior HM signs (HM alone cannot approve) · scrutinised against frequency
  • Fee-collection · auto-triggered for fee-owing students at scheduled exeats · trip home is for fees · marked distinctly in records · invoice settled before return-by

Why fee-owing matters

  • The school cannot detain a student for unpaid fees. GES rule.
  • But it can require fee-collection via exeat. Built into the workflow. The "FEE" type pre-fills the reason on the exeat card.
  • The parent SMS makes it explicit · "Your ward will be home from [date] to [date]. Please clear outstanding fees of GHS 340.00 before return."

Late return is a slow alarm

  • +5 min · auto-SMS to parent only · friendly
  • +30 min · Senior HM cc'd · stronger
  • +1 hour · formal NOTE on student record · phone call from HM
  • Beyond → discipline ladder (surface 07)

Why exeats deserve their own surface
Editorial · the gate-crossing contract
The exeat as the boarding system's most legally consequential event

When a boarder crosses the school gate, the school owes accountability to itself, the parent, and the law.

Until the moment a boarder steps out of the gate on Saturday morning of exeat weekend, the school is responsible for them — physically, legally, and pastorally. The instant they step through that gate, the school is no longer responsible — until the moment they step back through. The exeat card is the legal record of this transfer of responsibility. It says: at this time, with this approval, in this dress, to this destination, returning by this time. That sentence is what makes the transfer auditable.

This is why the workflow has five distinct stages, each timestamped, each requiring a different actor. The parent requests. The Housemaster reviews, checking the student's record (last 3 exeats, fee status, discipline standing). The Senior HM signs if it's a special exeat. The gate logs departure with HM verification. The gate logs return with HM verification. Five stages, five signatures, five timestamps. The PDF card the student carries is a snapshot of this record at moment of departure. If anything goes wrong — the student is in an accident, the parent disputes the route, the dean asks who approved this — the chain is one query deep.

The fee-collection exeat type is small but consequential. Under Free SHS, schools cannot withhold education from fee-owing students. But operationally, fees still must be collected — the school still has to pay for what Free SHS doesn't cover. The exeat workflow handles this elegantly: a student with unpaid fees at the time of a scheduled exeat is automatically routed to take the trip home (the school cannot detain them), with the explicit purpose of fee collection written on the card and into the parent SMS. By the time they return, fees should be cleared. If not, the discipline ladder is informed. This is the kind of cross-module integration that day-school management systems don't need to model — billing and boarding meet at the exeat workflow.

One more design choice worth defending: auto-approval for scheduled exeats with clean records. A Form 3 student with no infractions, fees clear, three previous on-time returns this year — that student does not need a Housemaster to manually review their request for Sunday's exeat. The system can approve it, the parent gets the SMS, the card is generated. The Housemaster's eye goes only to the special exeats (5 of 24), the fee-flag exeats (3 of 24), and the discipline-flag exeats (0 of 24). The system reserves the Housemaster's attention for the cases that need it.

The late-return chain is the part of this surface most likely to be invoked, and it is deliberately gentle at first. A five-minute lateness gets a single SMS to the parent — not the headmaster, not the dean, not yet. Most lateness is traffic. Half an hour late gets the Senior HM involved. An hour late is a formal NOTE on the student's record, which becomes the first rung of the discipline ladder if it recurs. The escalation is designed to give the parent the chance to explain before it becomes a school-side problem. Most lateness is resolved at stage 1; some at stage 2; rarely does it reach stage 3. The system is calibrated for what actually happens, not what theoretically could.

What the exeat card is and isn't

  • Is · a legal artefact recording transfer of student responsibility from school to parent for a defined window
  • Is · a printable PDF the student carries with them home
  • Is · a digital record auditable by any of HM, Senior HM, dean, or headmaster
  • Isn't · a permission slip · the school does not authorise the trip, the parent does
  • Isn't · transferable · one card per exeat per student · refusable at gate if signature missing
