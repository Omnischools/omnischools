Omnischools Oversight · the first sign-in

A new director's first day.

An Oversight user is never self-registered. When GES appoints a new district director, a designated GES Oversight administrator provisions their account — bound to one named jurisdiction, the role decided before the person ever signs in. This surface is what the new director sees on first sign-in: they verify their GES-issued credentials, confirm the jurisdiction the account was created against, read a short briefing on what Oversight access does and does not permit, acknowledge the accountability terms, and arrive at their dashboard. The demo new director is D. Mensah-Bonsu, provisioned as District Director of Wassa Amenfi West.

Sign-in that reflects the provisioning model

  • There is no "create account" anywhere on this surface. Oversight users are a separate, GES-provisioned pool (BUILD_STACK Decision 5). The new director's account already exists before they arrive; first sign-in is verification, not registration. The absence of a sign-up link is the design carrying the decision.
  • The phone number is pre-filled and locked. The account was bound to the number on the director's GES appointment record. They cannot type a different one — if it is wrong, only the GES administrator can fix it. This is what "authenticate against GES-held identity" looks like without a federated SSO existing.
  • Same phone-OTP mechanism as the whole of Omnischools. No new auth paradigm for Oversight — the operational app's phone-OTP flow, reused. Decision 5 said federation is abstracted-for-later, not built now; this surface uses what works in Ghana today.
  • The "provisioned by" panel names names. Who created the account, when, and which jurisdiction it was bound to. The new director sees their account's provenance on day one — and it states plainly that role and jurisdiction are GES-set and not user-changeable.
  • Deep-navy sign-in frame, distinct from the operational app. A GES officer signing into Oversight should feel they are entering the government oversight tool, not a school's app. The gradient frame and GES crest set that register before they are even signed in.

Jurisdiction confirmation, before any data

  • The onboarding uses the canonical vertical step nav. Per the design system convention locked in BUILD_STACK, multi-step guided flows use the wizard pattern — vertical steps, checkmarks for done, gold for active. Oversight onboarding is a guided flow, so it inherits that pattern rather than inventing one.
  • Jurisdiction is confirmed, never chosen. The card is marked "GES-set · locked." The director's only inputs are "yes, correct" or "this is not my jurisdiction" — the second is an escape hatch to the administrator, not a picker. A director must never be able to widen their own scope.
  • The jurisdiction card front-loads the real numbers. 40 schools, 34 on Omnischools, and the genuine 2021 GSS district population of 129,882. The director learns the shape of their patch — and its coverage gap — before they ever reach the dashboard.
  • The access scope is stated in plain words at confirmation time. "34 schools only, no other districts, compare within your region." The director acknowledges the boundary as part of onboarding, so the dashboard's scope-chip later is a reminder, not a surprise.
  • The GSS population figure threads from here to the enrolment-vs-population surface. The same 129,882 a director sees here is the denominator for the signature comparative analysis in Batch 2 — onboarding plants the number the analysis will pay off.

Step 3 of 5 · access briefing
What Oversight access means.
Four things to understand before you begin. Oversight is a powerful lens on the schools you serve — and a bounded, accountable one.
1.
You see schools that are on Omnischools
Oversight can only show data for schools that use Omnischools operationally — that is what generates the figures. 34 of the 40 schools in your district's EMIS register are on Omnischools and report today. The other 6 are not yet on the platform and are invisible to Oversight; getting them onboarded is part of your work. (This is a platform-coverage gap, not a question of a school agreeing to GES oversight — GES governs all 40 regardless.)
2.
Almost everything you see is aggregate
Dashboards, trends, comparisons — counts and rates, no individual identified. You can open these freely; they are not logged as record access.
3.
Named records need a stated reason
To open an individual student's or teacher's record — for Free SHS claim verification, anomaly investigation, or statutory audit — you must state a reason. The access is logged and reviewable by GES leadership. Use this sparingly.
4.
Your activity is reviewable within GES
Your named-record accesses are logged, and your regional director, the national tier, and GES internal audit can review them. Accountability runs upward within GES — the agency holds its own officers responsible for how access is used. This is normal for a governing body handling records of those it regulates.
✓
I understand that my Oversight access is scoped to Wassa Amenfi West, that named-record access requires a logged justification, and that my activity is reviewable by GES regional, national, and internal-audit staff.

You're set up, Director.

Your account is active and bound to Wassa Amenfi West. Your dashboard is ready — 34 schools, 14,820 pupils, the 2025/26 academic year. Welcome to Omnischools Oversight.

The briefing is the trust model, taught on day one

  • The four briefing points are the four pillars of the access model. Platform coverage, aggregate-by-default, justification-gated named records, GES-internal accountability. A director who has read these once will read the dashboard's scope-chip, the detail view's boundary block, and the compliance surface correctly — onboarding teaches the vocabulary the rest of the product assumes.
  • Point 1 frames the coverage gap as the director's job — and is careful about why. "6 schools not on Omnischools — onboarding them is part of your work." The gap is a platform-adoption gap, not a school declining GES oversight; the briefing says so explicitly, because a director must not mistake EMIS-register-minus-Omnischools for schools that have opted out of GES. The gap is handed over as a task, the same framing the dashboard's anomaly panel uses.
  • Point 4 establishes that GES audits its own officers. A director's access is reviewable by their regional director, national, and GES internal audit — accountability runs upward inside the agency. An earlier draft had schools reviewing GES activity; that wrongly implied a peer relationship between a regulator and the institutions it governs, and it was corrected. The legitimacy of an oversight tool comes from the governing body controlling how its own staff use access, not from the governed schools watching back.
  • The acknowledgement is specific, not a blanket "I agree." It names the three things that matter — jurisdiction scope, justification-gating, and GES-internal reviewability. The director acknowledges the actual model, so the consent is meaningful rather than a checkbox.
  • Arrival hands off to the dashboard with its real numbers. "34 schools, 14,820 pupils, 2025/26" — the same figures the district dashboard (surface 1) opens with. The three Batch 1 surfaces connect: onboarding ends where the dashboard begins, and the dashboard lists schools that drill into the detail view.
  • This completes Oversight Batch 1. Onboarding (provisioning & the access model), the district dashboard (the anchor), and the school detail view (drill-down & the boundary) are a coherent first slice — a new director can be provisioned, sign in, understand their remit, survey their district, and inspect any school, all within the model BUILD_STACK scoped.
