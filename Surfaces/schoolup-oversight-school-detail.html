Omnischools Oversight · the drill-down target

One school, in full.

The school detail view is where a district director lands when they tap a school in the dashboard list — and where a regional or national user lands when they drill down through their own tier. It holds one school's complete Oversight profile: the five themes at school depth, the year-over-year trends a single dashboard figure can't show, and a "compare against" picker that opens this school beside its peers. It is also where the access boundary becomes visible — the line between the aggregate data any jurisdiction user can see, and the named-record compliance path that requires a stated, logged justification reviewable within GES. The demo school is Asankrangwa SHS, the operational demo's anchor institution, seen here through the GES lens.

The detail view, themes at school depth

  • The hero strip carries the operational link. Asankrangwa SHS is a live Omnischools school, so the detail view offers "School on operational app" — a director can cross from the GES lens to the school's own tool. The green "live operational data" tag distinguishes a Omnischools school from one whose figures come from older GES returns.
  • Same four-KPI strip as the dashboard, re-scoped to one school. A director who has internalised the district numbers reads the school's instantly — and every school KPI is contextualised against the district ("above district avg," "best of the 3 district SHS"). A number alone is not oversight; a number against its peers is.
  • Theme tabs keep the profile dense but navigable. Five themes — enrolment, performance, attendance, staffing, fees — as tabs rather than five stacked panels. The detail view goes deep; tabs stop it sprawling.
  • Trends are the detail view's reason to exist. The dashboard shows this year; the detail view shows the five-year arc. "Enrolment up 34%, girls' share climbing to parity" is a story only the trend reveals — and exactly what a director carries into regional reporting.
  • Real anchor data. Asankrangwa SHS, ASANCO, founded 1960, Headmaster V. Yanney — verified detail, consistent with the operational demo. The institution is the same across both product lines.

Performance depth, and comparison made reachable

  • The compare-against picker sits at the top of the performance tab. Decision 2 made comparison first-class; the school detail view is where a director most wants it — looking at one school and asking "compared to what?" The picker offers peer schools, district average, regional average. It states the scope ceiling: within your jurisdiction and one tier up, never sideways into another region.
  • Subject-level grade bands are the depth the dashboard can't carry. The district dashboard showed one BECE and one WASSCE bar. Here it breaks to core subjects — and Core Maths at 57% against 69–70% elsewhere is the kind of specific, actionable weakness Oversight exists to surface.
  • Every figure is contextualised against the peer set. "1 of 3 district SHS," "Asankrangwa leads," trend shown beside the district average. A school's number is only meaningful relative to its peers — the detail view never shows a bare figure.
  • The Core Maths note points outward. It observes that the weakness is district-wide and names where to see it school-by-school — the comparison workspace. The detail view answers "what about this school" and routes onward to "is this everywhere."
  • WASSCE grade bands use the real A1–F9 structure. Distinction / Credit / Pass / Fail mapped to the actual WAEC grade groupings — a Ghanaian director reads this in their own terms, not an invented scale.

Aggregate by default, gated for casework.

GES is the governing body of this school and has standing authority to view its data. Almost all of that viewing is aggregate. Opening an individual student's record is a separate, deliberate, logged action — a control GES holds over its own officers.
!
Named-record access is justification-gated
The profile, the themes, the trends, the comparisons — all of it is aggregate. No student is identifiable. For specific compliance work — Free SHS claim verification, a withdrawal-anomaly investigation, a statutory audit, safeguarding casework — GES sometimes needs an individual student's record. GES has the authority to do this; it is the schools' governing body. But it is never a casual click. Every named-record access requires a stated reason and is written to an audit log — so that GES leadership can hold its own officers accountable for how access is used. The gate is GES's internal control, not a permission a school grants.
1.
State a reason. Pick from the controlled list — claim verification, anomaly investigation, statutory audit, safeguarding — or describe the compliance need.
2.
Access is logged. Your name, jurisdiction, the record opened, the reason, and the timestamp are written to the Oversight access & audit log.
3.
GES reviews it. Your regional director, the national tier, and GES internal audit can review the log. Accountability runs upward within GES.

The access boundary is an internal-to-GES control

  • This section makes the aggregate-vs-named line visible. A director scrolling the detail view reaches an unmistakable point where aggregate ends. The warn-toned boundary block is that point — it does not hide that named-record access exists, it frames it as deliberate, gated, and reviewable.
  • GES has the authority; the gate governs how that authority is used. GES is the schools' governing body and has standing power to view and request their data — named-record access is not something a school authorises. What the justification gate does is make GES officers' use of that power purpose-bound and reviewable. The friction is internal discipline, not a permission negotiation with the school.
  • Accountability runs upward within GES, not down to schools. Step 3 of the gate is "GES reviews it" — a district director's named-record pulls are visible to their regional director, the national tier, and GES internal audit. This is the correct shape for a regulator: a government body audits its own staff's access to records, the same way any agency does, without implying the governed institution monitors the regulator. An earlier draft had the school watching GES's accesses; that wrongly framed a governing-body relationship as a relationship between peers, and it has been removed.
  • The data-categories card reframes "sharing" as statutory reporting. A school's data reaches GES because GES governs it — not by opt-in consent. The card's real job is the aggregate-vs-named distinction: four aggregate categories open to any jurisdiction user, two named categories gated behind the log.
  • The access-history panel is deliberately sparse. Three named-record accesses in twelve months — claim verification and one anomaly investigation. "Sparingly" from Decision 6 shown as a checkable number. Aggregate views are unrestricted and uncounted; only the gated path is logged, and that same log feeds the GES-internal audit surface.
  • This closes Batch 1's design job on the boundary. The district dashboard and this detail view together establish that Oversight is powerful and bounded — bounded by GES's own internal controls. Batch 3's compliance-record view and access & audit log build directly on the model this section makes concrete.
