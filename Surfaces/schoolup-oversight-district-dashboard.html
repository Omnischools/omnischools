Omnischools Oversight · the GES district view

What a district director sees on a Tuesday.

Omnischools Oversight is the third product line — observational dashboards for GES district directors, regional directors, and MoE national staff, reading from a separate analytics database that is never the operational one. This is the anchor surface: the district director dashboard. It answers the questions a director of education actually carries into a working week — how many children are enrolled and is that keeping pace with the district's population, how are schools performing in BECE and WASSCE, where are the teacher gaps, what fees are being charged. Every figure rolls up from schools that have signed the GES data-sharing agreement; every figure can be compared school-against-school. The demo jurisdiction is Wassa Amenfi West Municipal District, Western Region — the real district that contains Asankrangwa SHS from the operational demo.

What the anchor surface establishes

  • Oversight gets a visibly different chrome. Deeper navy sidebar (#13203A vs the operational #1A2B47), a GES crest mark instead of a school logo, and a jurisdiction scope-chip where the operational app shows a school name. Same design system, same fonts and tokens — but a director never confuses "am I in the GES tool or a school's tool."
  • The scope-chip is the access model made visible. "Your jurisdiction · Wassa Amenfi West" is always on screen. A district director is structurally incapable of seeing another district; the chip reminds them what they're scoped to without it reading as a restriction.
  • Coverage is stated honestly up front. 34 of 40 schools — not every school in the district is on Omnischools yet, and the dashboard says so rather than implying the district is fully observed. Directors must know the denominator.
  • The data-period banner kills the "is this live?" question. Oversight reads a nightly-synced analytics DB, never the operational database. The banner states the sync time and which examination cohorts the performance figures draw on — a director presenting these numbers upward needs to know their vintage.
  • The provenance row is a trust artefact. Source, coverage, mode (aggregate — no named records here), and scope ceiling. It pre-answers the questions an auditor or a sceptical headteacher would ask about what this dashboard is and isn't.
  • Demo director · D. Mensah-Bonsu, District Director of Wassa Amenfi West. A named GES officer, provisioned into the jurisdiction — consistent with the separate-user-pool decision. Not a headteacher with bolted-on permissions.

The four-theme panel grid

  • Enrolment-by-sex drills from level to class. "How many boys versus girls" was a named scoping question — and the Basic line now expands in place to KG, Primary, and JHS, each with its own sex split, because a district's gender pattern differs by stage. The per-class breakdown (KG1, KG2, Primary 1–6, JHS 1–3) lives one level deeper, in each school's detail view: the dashboard answers "which stage," the detail view answers "which class."
  • Performance shows the grade-band shape, and the headline figure is explicitly the qualification rate. A bare "74%" is ambiguous, so the panel labels it: qualification rate is the share graded credit or above (BECE 1–6 / WASSCE A1–C6), the threshold that governs progression — not a raw pass rate. The four-band distribution behind it (distinction / credit / pass / below) shows whether a district is full of credit-grade pupils or scraping the threshold.
  • Teacher staffing leads with the establishment gap. The number a GES director is accountable for is vacancies-against-approved-posts. Trained-versus-untrained ratio matters for quality reporting. PTR is shown here and as a KPI because it is the single most-cited planning metric. (Establishment and posting figures are GES/EMIS reference data — see the provenance note.)
  • Fees are shown as per-level averages; the component breakdown lives one level down. The dashboard answers "what does each level cost on average" — Kindergarten through SHS boarding. What a fee is *made of* — tuition, stationery, PTA levy, exam fee, line by line — is in the school detail view, where exact per-class amounts belong. The dashboard stays a scannable overview; the drill into composition is a school-specific question. The locked GHS 340.00 currency format carries through.
  • The themes draw on feeds of differing reliability — and the surface is honest about it. Enrolment, attendance, and fees come live from schools' own operational Omnischools use. Examination results (WAEC) and teacher establishment (GES/EMIS) are reference extracts loaded into the analytics DB — there is no public school-level results dataset, so these arrive via official GES–WAEC and GES–EMIS arrangements. The data-period banner and provenance row state which figures are live and which are loaded, because a director presenting them upward must know.
  • Every panel is aggregate-only. No pupil names, no staff names, nothing identifiable. Named-record access is a separate, justification-gated path used only for compliance casework, and it is not reachable from these panels.

The list is the junction box of the whole product

  • Every row is a drill-down into the school detail view — surface 2 of this batch. The dashboard answers "how is my district," the list answers "which schools," and the detail view answers "what is going on at this one." A director's natural path is dashboard → spot an outlier → list → detail.
  • Asankrangwa SHS carries the anchor badge. Gold tile where others are navy — it is the school that connects Oversight to the operational demo. A director drilling into it lands on the same institution that has heads, teachers, and the bulk-admission flow in the Basic/Senior app.
  • The Compare chip is the comparison-mode entry point. Decision 2 made comparison a first-class capability; here it is concrete — select rows, hit Compare, land in the comparison workspace. The chip carries a live count ("Compare selected · 2") so the director sees their selection building.
  • PTR is colour-coded against GES policy. Green within the 25:1 target, amber 25–30, red above 30. Samreboi D/A JHS at 32.6:1 reads red — exactly the kind of staffing pressure a director needs surfaced without hunting for it.
  • Performance pills compress the grade-band detail into a sortable signal. The full band breakdown lives in the academic-performance surface and the school detail view; the list needs a single comparable token. Green / amber / red thresholds let a director sort the district by attainment in one tap.
  • Public and private schools sit in one list. GES oversight covers both; ownership is a meta-line under the school name, not a separate view. Nkwanta Methodist (private) reporting 88% BECE next to public schools is the kind of contrast the district director is meant to see.

From overview to action

  • The anomaly panel is a triage shortlist, not the whole queue. The dashboard shows the few signals worth a director's Tuesday; the full anomaly queue (a Batch 3 surface) holds the rest. Each flag links straight to the school it concerns — see a problem, reach the school in one tap.
  • Flags connect causally where the data supports it. Samreboi's PTR flag and its BECE-drop flag are shown adjacent and the second references the first. Oversight should help a director see that an 18-point results fall and an unfilled-vacancy problem are likely the same story — not two unrelated numbers.
  • The coverage gap is computed, not guessed — and flagged like any other anomaly. "6 schools not on Omnischools" is the district's EMIS school register minus the schools reporting into Oversight. Because Oversight is a GES tool, it can hold GES's own authoritative school register as a reference table; the gap is an exact figure a director can act on, and the path runs to the data-sharing-agreement surface.
  • Anomaly detection reuses the operational engine. The rule-based heuristic approach locked for operational audit anomalies (BUILD_STACK) carries to Oversight — same explainable, rule-based model, different rule set (enrolment spikes, PTR thresholds, results drops, attendance collapse). No ML black box deciding what a director should worry about.
  • The comparison strip names real neighbouring districts. Wassa Amenfi East, Wassa Amenfi Central, Prestea-Huni Valley — genuine adjacent districts in the Western Region. And it states the scope ceiling plainly: a district director compares schools and sibling districts, never regions they cannot see. The capability and its limit are described in the same breath.
  • This section closes the anchor surface's job. Section 1 framed the district, 2 broke it into themes, 3 listed the schools, 4 points at what to do next — the anomaly queue, a specific school, or the comparison workspace. The dashboard is a starting point that always hands the director somewhere to go.
