If you forgot the password, generate a fresh archive

Archive passwords are not recoverable by Omnischools — by design, not by oversight. The cryptography that protects the archive is the same cryptography that prevents us from recovering the key. This page explains why that's the right answer, what to do in each of the three scenarios where it bites, and how to avoid being there in the first place.

01
Why we cannot recover your password
The cryptographic principle · plain English
The bank principle

If we could recover your password, so could anyone who compromised our servers.

The whole point of an encrypted archive is that only the password unlocks it. We never store the password — only a one-way hash that we use to verify when you re-enter it. The encryption keys are derived from your password at request time and discarded immediately after the archive is sealed.

This is the same model used by Ghana's commercial banks, GCB Bank's online portals, and your MTN MoMo PIN. Lose the PIN, lose the access — the bank cannot tell you what your PIN was. They can only let you set a new one.

Omnischools can let you generate a fresh archive with a new password. That is the entirety of the recovery answer.

How archive passwords are handled

What Omnischools stores
  • Hash of your password (one-way)
  • Date the archive was requested
  • Which admin requested it
  • Archive expiry timestamp
  • Audit log entry for the request
What Omnischools does not store
  • Your password itself, anywhere
  • The encryption key derived from it
  • Any "hint" or recovery question
  • A copy of the unencrypted archive
  • Logs containing the password text
Editorial principle

"We cannot recover your password" reads like an apology in most software. In Omnischools it's a commitment — the same property that prevents recovery is the property that makes the archive safe to share with auditors, banks, MoE inspectors, and anyone else who needs the data without needing access to your live Omnischools account. The inability is the feature.

Calls worth flagging

  • The bank analogy is doing real work here — Ghanaian admins understand MoMo PIN recovery better than they understand AES-256 key derivation. Anchor in lived experience.
  • The "What we store / What we don't" table is the trust-significant artefact. Some schools will ask specifically for this list before agreeing to use the export feature for sensitive data.
  • Hash storage is for verification only — when admin re-enters the password to download a still-valid archive, we hash the entry and compare. Never decrypt.
  • "No hint, no recovery question" is an explicit no — recovery questions are the weakest link in most systems. Omnischools does not adopt the practice.
  • This page may need to be linked from the data-export interface as an "About archive passwords" info link — the policy is part of the export trust story.
02
Three scenarios · three answers
When admin forgets the password · the cases
Easy
A

Forgot before downloading

Archive requested, still on Omnischools servers, not yet downloaded
Florence requested a full archive at 14:00. Omnischools emailed her the secure download link 28 minutes later. She opened the link, was prompted for the password — and realised she'd forgotten what she set. The encrypted ZIP is sitting on the server, ready to download, but she can't unlock it.
The answer
Generate a fresh archive. Old one expires immediately.
No data lost — the archive is just a snapshot. The fresh one is more current than the original would have been. Wait time: another 15-30 minutes.
Awkward
B

Forgot after downloading

Archive on Florence's laptop, password forgotten locally
Florence successfully downloaded the encrypted ZIP three days ago. The file is on her laptop, named christ-the-king-jhs-archive-2025-04-29.zip. She tried to open it today and realised the password is gone — not in her notes, not in her password manager.
The answer
Generate a fresh archive. The downloaded file is permanently locked.
No path to recovering the local file's password — it can be safely deleted. The fresh archive supersedes it. Audit log records both events.
Tricky
C

Shared with a third party, password lost

Archive sent to school's auditor, auditor lost the password
Florence generated an archive last term for the school's external auditor and sent it via email along with the password (in a separate email — best practice). The auditor's office has now lost the password and can't open the archive. The deadline is in two days.
The answer
Generate a fresh archive. Send fresh copy + new password.
Old downloaded copy at the auditor's office stays locked permanently — they can delete it. Florence shares the new archive + new password with the auditor, ideally via separate channels (one by email, one by phone or chat).
The shape of the answer

Every scenario has the same shape: generate fresh, supersede the old. Omnischools does not invent recovery mechanisms for forgotten passwords because the feature is the inability. What it does instead is make "generate fresh" as fast and frictionless as possible — Section 03 shows how.

Calls worth flagging

  • Three difficulty tiers — Easy / Awkward / Tricky — set expectations honestly. Scenario A is a non-event. Scenario C costs the auditor a half-day.
  • Scenario B's answer is the most pedagogically important: the local file is permanently locked. Florence shouldn't keep the file thinking she'll figure it out — the answer is to delete it and generate fresh.
  • "Separate channels" advice in Scenario C is real — many schools will email the archive and the password in the same thread. Separating channels is the single best habit to build.
  • For Scenario C, Omnischools could offer to email the auditor directly with a fresh secure link. Considered but deferred — auditor email isn't on file, and adding contact management for third parties is its own surface.
  • None of these scenarios involve Omnischools Support — they are all self-service. Support is only involved if the admin's account itself is compromised (different problem, see 2FA enrolment file).

Why the flow looks like the original flow

"Set a new password" deliberately mirrors the original archive request — same fields, same strength meter, same passphrase guidance. Florence learned this surface once. She doesn't need to learn a "recovery" surface that looks different. One pattern, used twice.

Calls worth flagging

  • The "I forgot the password →" link is small but always present on the password prompt. No buried "recovery" page elsewhere — admin doesn't need to hunt for it.
  • Step 2 is the trust moment. The acknowledgement is not malicious-pattern (no scary red); it's a clean checkbox with honest copy. The admin reads, understands, proceeds.
  • "Original archive expired before new one generated" matters — Omnischools doesn't want admin to have two valid archives floating around. The set is always size one.
  • The audit log entries with "Forgot password — regenerated" as the reason are useful for the school's own records. Forgotten passwords are not a stigma — they are normal admin behaviour worth tracking.
  • This same flow handles all three scenarios from Section 02 — "generate fresh, supersede old" works whether the old was downloaded or not. No branching paths.
04
Best practices · staying out of recovery
Four habits that prevent forgetting
PM

Use a password manager

The single best habit · saves you from yourself

Password managers like Bitwarden (free, open source) or 1Password store passwords behind one master password and sync across devices. Generate a strong archive password, save it in the manager, never type it again. Bitwarden's free tier covers everything a school admin needs.

Avoid: saving the password in your phone's notes app or in a Word document called "passwords.docx". Both are insecure and easy to lose.
SC

Send the password via a separate channel

When sharing the archive with someone else

Email the archive link. Send the password by SMS, WhatsApp, or phone call. Two channels means a single compromised inbox doesn't compromise both. This is the same logic as why MoMo confirmations come by SMS rather than email.

Avoid: putting the archive and the password in the same email. If anyone intercepts that email, they have everything they need to open the archive.
PP

Use a passphrase, not a password

Easier to remember, harder to crack

Three unrelated words plus a number is "correct horse battery staple 7". Easy to type, easy to remember, mathematically harder to brute-force than a 10-character random string. Omnischools's strength meter rewards length — a 16-character passphrase will score higher than an 8-character soup of symbols.

Avoid: "Password123!" or any single dictionary word with a number on the end. These are the first thing every cracking tool tries.
PR

Print the password on the request acknowledgement

For schools that prefer physical records

Some Ghanaian schools prefer paper trails to digital ones — Florence prints the archive request acknowledgement and clips it into a folder. Omnischools can include the password on the printable receipt at request time (admin opt-in). The folder lives in a locked drawer, the password lives with it. Physical security ≠ digital security, but for a small school, it's often more reliable.

Avoid: writing the password on a sticky note attached to the monitor. The drawer is the difference.
The pragmatic stance

Omnischools's job is not to lecture admins about password hygiene — it's to make the right behaviours easy. The strength meter rewards length over complexity. The print-with-password option respects how Ghanaian schools actually keep records. The "separate channels" guidance is paired with the actual flow that prompts admin to share via SMS/phone, not email. Make the easy thing the right thing.

Calls worth flagging

  • Bitwarden specifically named because it's free, open source, available on all platforms, and works in Ghana without payment-card friction. 1Password mentioned for completeness but it's a paid product.
  • "Print with password" is an opt-in feature — not the default, because most schools should not be printing passwords. But for the schools that do work this way, the option is honest about it rather than pretending the practice doesn't exist.
  • The passphrase example "correct horse battery staple" is borrowed from the famous xkcd comic that taught the world this pattern. Omnischools could swap for something more local — three words from Twi/Ga/Ewe with a number — if the team prefers, but the meaning translates.
  • The anti-pattern boxes in terra are the editorial spice — they name the bad habits without scolding. Admin sees their own bad habit reflected and corrects.
  • None of these tips are Omnischools-specific — they apply to any encrypted archive admin handles. Frame as transferable knowledge, not platform-specific busywork.
