What Patricia sees on her end

Parents see their own conversation history — but as a deliberate subset of the admin view. Internal admin notes ("Patricia is reliable — last term she paid in 2 instalments") cannot leak; system auto-route events ("Auto-routed to Joyce · billing topic detected") expose internal mechanics that confuse rather than inform; cost data and segment metadata are operational and not relational. The parent view shows what was sent to them, what they replied, and whether messages got through — and nothing else.

Parent's view

  • Phone frame on a calm gradient stage shows the parent portal's mobile mockup. Different device from the admin view — admins use desktop, parents use phones. The aesthetic shifts: less data density, more breathing room, calmer typography.
  • 5-tab bottom nav: Home / Akosua / Billing / Messages / Help. Messages is one of five primary tabs in the parent app — same hierarchy parents have for billing, child progress, etc. Conversation history isn't buried.
  • Parent greeting in editorial Fraunces: "Your conversation with the school." Frames the page as relational, not transactional; this isn't an inbox, it's a record of communication with people Patricia knows by name.
  • Tone summary is calmer than the admin version: "You and the school have exchanged 14 messages. You replied to 5 of them." 14 not 18 — system events (2) and internal notes (2) are filtered out before the count is computed. The arithmetic itself reflects the privacy boundary.
  • Filter pills with parent-friendly labels: All / From school / Your replies / WhatsApp / SMS / Email. "From school" / "Your replies" instead of "outbound / inbound" — parents don't think in admin terminology. The channel pills carry the same locked vocabulary as the admin view (WA gold, SMS gold-bg, email neutral).
  • Two visual treatments for messages: school messages get gold left-border + white background; parent's own replies get gold-tinted background. Mirrors WhatsApp's "their messages on left, your messages on right" convention parents already know.
  • Each school message names the sender by role: "Joyce Owusu-Ansah · Accountant" or "Christ the King JHS · fee reminder" for system-templated sends. Real people get named when there's an actual person behind the message; system-generated reminders get the school's identity. Parent knows who to thank, who to follow up with, who not to expect a reply from.
  • Delivery state visible only on school-to-parent messages: "✓ Delivered" or "✓✓ Read." Patricia can verify she received what she was supposed to — useful when she later says "I never got that reminder" and the school replies "yes you did, see your record."
  • Reply → link on every school message in gold. Parent can respond inline within the portal even if the original message came via WhatsApp or SMS; Omnischools routes it back through the appropriate channel.
  • "Earlier this term · 8 messages" day header uses Fraunces italic gold for the chunking. Parents think in terms, not in days for older messages; collapsing earlier weeks reduces visual noise.
  • Privacy footer at the bottom of the messages list states the policy plainly: "What you see here: messages the school sent you, your replies, and whether your messages were delivered. What you don't see: internal staff notes about your account, system routing details — those are private to the school." Transparent about what's filtered; doesn't pretend the parent is seeing everything.
  • "If you have a question about your record, contact the school office" closes the privacy footer. Sets the right expectation: the parent portal isn't a self-service complaint mechanism for what the school records about them; that's a person-to-person conversation.
  • Phone status bar at top: 9:42 / signal indicators / battery. Just enough device chrome to ground the mockup as a real mobile experience, not so much it distracts.
  • What this view deliberately doesn't show: no "frequency check" bar, no cost-per-message data, no segment-membership metadata, no system event entries. Parents don't need or want to see the operational layer; admins do. Two views, one source of truth.

Why these specifically? Internal admin notes contain candid working-language about parents ("Patricia is reliable — last term she paid in 2 instalments") that's appropriate for staff coordination but inappropriate to surface back. System auto-route events ("Auto-routed to Joyce · billing topic detected") expose internal mechanics — parents don't need to know the system uses keyword routing. Cost data (GHS 0.15 per SMS) is operational, not relational. Sender names within messages are visible to parents because the messages were already addressed to them with sender attribution; parents already saw who messaged them — the portal just preserves the record.
Edge case · failed deliveries. The admin view shows when an SMS bounced or a WhatsApp message failed; the parent view does not show messages the parent never received. Patricia sees only what was actually delivered to her — the "ghost" of an undelivered reminder that she has no way to verify would only confuse her. Admin retains full visibility for operational follow-up.

Filter rules

  • Two columns showing 18 events admin sees → 14 events parent sees. Visual encoding of the filter as a transformation rather than a redaction — the parent's record is structurally complete from her perspective, just smaller.
  • "Shown" / "Hidden" badges on each row of the parent column make explicit which event types are filtered. System auto-route events / internal notes / cost & segment metadata all marked Hidden in terra; school messages and own replies marked Shown in green.
  • Editorial explanation of why each type is filtered: internal notes contain candid working-language; system events expose mechanics; cost data is operational. Each rule has a defensible rationale; nothing is hidden arbitrarily.
  • Edge case callout for failed deliveries: the parent view doesn't show messages the parent never received. "Ghost" of an undelivered reminder would confuse her with no way to verify; admin retains full visibility for operational follow-up. Different fitness for purpose on each side.
  • "Sender names within messages" addressed explicitly: parents see staff names ("Joyce Owusu-Ansah · Accountant") because they were already in the original messages. The portal preserves what was already public; it doesn't introduce new exposure.
  • Diagram is part of the documentation, not part of the runtime UI. Bank reviewers, regulators, or curious parents who ask "what does my parent portal actually contain?" can be shown this filter table as the policy. Visible policy is enforceable policy.
