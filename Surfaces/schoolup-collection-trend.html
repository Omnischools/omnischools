What this shape says
Most outstanding sits in the 31-60 day bucket (54%) — these are families who didn't pay in the front-loaded weeks 1-2 surge and haven't caught up since. The 1-30 day bucket is normal — every term carries a tail of recent invoices being paid down. The 61-90 day bucket has only 2 invoices, which is healthy at this point in term. Watch the 31-60 day bucket carefully: if it migrates to 61-90 without payment, those families may need direct outreach.

Why this layout

  • The headline insight isn't "how much have you collected" — it's "how fast is collection happening, vs expected pace." The dashboard already shows totals; the trend report shows velocity. Region 1's hero tile in navy gives the absolute (GHS 178,420) but the prominent gold-tinted velocity tile next to it shows the real story: "8% behind · −GHS 19,712". Velocity is the metric this report exists for.
  • Hero tile uses navy + gold treatment matching the GES return cover and admin attendance dashboard. Pace bar inside the hero shows current 72.4% with a vertical "expected pace" marker at 80% — the gap between bar end and marker is the visual story. Same pattern as the planned vs actual progress bars elsewhere.
  • "Expected pace" is school-specific, not generic. The expected pace at week 6 (80%) is computed from the school's last 3-4 terms of historical data — what does this school's collection curve typically look like at week 6? Comparing to a generic 80% would be wrong; comparing to your school's 80% is meaningful.
  • Velocity tile is gold-tinted with a terra "8% behind" badge — visually distinct from the four other tiles. The badge changes colour and word: green "ahead" if positive, terra "behind" if negative, no badge if flat. The badge IS the report's executive summary; if the headmistress reads only one thing, this is what she gets.
  • Avg-weekly-intake tile shows the trend within the trend. "GHS 11,840 down from GHS 14,200 in wks 1-3" — collection isn't just behind cumulatively, the weekly velocity has slowed. Two related but distinct facts. The dropping weekly average is a stronger leading indicator than the cumulative total because it shows what's happening NOW, not historically.
  • Region 2 — Term overlay chart is the centerpiece. Two lines: gold (current term, 6 weeks of data) and navy (last term, all 15 weeks). The current line stops at week 6 because that's all the data we have; the prior line extends through the full term to show "where we ended up last time." The vertical "NOW · WK 6" marker in dashed gold separates "what's happened" from "what's still to come."
  • "The shape matches, but the curve is flatter" headline on the chart card is the editorial framing in display Fraunces. The system telling the story rather than just showing data. Honest assessment of the shape (similar S-curve but with reduced amplitude) without alarmism.
  • Three lines on the chart: current (gold), prior (navy), expected pace (dashed grey). The dashed line is a "where you should be by now" baseline computed from prior terms. Three reference frames: where you are, where you were, where you should be. Each answers a different question.
  • Both endpoints labelled inline with the chart. "GHS 178k · today · 72.4%" on the gold dot, "198k · last term wk 6" on the navy dot at the same x-axis position. Same-week comparison is the most informative; reading horizontally at week 6 shows the gap directly.
  • Chart footer in 4 cells: last term final (GHS 241,800 · 98.1%) / this term at wk 6 / last term at wk 6 / gap. "Last term final" gives admin a target; "98.1% by end of term" is what they reached before, and the report's implicit question is "will we get there again?"
  • Region 3 — Weekly breakdown as 10-tile grid (5 columns × 2 rows shown). Each tile is one week. Past weeks have data; current week (Wk 6) has gold border + tinted bg + "this wk" tag; future weeks are dashed-border placeholders with last-year's data as a hint of what to expect.
  • Each weekly tile shows: amount, payment count, mini-fill bar, comparison to last year. The mini-bar visualises the week's intake relative to the term's biggest intake (Wk 1 with GHS 62k = 88% of bar length). Week-to-week proportional comparison at a glance; shows the front-load + decay shape literally.
  • "vs last year" comparison on every weekly tile in green/terra/grey. "↓ 12%" / "↓ 8%" / "— flat" / "↓ 27%" — the per-week deltas tell you which weeks are dragging. Wk 5 is the worst week (−27%); that's the week where the gap opened most. Without per-week comparison, the cumulative gap doesn't reveal where it came from.
  • Wk 6 (current) shows partial data: "20-22 Oct · 3 of 5 days · partial" + "~ on pace" rather than a definitive comparison. Honest about the partial week — admin doesn't get tricked by a low-looking number that's just "Tuesday + Wednesday + Thursday" so far.
  • Future weeks show last year's number as guidance. "Wk 7 · Last yr GHS 14k" / "Wk 8 · mid-term break" / "Wk 9 · Last yr GHS 9k." Telling admin what to expect without claiming it's a forecast. Mid-term break gets called out so admin doesn't expect collection that week.
  • Region 4 — By-class collection rate sorted lowest-first. The classes that need attention float to the top of the list; the high performers settle at the bottom. JHS 3C at 54% is the visual outlier — short green bar against a long terra background. Pattern is clear: JHS 3 classes (54%, 62%, 68%) trail the others significantly.
  • Class bar shows collected (green) vs outstanding (terra-bg). Same colour vocabulary as everywhere else. "Expected pace" gold marker at 80% — the same school-historical baseline. JHS 1A at 84% has crossed the marker; JHS 3C at 54% is far short. Visual distance from the marker = how worried to be.
  • Three numeric columns: collected GHS / outstanding GHS / collection %. Three different framings of the same data. Some readers want absolute numbers (GHS 13,140 outstanding for JHS 3C is a lot of money); some want percentages (54% is below threshold); some want both. The grid serves all three.
  • "JHS 3 classes lag" pattern shows itself without commentary. The system doesn't say "JHS 3 has a problem" — it just sorts by rate. The pattern emerges from the data. Implicit narrative rather than explicit hand-holding. Admin notices and asks "why?"
  • Total row at the bottom in navy — same row structure but with the school-wide aggregate. The 72.4% navy bar with the gold expected-pace marker at 80% replicates the hero tile's pace bar at the class-table level.
  • Region 5 — Aging of outstanding uses 4 vertical bars in a row, growing taller for larger amounts. Bucket colours match the urgency vocabulary: green (1-30 = recent, normal), gold (31-60 = should be paid by now), warn (61-90 = significantly overdue), terra (90+ = collection problem).
  • Amount inside each bar in mono with a shadow for legibility against the saturated colour. The 31-60 bucket is the tallest at GHS 36,560 — visually clear that the bulk of outstanding sits there, even before reading the numbers.
  • "none yet · term started 37d ago" on the 90+ bucket explains why that bar is empty. Honest reasoning rather than just zero — at week 6 of term, no invoice can be 90 days old yet. The bucket exists for completeness; it'll fill up later.
  • Aging callout in gold-bordered italic reads the data interpretively: "Most outstanding sits in the 31-60 day bucket (54%) — these are families who didn't pay in the front-loaded weeks 1-2 surge and haven't caught up since." The system explains what the shape means, then advises: "Watch the 31-60 day bucket carefully: if it migrates to 61-90 without payment, those families may need direct outreach."
  • The callout suggests action, not panic. "May need direct outreach" — soft, conditional, agency-respecting. The system surfaces a pattern; the headmistress decides what to do about it.
  • "Open balances report" CTA in the header connects the trend report to its operational sibling. The trend tells you the shape; the balances report tells you who specifically to call. Two reports, one workflow: read the trend, drill to balances, send reminders.
  • What's not here: No predictive model ("at this rate, you'll end the term at GHS 218k"). No comparisons to other schools. No teacher-attribution ("classes with Mr. X collect faster"). No fee-structure changes recommended. The report describes; the headmistress decides.
  • What's also not here: No charts beyond the one on Region 2. No granular by-day breakdown. No payment-method splits (MoMo vs Cash vs Bank). The report focuses on temporal trend; payment-method analytics belong in a separate finance report if needed.
