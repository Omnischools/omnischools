Why this layout

  • "Where the money is" page headline. Five regions vertically stacked answering five distinct questions: how the school is doing (surplus or deficit), where money came from and went (composition), how each cedi was spent (breakdown), how things are trending (chart), what's unusual (anomalies). The headmistress can scroll the page top-to-bottom and have her five biggest financial questions answered.
  • Sidebar shows new "Books" parent nav with 5 sub-items (Dashboard, Expenses, Income, Fixed assets, Reports) and a small gold "NEW" tag. The hierarchical navigation accommodates the breadth of the bookkeeping module without flooding the top-level sidebar. Subsequent files in this MVP2 work package live under this same parent.
  • "Detailed mode" indicator pill in gold-tinted card next to period selector. Same gold-italic value convention as user-tunable parameters elsewhere. Florence sees at a glance which mode her school is in — affects the granularity of drill-through she can do. The pill is also a navigation hint; clicking it would take her to the bookkeeping settings.
  • Period pills: This month / Term 1 (active, italic gold) / Year-to-date / Last term / Custom. Five common time windows, with Term 1 default because that's the most operationally-relevant view at this point in the year. "Custom" trails the row with arrow because it's the escape hatch for non-standard ranges.
  • Region 01 — Surplus hero in navy/gold matches the dashboard pattern from collection trend and admin attendance. This is the most important question on the page ("are we making money?") and gets the biggest visual weight. The hero shows GHS 42,072 in big italic gold with "Surplus · running ahead" tag in green-tinted pill.
  • Hero delta line in editorial language: "↑ 18% vs Term 1 last year (GHS 35,640) at this point in term · driven by fewer repair costs and BECE registration income". Specific causal explanation rather than just the number. Tells Florence not just that surplus is up but why — same kind of interpretive framing as the collection trend report.
  • Three side tiles to the right of hero: Income / Expenses / Bank balance. Sub-text on each tile is editorial and operationally meaningful: "97% from school fees" tells her about income concentration; "within historical range" reassures about expenses; "5.2 weeks of operating runway at current burn" — the runway figure is a leading indicator banks watch and Florence rarely hears framed this way.
  • "5.2 weeks runway" is the most commercially valuable number on this page. A school operator who internalises runway thinking makes better decisions about timing of major expenses, urgency of fee collection, and whether to seek bridging finance. Translating bank balance into runway is genuine financial education embedded in the dashboard.
  • Region 02 — Income vs expenses split into two side-by-side panels within a single card. Income panel has green-tinted icon and total; expenses panel has terra-tinted icon and total. Visual symmetry enforces "these are the two sides"; the split-view layout is more honest than separate cards because it shows the relationship.
  • Each side has a total row with editorial meta: Income "100% of inflow · 312 students contributing"; Expenses "83% of income · 17% retained as surplus". The percentage-of-income framing on expenses tells the headmistress what fraction of her income she's spending — a useful structural insight.
  • Income line items reveal the school's revenue concentration risk: School fees 96.9% / BECE registration 1.7% / PTA 0.9% / Donations 0.3% / Other 0.2%. The 96.9% concentration is itself an insight — one bad year on enrolment and the school is in trouble. Banks look at this; honest dashboards should too.
  • Expense line items have realistic Ghanaian school sub-categories: "Salaries · 14 teaching · 3 non-teaching" / "Utilities · ECG · GWCL · internet" / "Bank & payment fees · Hubtel 1.95% · bank charges". Real categories, real vendors, real fee structures — same localisation depth as the GES annual census.
  • Hubtel fees as their own line ("GHS 2,068 · 1.0%") connects this dashboard to the aggregator integration designed earlier. The fee absorbed by the school shows up here as a real expense category. Operational consistency across MVP2 surfaces; what was promised in aggregator settings ("GHS 2,068/term") materialises here as actual expense data.
  • GES staff footnote in dashed-bordered card explains the public school context: "schools running this module as public would also see a footnote line for staff effectively compensated by GES — those salaries don't appear above because they don't hit the school's books, but they're not zero in the school's true cost picture." Honest about the dual-mode design even when showing a private school's view.
  • Region 03 — Stacked horizontal bar showing all 8 categories as proportional segments. Salaries dominates at 80.4% and gets the navy treatment; smaller categories stack in the remaining 20%. The visual proportion is the headline — Florence sees that salaries are 4/5 of her cost structure and everything else combined is 1/5.
  • "Salaries are 80% of operating cost" headline in italic gold display Fraunces. The system tells the story rather than just showing data. Same editorial framing as collection trend's "the curve is flatter" headline.
  • Category list in 2-column grid below the bar with colour swatches matching the stacked bar segments. Each row: swatch / category name + sub-line / amount in mono / percentage. Two-up rather than full-width to keep the page scannable; admin doesn't need to scan a long thin column.
  • Region 04 — 12-month trend chart showing income (green), expenses (terra), and surplus (gold) lines monthly across the past year. Term boundary markers in dashed gold separate Term 1 / Term 2 / Term 3 / Term 1 again. Vertical school-fee surge pattern visible at term starts (income jumps), expense pattern relatively flat, surplus oscillates.
  • The trend reveals the cyclical nature of school finances: income spikes at term starts, smooths through the term, then surges again. Expenses are more linear. Surplus is highest at term starts and tightens through the term — visible in the gold line dipping at term ends. This pattern is invisible in any single month's view.
  • Region 05 — Anomalies panel side-by-side with the trend in a 1.4fr / 1fr layout. Trend is visual and historical; anomalies are textual and recent. The two together cover "what's the long arc?" and "what's happening right now?"
  • Anomaly cards use 4 distinct icon states: warn (caution), terra (concerning), green (positive), neutral dot (housekeeping). The first three match the locked colour vocabulary; the fourth is for benign maintenance items like uncategorised entries.
  • Anomaly headlines are editorial: "Utilities up 50% in October" / "Repairs down vs last term" / "Bank balance tightening" / "3 entries uncategorised". Italic gold on the operative phrase in each headline matches the system's "this is what to look at" visual signature.
  • Each anomaly has a hypothesis rather than just an alert. Utilities anomaly: "Could be the new ICT lab drawing power, or a billing irregularity worth checking with ECG." The system surfaces the issue and gestures at possible causes; the headmistress decides what to investigate. Same interpretive style as JHS 3C in the GES return and the collection trend interpretive callouts.
  • Bank balance anomaly cross-references collection trend: "Outstanding fees of GHS 67,980 would restore comfortable buffer if collected." The dashboard knows about the rest of the system; finance anomalies link to operational levers. Florence reads this and immediately thinks "I should look at outstanding balances and consider sending reminders."
  • Anomaly action links in italic gold ("Review →" / "Detail →" / "Open →") drill into the relevant detail page. Each anomaly is a hypothesis the system raises; the action is to investigate it. Same anomaly-with-action pattern as the attendance alerts page.
  • What's not on this page: No tax projection (schools handle tax through external accountants), no cash-flow forecast (predictive, harder to do honestly), no multi-school comparison ("how does CTK compare to other schools?" — privacy-violating and creates wrong incentives), no scenario planning ("what if we hire 2 more teachers?" — over-engineered for MVP).
  • What's also not here: No depreciation summary (lives in fixed assets module), no payroll detail (lives in staff compensation module), no chart-of-accounts editing (lives in books settings), no detailed transaction list (drill-through from any line item shows it). The dashboard is a summary surface; details are one click away.
  • Bank-ready statement export is reachable from the page header as "Export reports →". This dashboard is for internal management; the bank-ready PDF (P&L + balance sheet, formatted for external readers) is a separate output format. Both produced from the same underlying data.
