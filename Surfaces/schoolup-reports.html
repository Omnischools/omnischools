Omnischools · Reports

Answers, not spreadsheets

Reports answer recurring questions: how much have we collected, who hasn't paid, how is each class doing. Each report leads with the answer, shows the trend, and lets the admin drill into the detail. MVP1 ships finance reports first; academic and operational follow as the data accrues.

Why this layout

  • Page title is a question: "What would you like to know?" Reports module is fundamentally a question-answering surface, not a data warehouse browser. The framing matches the user's intent.
  • Three category tabs: Finance (5 reports, MVP1) / Academic (2, MVP2) / Operational (3, mostly MVP2). Default tab is Finance because that's what 80% of report visits are for. Tab counts include both shipped and coming-soon reports for transparency.
  • Each card shows a live snapshot, not just a name + description. "Term 1 · 2026 collected: GHS 94,830.00 — 62% of GHS 152,400.00 billed, +12% vs Term 3 last year." The snapshot is the answer; the card is the question. Many days the headmistress doesn't even need to click in.
  • Featured "MOST USED" badge on Term collection summary. Promotes the report that headmistresses open every Monday — first thing they see in the grid. The featured card has a subtle gold background gradient that visually anchors it.
  • Snapshot includes comparative context. "+12% vs Term 3 last year at this point" is more useful than just the absolute number. Reports without comparisons are just numbers; reports with comparisons are insights.
  • Color-coded icons by report character: dollar (gold) for collection, terracotta exclamation for outstanding, green percent for discounts, navy arrow for trends. Visual scan tells admin what kind of answer each card gives.
  • Coming-soon cards visible at reduced opacity, with "MVP2" badge and explanation of what's needed before they activate. Sets expectations honestly without pretending the feature isn't planned.
  • Two header actions: "Schedule reports..." and "Export all (zip)". Schedule lets admin set "email me Term collection every Monday at 7am". Export-all lets accountant grab everything at once for end-of-month filing.
  • Reports lives at the top level of the sidebar, not nested under Billing. It's a destination of its own, used by both admin and accountant. Nesting it would bury the most-used finance views.

Why this layout

  • Period bar at the top defaults to the current term — that's the question 90% of admins are asking. "This week" and "This month" are quick filters; Custom opens a date picker.
  • Four-KPI strip answers the question in four numbers: billed, collected (featured navy card — the headline answer), outstanding, reversed. Same shape as the financial audit summary; reuse intentional.
  • Featured KPI card in navy with the collection figure — visual anchor for the page. The arrow with comparative context ("vs 50% same week last term") shows trajectory, not just absolute value.
  • Weekly bar chart shows the peak-and-decay pattern that's universal to school terms. Week 3 is colored green (peak); subsequent weeks fade. The annotation ("Term started 15 Jan, now in week 11 of 11") sets context without legend friction.
  • Method breakdown ranks by amount with percentages. MTN MoMo dominates at 75% — useful for the school to understand its collection mix and for cash-flow planning around MoMo settlement timing.
  • By-class table with progress bars color-coded by collection rate: green ≥70%, gold 50-69%, terra <50%. The class scoring 31% (JHS 3B) reads as immediately problematic; the headmistress can click View and address it.
  • Drill-through on every breakdown. Click a class → see outstanding students. Click a method → see those payments. Click the bar chart → see that week's payments. Reports without drill-through are dead-ends.
  • Sub-line context lives in the rcb-head — "Peak in Week 3 when most parents settle term fees" — adding interpretation without requiring the user to figure it out themselves.
  • Export CSV and PDF in the page header. CSV for spreadsheet work; PDF for filing or board meetings. Same data both formats.

Why this layout

  • Page title is direct: "Who hasn't paid". The outstanding balances report is action-oriented — not "review the data" but "do something about it." The title and the gold "Send reminders to all overdue (29)" CTA make the action clear.
  • Big terracotta headline number shows the total at-risk: GHS 57,570.00. With context: 142 students, 29 overdue 30+ days, 38% of billed unsettled with 1 week left. This is what the headmistress brings to the next staff meeting.
  • Four aging buckets with colored top-bars: navy (not yet due), green (due this week), warn-gold (1-30 overdue), terra (30+ overdue). The eye scans left-to-right from "expected" to "problem." Future + due-this-week sit in the green; the action items live in the warn/danger.
  • Filter strip mirrors the aging buckets. Pills with counts: All / Not yet due / Due this week / 1-30 overdue / 30+ overdue. Click any to filter the table. Plus class dropdown and free-text search.
  • Table rows in 30+ days overdue are tinted soft red. Same flagging pattern as the audit log — when scanning, the eye finds the cases needing attention.
  • Aging pill on each row tells the exact age: "42 days", "18 days", "due Fri", "due 12 May". The pill colour matches the bucket; the text gives precision.
  • Send reminder button per row — admin can take action without leaving the page. For "not yet due" rows the button is disabled (no reminder needed yet). For overdue rows the button is gold-outlined and sends an SMS using the overdue-notice template.
  • Bulk action in page header — "Send reminders to all overdue (29)" — for the weekly Monday morning sweep. Headmistress hits it once and 29 SMS reminders go out from the templates we built.
  • Guardian name + phone shown next to student name so admin knows exactly who's getting the reminder. Anchors the action to a specific person, not just a row.
  • Paid column shown alongside balance — useful context. A student with GHS 200 paid and GHS 220 outstanding reads differently from one with GHS 0 paid and GHS 400 outstanding. Both might be "overdue" but the conversation differs.
