Visiting Sunday · the school's front door, opened monthly

Once a month — the second Sunday — Asankrangwa's gates open to parents and guardians from noon to 4 PM. Lunch moves earlier (11:30) to clear the dining hall. Dormitories remain out of bounds. Students meet families in designated visitor areas. The Senior Housemaster runs this from the digital Visitor's Book — a replacement for the paper register that the Student on Duty has held for sixty years. This view shows the run-up: three days out, with parent RSVPs accumulating, approved-visitor lists pulled per student, and visitor area capacity being managed.

DORMITORIES & STAFF QUARTERS · OUT OF BOUNDS · STRICT

No visitor may enter a dormitory · or any staff bungalow

Students may not sit in visitors' cars. Cars in designated parking only. Beyond 4 PM, no visitor may remain on campus. Past 4:15 PM, SoD logs visitor as overstaying and HM is notified. Past 4:30, Senior HM intervenes directly. The rule is what makes the visiting day feel safe, especially for the youngest boarders.

What this surface replaces

  • The Visitor's Book. A physical hardback ledger held by the Student on Duty at the gate. Each entry: parent name, student name, relationship, time-in, time-out, signature. Sixty years of operation. Illegible by Year 3.
  • The clipboard at the kitchen. Coordinating early-lunch shift with the catering team. Now flows from the RSVP system.
  • The phone call to the parent. "Did you get the SMS about Sunday?" Now the SMS is the RSVP, with read-receipts.

Approved visitor list mechanics

  • Max 6 visitors per student. Forces parents to think carefully about the list.
  • Nominated by parent / guardian. Approved by Housemaster after age/relationship verification.
  • Pastoral-sensitive cases require Dean approval. J. Manu's list was reviewed by the Dean after his father's death.
  • Names persist across years · siblings, grandparents, family pastors remain approved unless removed
  • Verified at gate via Ghana Card or NHIS · ID checked, name matched to list, time stamped

Why a digital Visitor's Book matters more than it seems
Editorial · the front-door problem
The Visitor's Book as a security surface

A school's front door is its most important security surface. The visiting Sunday is when that door is most open.

Twelve hours of the school year are when the school's gate is wide open to people who are not staff and not students. Four hours per visiting Sunday, once a month, eight months of the academic year (no visiting in vacation; sometimes adjusted in WASSCE weeks). During those twelve hours, between four hundred and seven hundred adult visitors flow through a campus full of teenagers. The Visitor's Book is what makes that flow auditable.

The paper version is failing. Not because anyone is malicious — schools largely run on goodwill — but because the SoD writing the book at 1:30 PM cannot remember whether the third Mrs Mensah of the day is actually approved to visit student JKL or whether they are the wrong Mrs Mensah. The book records "Mrs Mensah · mother · noon · 3:45 PM" and that's what it has. There is no cross-reference to whether this Mrs Mensah is on JKL's approved visitor list. If a non-approved visitor enters under a common name, the book records it as if it were fine.

The digital approach reverses this. The student's approved-visitor list is pulled at gate sign-in. The visitor's name is matched. The relationship is verified. If the visitor's not on the list, the gate flags it — sometimes legitimately (new aunt visiting from abroad, never met) but the resolution is the SoD calling the HM for verbal authorisation, not the SoD writing down a name without checking. The gate becomes a list-check rather than a list-record.

There is a quieter benefit to surfacing this data: schools start to see patterns. Which students get visited every month? Which students never get visited? Which Houses have stronger parent engagement? Which families travel from Kumasi vs. from Accra vs. from Wa? The Senior HM running this surface for three years has data the paper book never produced — about which Form 1 boarders haven't had a single visit (a pastoral flag), which families have logistical struggles getting here (a transport coordination opportunity), which months have lighter visiting (potential to expand to also include the third Sunday in WASSCE term).

One design decision worth defending: the surface does not display visit photos or videos. No QR scanning in front of the parent. The check is done by the SoD with a tablet, name spoken aloud, parent ID checked once, the green tick happens out of the parent's sight. Visiting day is a moment of family connection. The technology stays behind the scenes; the warmth stays in front. The Visitor's Book worked for sixty years not because it was a great record-keeping system but because it was background. The digital version aims to preserve that.
