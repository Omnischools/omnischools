Why this layout

  • Two-pane: brand and value on left, form on right. Headmistress sees what she's signing up for, not just a wall of fields.
  • Three sections, not three steps. A wizard hides what's coming and feels longer; one scroll feels honest and quick.
  • Education level + School type as cascading multi-selects. Captures the structural complexity of Ghanaian schools (many run multiple levels) without overwhelming the form. School type options dynamically filter to the selected levels — pick Basic and you see Creche/KG/Primary/JHS; add Secondary and SHS appears too.
  • "Approx. students" sorted ascending from "Under 100" up. Most basic and JHS schools cluster in 100–500 range; ascending order matches scanning intuition. Range tiers shift if Tertiary is selected — they jump to 500–2,000 / 2,000–10,000 / 10,000–30,000 / 30,000–70,000 / 70,000+ to match the realistic tertiary distribution.
  • Region + district as cascading dropdowns. Static data, no API, ministry-grade tagging from day one.
  • "Use my location" is gold-bordered, not gold-filled. Important enough to spot, optional enough that skipping it doesn't feel wrong.
  • Captured GPS shows a green confirmation chip with rounded coordinates and inferred area — gives the user proof their tap did something without scaring them with raw decimals.
  • Confirm password on a desktop field is cheap insurance — admin accounts are long-lived and rarely re-authenticated; a typo here means a frustrating recovery flow on day two.
  • Ministry opt-in is unchecked by default, with full transparency about what's actually shared. Active consent is the right default for any data-sharing decision, and ministry adoption built on coercion is brittle.
  • Terms checkbox unchecked too. Same principle — active consent.
  • "Save & finish later" sits next to Create. Some headmistresses will hand the rest to an IT-savvy teacher; honor that workflow.

Why this layout

  • Six steps, visible up front. No surprises. The headmistress can see how much is left at any moment, which lowers abandonment.
  • Auto-save with a quiet receipt ("Saved 4 seconds ago"). Critical for users worried about losing work on a slow connection.
  • Term dates pre-filled with sensible Ghanaian basic-school defaults — easier to adjust than to construct from scratch.
  • Grade scale uses letter+description+threshold, not just numbers. A teacher reading "from 80" knows that means A; the colored badge does the visual work.
  • Preset link for WASSCE-style scale. Many schools want to mimic BECE/WASSCE grading; offering the preset means they don't have to relearn it.
  • "Save & come back" implied, not a button. The auto-save plus the rail navigation means leaving and returning is the same gesture as clicking continue.
  • Logo upload was step 2, ahead of structure. Visible reward early — the headmistress sees her school's identity reflected before doing the heavier setup work.

Why this layout

  • This screen alone decides whether the pilot succeeds. If a school has to debug a CSV by line number in a text editor, they quit. Validation has to be a UI, not an error message.
  • Default view shows errors first, not "all 142 rows". The admin came here to fix problems; lead with them.
  • Errors block, warnings don't. Missing DOB blocks (school records require it). Missing guardian warns (the student record can exist; the parent-invite step just gets skipped). The distinction matters in real Ghanaian schools where guardian info is sometimes incomplete.
  • Each error explains itself in plain language ("Invalid month — should be 1–12") rather than "ERR_001 row 7 col 4". Headmistress can read this; she can't read a stack trace.
  • "Fix inline" opens a tiny edit modal on that row — no need to re-export the CSV from Excel and re-upload.
  • Two CTAs at the foot: "Skip errors & import" for the pragmatist, "Fix & import all" for the perfectionist. Don't force a workflow.
  • Phone validation accepts both "+233 24..." and "024..." formats. Either is normal in Ghana; rejecting one creates needless friction.

Why this layout

  • One card, one action. No top nav, no sidebar — the teacher hasn't joined yet, so the app shell would be premature and confusing.
  • School block above the welcome line. Teacher confirms "yes, this is my school" before reading anything else — protects against phishing-style misdirection.
  • The Headmistress's name appears in the invite copy. Social proof: this isn't a system email, it's a person at a school you know inviting you.
  • Class/subject assignments shown up front, before the password fields. Teacher knows what they're saying yes to before committing.
  • Email locked, not editable. Prevents an invite link being repurposed for a different person — the email is part of the invite identity.
  • "Not Mr. Mensah?" escape hatch matters when the school's IT lead emailed the invite to themselves to forward and clicked it themselves by accident.
  • SMS-version of this same flow uses a 6-digit code instead of a tokenised URL — for teachers without easy email access. Same destination, same data.

Use the student code
If a parent's phone changed or they didn't get the SMS, the school gives them a printed letter with their child's student code (e.g. CTK-2024-018). Enter the code on the parent app's "Link a child" screen — the system asks 2 verification questions (date of birth, class) before linking.

Why this layout

  • Phone is the default, visible first. Most Ghanaian parents will use it; making it the path of least resistance reduces drop-off.
  • Email toggle exists for the educated/urban subset who prefer email. Common with parents working in offices, NGOs, or abroad.
  • Google sign-in sits above manual email, not below. Tapping a button is faster than typing on a phone keyboard; the parent who can use Google saves 30 seconds.
  • "OR USE EMAIL" divider is intentionally not "or sign up manually" — the choice between Google and manual is mechanically the same outcome (an email-based account), so the divider treats them as equals.
  • Confirm password on both paths. Cheap insurance against a typo on a phone keyboard, and parents are creating long-lived accounts they'll rarely re-authenticate.
  • Phone is locked from the SMS, email is editable. Different sources of truth — SMS pre-verifies the phone number, email path requires verification email after.
  • Child name is the hero on both paths. Ama opened this because of Akosua, regardless of which sign-in method she picks.
  • Switching the toggle preserves entered passwords, just changes the identifier field. Parent shouldn't have to retype everything if they hesitated and switched.
